PART 1: MEDIEVAL WORLDVIEW & ITS COLLAPSE

1. The Great Chain of Being portrayed social hierarchy as divine order from God.
2. Name the FOUR forces that broke the Great Chain:
  • Black Death (labor scarcity)
  • Longbows & gunpowder (military)
  • Sola scriptura (Reformation/spiritual)
  • State centralization (political)
3. The Black Death killed two-thirds (or 2/3) of Europe's population. England's Statute of Laborers (1351) tried to freeze wages but failed because: workers could leave for better opportunities; more land available; labor scarcity gave workers bargaining power; peasants could negotiate; lords had to compete for workers
4. 1381 English Peasants' Revolt key facts:
  • Triggered by: head (or poll) tax
  • Leaders: Wat Tyler and priest John Ball
  • Woman leader: Johanna Ferrour
  • Outcome: Crushed, but head tax never imposed again
5. QUESTION: Why were women "written out" of 1381 histories? What sources reveal them?
ANSWER: Traditional histories were written by male elites who assumed political/military action was exclusively male domain. Chronicles written by churchmen and nobles systematically ignored or erased women's participation. Court records reveal women's participation because they are bureaucratic/legal documents that must record everyone accused of crimes by name and action, regardless of gender. Court records can't erase women the way narrative histories can.

13. QUESTION: How does legally executing a king contradict the Great Chain of Being?
ANSWER: The Great Chain of Being held that kings received authority directly from God and occupied a fixed position in divine hierarchy, making them sacred and untouchable. Legally executing a king for "crimes against the nation" asserts that national law stands above the monarch, that authority comes from the nation/people rather than God, and that kings can be held accountable. This directly contradicts the idea that social hierarchy is divinely ordained and unchangeable‚Äîit shows authority is political and conditional, not divine and absolute.
14. What survived 1660 restoration: Divine right crushed, Parliament supreme, wages rose, British became tallest Europeans, Bill of Rights (1689).

PART 7: KEY DATES & WHY THEY MATTER

40. 1381 (Peasants' Revolt): English revolt triggered by poll tax; though crushed and leaders executed, head tax was never imposed again, showing lasting impact of popular resistance and that peasants had gained leverage from post-plague labor shortage.
41. 1648 (Westphalia): Treaty ending Thirty Years' War; Spain recognized Dutch independence, showing commercial society could defeat feudal empire and marking shift toward modern state system.
42. 1649 (Charles I executed): First legal execution of a monarch for "crimes against the nation," establishing precedent that no one above the law and shattering divine right of kings.

44. Jan 1793 (Louis XVI executed): French king executed following precedent of Charles I (1649), demonstrating that Enlightenment ideas about conditional authority and national sovereignty could be institutionalized.

BONUS SYNTHESIS QUESTIONS

A. Compare English and French Revolutions: 2 similarities, 1 difference

Similarities:
  1. Both legally executed kings (Charles I 1649, Louis XVI 1793) establishing that monarchs could be held accountable to law/nation rather than ruling by divine right
  2. Both emerged from combination of material crisis (economic hardship), political exclusion (Parliament vs. king in England; Third Estate vs. Estates-General in France), and ideological alternatives (constitutional ideas, Enlightenment thought)
Difference:

English Revolution successfully established constitutional monarchy with Bill of Rights (1689) that lasted, while French Revolution radicalized into Terror, then Napoleon's dictatorship, then restoration‚Äîthough both achieved permanent transformations (divine right crushed, feudalism abolished, etc.)

OR: English Revolution primarily political (Parliament vs. king over taxation/authority), while French Revolution involved both political AND economic demands (bourgeois vs. sans-culottes conflict over price controls, economic equality)

B. How did Black Death ‚Üí 1381 revolt connect?

Black Death (1347-51) killed 2/3 of Europe's population, creating severe labor scarcity. Surviving peasants found themselves with bargaining power for first time‚Äîmore land available, lords competing for workers with offers of wages and freedoms. England's Statute of Laborers (1351) tried to freeze wages at pre-plague levels, but failed because workers could leave for better opportunities. By 1381 (30 years later), peasants had experienced this leverage and knew they could make demands. When head/poll tax was imposed, peasants revolted with specific political demands: abolish poll tax, written charters outlining rights, reduce rents, execute oppressive officials. Even though revolt was crushed, head tax was never imposed again‚Äîshowing peasants had gained permanent leverage from post-plague economic transformation. Material change (labor shortage) created conditions for political action.

C. Choose ONE revolution: How did common people/women shape it?

English Revolution:

Common people and women actively shaped English Revolution rather than passively following elite leaders. New Model Army soldiers participated in Putney Debates (1647), debating sovereignty and rights as citizens not subjects, pushing revolution beyond elite intentions. Levellers organized petitions and published manifestos demanding expanded suffrage, while Diggers occupied common land challenging private property. These groups pressured from below, forcing consideration of more radical changes than Cromwell and Parliament wanted. This "bottom-up" perspective shows revolution wasn't just about elite political conflict (king vs. Parliament) but involved multiple groups with competing visions pushing for different futures. Common soldiers and radical groups made revolution more than constitutional crisis‚Äîturned it into debate about who should have political voice and economic rights.

French Revolution:

Common people and women actively shaped French Revolution rather than following elite leadership. Bastille crowd (July 14, 1789) was composed of craftsmen, journeymen, shopkeepers (not criminals per Rud√©'s research) who took strategic action attacking symbol of arbitrary power on day bread prices peaked‚Äîcombining material necessity with political consciousness. Women's March to Versailles (October 1789) forced king to Paris through combination of material demands (bread) and political strategy (surveillance). Sans-culottes consistently pushed revolution leftward, demanding price controls, direct democracy, and economic equality‚Äîforcing bourgeois republicans to respond. Women organized clubs, workshops, published feminist declarations. This "bottom-up" perspective reveals revolution wasn't just elite Third Estate vs. nobles but involved conflicts within revolutionary coalition‚Äîbourgeois wanted political equality while sans-culottes demanded economic justice. Shows common people had agency, political goals, and power to pressure government, not just passive masses manipulated by leaders.
