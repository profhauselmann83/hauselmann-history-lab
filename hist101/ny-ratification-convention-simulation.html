Stakes & Ground Rules

"Here's what's at stake: If New York rejects the Constitution, the nation splits geographically. The southern states would be cut off from New England. Some say this could mean war, economic collapse, or foreign invasion.

Your job today: debate three specific issues about representation in the new Constitution. At the end, you'll vote on whether to ratify. But remember ‚Äî this isn't about winning or losing. It's about understanding the arguments that shaped American democracy."

I
Size of Electoral Districts
5:00 ‚Äì 25:00  ¬∑  20 minutes
‚ñæ
Write on board: "Should House representatives be elected from SMALL districts (local, familiar) or LARGE districts (broader perspective)?"
"The Constitution says House districts will have about 30,000 people each. Antifederalists say this is too large ‚Äî representatives won't know the people. Federalists say large districts prevent local demagogues from getting elected.

Federalists, you have 3 minutes to make your opening argument. Who's your speaker?"
‚öñ Federalist Argument (Large Districts)
  • Filters demagogues ‚Äî In a district of 30,000, only people with real merit can win. Local troublemakers can't manipulate that many voters.
  • Prevents corruption ‚Äî Much harder to bribe 30,000 voters than 5,000.
  • Attracts better candidates ‚Äî Prominent, experienced leaders will run.
  • National perspective ‚Äî Reps think about the nation, not just hometown concerns.
"In large districts, the corruption of the electors is much more difficult" ‚Äî Hamilton, NY Debates, June 21
"Refine and enlarge the public views" ‚Äî Madison, Federalist No. 10
Strategy: Ask Moderates ‚Äî "Do you want your doctor to be your neighbor, or the best doctor available?"
‚ú¶ Antifederalist Argument (Small Districts)
  • Only elites get elected ‚Äî In a district of 30,000, only wealthy, well-known aristocrats can win.
  • Representatives lose touch ‚Äî How can someone represent 30,000 people they don't know?
  • Resemblance matters ‚Äî A true representative should BE like the people.
  • Local knowledge lost ‚Äî Small districts mean reps know your roads, taxes, concerns.
Representatives should be "a true picture of the people, possess a knowledge of their circumstances" ‚Äî Melancton Smith, NY Debates, June 21
"They are the sign ‚Äî the people are the thing signified" ‚Äî Brutus, Essay 3
Strategy: Ask Moderates ‚Äî "Do you want to be represented by someone who KNOWS you, or a stranger 50 miles away?"

Moderate Questions (5 min) ‚Äî Prompt if Needed

  • "Federalists: Won't large districts just elect rich people?"
  • "Antifederalists: Don't small districts elect unqualified people?"
  • "Federalists: How does a rep from a large district actually LEARN what people need?"
  • "Antifederalists: What if my local district elects a corrupt demagogue?"

Open Debate (6 min) ‚Äî Your Role as Moderator

  • Keep time (1 minute per speaker)
  • Call on different students; redirect off-topic: "Bring it back to district size"
  • If stalling: "Moderates, what do you need to hear to make a decision?"

Vote (3 min)

"Time to vote. The question: Should the Constitution REQUIRE small electoral districts?
YES = Antifederalist position (add amendment for small districts)
NO = Federalist position (keep Constitution as written)"
II
Senate Term Limits & Recall
30:00 ‚Äì 50:00  ¬∑  20 minutes  ¬∑  connects to "Put Country First" (Haass)
‚ñæ
Write on board: "Should Senators face MANDATORY ROTATION (term limits) and be subject to RECALL by state legislatures?"
"Welcome back. Issue 2 connects directly to the democratic norm 'Put Country First' from Richard Haass. The question: Should senators serve unlimited terms, or face mandatory rotation ‚Äî maybe 6 years on, 6 years off? And should state legislatures be able to RECALL senators mid-term?"
‚ú¶ Antifederalist Argument (Term Limits & Recall)
  • Prevents aristocracy ‚Äî Without term limits, senators become a permanent ruling class.
  • Keeps them accountable ‚Äî State legislatures sent these senators; if they betray their state, fire them.
  • Refreshes ideas ‚Äî Rotation brings new voices; long tenure breeds arrogance.
  • They're AGENTS, not kings ‚Äî When your agent betrays you, you fire them.
"Senators are agents of their states" ‚Äî Melancton Smith, NY Debates, June 24
"Office separates its holder from the people served" ‚Äî Federal Farmer, Letter 8
Strategy: "If your senator votes against everything you believe, shouldn't you be able to fire them?"
‚öñ Federalist Argument (No Rotation/Recall)
  • Encourages selfishness ‚Äî A senator who must leave soon grabs power while they can.
  • Destroys independence ‚Äî Senators should vote for the national good, not whatever their state legislature wants this week.
  • Expertise matters ‚Äî It takes years to master foreign policy. Forcing experienced senators out wastes that knowledge.
  • They're TRUSTEES, not messengers ‚Äî Need freedom to do what's right for the country.
"Senators are agents for the Union, trustees of the common good" ‚Äî Hamilton, NY Debates, June 25
"When a man knows he must quit his station, he will turn his attention chiefly to his own emolument" ‚Äî Hamilton, NY Debates, June 25
Strategy: "Do you want senators focused on the nation's long-term good, or scared of being recalled every time they make a tough vote?"

Moderate Questions (5 min)

  • "Antifederalists: What if rotation forces out our best senators?"
  • "Federalists: Without recall, what stops a corrupt senator?"
  • "Both sides: How does this affect 'Put Country First'?"

Moderator Prompts for Open Debate (6 min)

  • "How does this connect to Burke's speech? Is a senator an 'agent' or a 'trustee'?"
  • "Moderates, which side better serves the LONG-TERM health of the republic?"

Vote (3 min)

"Vote: Should the Constitution be amended to require rotation (term limits) and allow state legislatures to recall senators?
YES = Antifederalist position  ¬∑  NO = Federalist position"
III
Instructed Voting
50:00 ‚Äì 70:00  ¬∑  25 minutes  ¬∑  ‚≠ê Core issue ‚Äî connects directly to research papers
‚ñæ
Write on board: "Should representatives be BOUND by instructions from constituents, or use their own JUDGMENT?"
"This is the BIG ONE. This is the heart of your research papers. Edmund Burke said representatives owe constituents their judgment, NOT obedience. Antifederalists say that's aristocratic nonsense ‚Äî representatives should FOLLOW instructions from the people.

This gets directly at: What IS representation? Are reps agents or trustees? Should they mirror public opinion or refine it?"
‚ú¶ Antifederalist: Instructed Voting is ESSENTIAL
  • People know their own interests ‚Äî We don't need aristocrats telling us what's good for us.
  • Prevents betrayal ‚Äî Without instructions, reps can ignore us completely.
  • True democracy ‚Äî If reps override the people's will, how is that different from monarchy?
  • Resemblance requires obedience ‚Äî If reps should BE like us, they should VOTE like us.
"There is no possible way to effect this but by an equal, full and fair representation" ‚Äî Brutus, Essay 4
Representatives should collect "interests, feelings, opinions and views of the people...as they would be were the people all assembled" ‚Äî Federal Farmer, Letter 7
Strategy: "If the people can't instruct their reps, then the people aren't really governing at all."
‚öñ Federalist: Instructed Voting DESTROYS Good Government
  • Passion vs. Reason ‚Äî The people feel their grievances but don't always know the best solutions.
  • National good sacrificed ‚Äî If every rep must obey local instructions, national interests are ignored.
  • Deliberation becomes impossible ‚Äî Congress should debate and compromise. Can't do that if everyone's hands are tied.
  • Accountability still exists ‚Äî We have ELECTIONS. If reps betray us, we vote them out.
"Your representative owes you...his judgment; and he betrays, instead of serving you, if he sacrifices it to your opinion" ‚Äî Burke, Bristol Speech, 1774
"Parliament is...a deliberative assembly of one nation, with one interest, that of the whole" ‚Äî Burke, Bristol Speech
Strategy: "Do you want your representative to be a messenger, or a leader? A parrot, or a statesman?"

Moderate Questions (6 min ‚Äî extra time)

  • "Federalists: What if a rep's 'judgment' means ignoring the people completely?"
  • "Antifederalists: What if the people's instructions are based on false information?"
  • "Both: How do elections provide accountability if reps can ignore us between elections?"
  • "Federalists: Isn't Burke's argument basically saying 'we're smarter than you'?"
  • "Antifederalists: If reps must follow instructions, why have reps at all? Why not direct democracy?"

Open Debate (10 min ‚Äî extra time) ‚Äî Key Moments to Highlight

  • Federalist strength: "We elect people smarter/more informed than us for a reason"
  • Antifederalist strength: "If they can ignore us, they're not really representing us"
  • The paradox: "Can representatives be independent AND accountable?"
  • Draw out competing visions of democracy ‚Äî ask Moderates: "What does 'government by the people' actually MEAN?"
  • Connect to research papers: "How does your democratic norm relate to this?"

Vote (3 min)

"Final vote: Should the Constitution be amended to allow constituents to give BINDING INSTRUCTIONS to their representatives?
YES = Antifederalist position (instructed voting)  ¬∑  NO = Federalist position (independent judgment)"

After the Votes ‚Äî Historical Reveal

"In real life, New York ratified on July 26, 1788, by a vote of 30‚Äì27. It was incredibly close. Alexander Hamilton threatened that New York City would secede from the state if the convention rejected. Melancton Smith actually switched sides at the last minute after hearing New Hampshire and Virginia had ratified.

The real convention lasted SIX WEEKS. We just did it in 70 minutes. But you experienced the core arguments that shaped American democracy."
Common Student Struggles & Your Responses
"I don't understand the difference between agents and trustees"
"An agent follows orders (like a lawyer following client's wishes). A trustee makes decisions for you (like a financial advisor managing your money). Which should a representative be?"
"These arguments seem contradictory"
"YES! That's the point. Both Federalists and Antifederalists had valid concerns. Democracy requires balancing them."
"How do I connect this to my democratic norm?"
"Ask yourself: How does [your norm] require representatives to behave? Does Burke's view support or undermine your norm?"

How Did Federalists Win Despite Being Outnumbered?

  • Delay: Hamilton stalled for time, knowing NH and VA were voting soon.
  • Threat: Hamilton warned that NYC would secede from NY State and ratify independently.
  • Persuasion: Genuine intellectual debates; some Antifederalists were genuinely swayed.
  • News as weapon: Once NH (July 2) and VA (July 11) ratified, the question shifted from "Should we?" to "Can we afford NOT to?"

Discussion Questions (10 min)

  • "How was our simulation similar to the real convention?"
    Expected: Core arguments were the same, Moderates as swing votes, close outcome
  • "How was it different?"
    Expected: We didn't know outside context (NH/VA ratifying), compressed 6 weeks into 70 min
  • "What does Melancton Smith's switch tell us about politics?"
    Expected: Context matters, pragmatism vs. principles, external events shape decisions
  • "Which faction do YOU think was right?"
    Let students debate ‚Äî no right answer

Agent vs. Trustee Framework (Write on Board)

Agent Model (Antifederalist)
  • Rep = Messenger
  • Follows instructions
  • Mirrors constituents
  • Accountability = obedience
  • Risk: Mob rule
Trustee Model (Federalist)
  • Rep = Leader
  • Uses judgment
  • Refines public opinion
  • Accountability = elections
  • Risk: Aristocracy
"Most modern democracies use a HYBRID model: Representatives use judgment day-to-day, but elections provide accountability. Burke's view won historically, but the tension remains."
