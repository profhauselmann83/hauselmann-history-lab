Ohio Valley, 1785

You grew it.
Now how do you sell it?

You're a farmer in western Pennsylvania. Your barn is full. The markets in Philadelphia and New York will pay good prices — but they're hundreds of miles away, on the other side of a mountain range.

Your Situation

You have barrels of corn, salt pork, and surplus grain. Eastern merchants want them badly. The question isn't whether to sell — it's whether getting your goods there is even possible.

Before you click forward, think: what obstacles might stand between your farm and those markets?

The obstacles

Every route is a problem

Tap each obstacle to understand why getting goods east was so difficult — and so expensive.

⛰️
The Appalachian Mountains
A wall between you and the coast

Few passes existed through the Appalachians, and those that did were steep, muddy, and treacherous. A loaded wagon could travel only 15–20 miles per day in good conditions — far less in bad weather.

Many wagons didn't make it at all. Broken axles, washed-out paths, and exhausted animals were routine.

The journey from Pittsburgh to Philadelphia overland: roughly 3–4 weeks each way.
💸
Overland freight costs
Transportation could cost more than the goods

Shipping one ton of goods 30 miles by road cost roughly the same as shipping it 3,000 miles by sea. Heavy, bulky farm goods — corn, grain, hay — were nearly worthless after paying wagon freight.

This is why farmers converted grain into whiskey. A bushel of corn (60 lbs) became 3 gallons of whiskey at roughly 25× more value per pound.

The Whiskey Rebellion of 1794 grew directly from this problem: a federal tax on whiskey felt like a tax on the only way western farmers could participate in the economy.
⚖️
No unified national market
Every state taxed goods from other states

Under the Articles of Confederation, Congress had no power to regulate interstate trade. States like New York charged tariffs on goods from New Jersey. Different states used different paper money of wildly varying value.

A merchant couldn't be sure what currency — or what price — awaited at the end of a month-long journey.

This chaos of competing state tariffs was a major reason founders pushed for the 1787 Constitutional Convention. The Commerce Clause was a direct response.
🌊
Rivers flow the wrong way
South to New Orleans — not east to markets

The Ohio and Mississippi rivers were cheap, reliable highways — but they led south, not east. Flatboats could carry enormous loads to New Orleans at a fraction of overland costs.

There was just one problem: New Orleans was controlled by Spain. And Spain could — and did — close the port to American trade.

This is the key tension. The rivers were the answer to overland costs. But using them meant depending on a foreign power's permission to survive economically.
The best bad option

Go with the current — south

Farmers figured out that rivers were the answer to crushing overland costs. But this created a new, dangerous problem.

The Flatboat Strategy

Farmers built flatboats and floated their goods down the Ohio into the Mississippi, arriving at New Orleans — a major port city where ships could carry goods to Atlantic markets. Flatboats could carry enormous loads for almost nothing compared to wagons.

The river route was fast, cheap, and reliable. Farmers could finally compete.

The Catch

New Orleans was controlled by Spain. The entire western economy now depended on Spain's permission to use their port. In 1784, Spain closed the Mississippi to American traffic.

Western farmers were furious. Some threatened to leave the United States entirely and seek alliance with Spain or Britain if Congress couldn't fix the problem.

Key Insight

The entire Mississippi River drainage basin — from Montana to Ohio — empties through a single point. Whoever holds that point holds the economic future of the American interior. This is why Jefferson called New Orleans "the single spot on the globe whose possessor is our natural and habitual enemy."

The Big Idea

Rivers were the roads. New Orleans was the gate.

The Ohio Valley crisis wasn't just about farmers not getting fair prices. It revealed a fundamental truth about geography and power: the natural drainage of the continent all flowed through one point.

Control New Orleans = control the economic future of the American interior. That understanding drove American foreign policy from the 1780s through the Louisiana Purchase — and shaped the Constitution itself.

Infrastructure is politics. Transportation is power. The founders understood that democracy requires economic participation — and economic participation required the river.
