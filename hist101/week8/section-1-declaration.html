Section 1: Declaration of Independence Activity

Analyzing Revolutionary Ideas and Historical Interpretations

Opening Frame

Before we look at what governments they built, we need to understand what they were fighting against and what they believed in. Let's examine the Declaration—not just as a statement of independence, but as a clue to what kind of government they would create afterward.

Part A: Two Historians' Interpretations

Historian #1: Bernard Bailyn's View (Ideological Interpretation)

The Declaration of Independence represents the colonists' deepest fears and beliefs. The colonists believed they saw a clear pattern: an evil and deliberate conspiracy to crush liberty in America. They saw evidence in the Stamp Act and the Coercive Acts. They also believed that America was destined to play a special role in history—to become "the foundation of a great and mighty empire...founded on such principles of liberty and freedom, both civil and religious." The colonists believed England was trying to enslave them, and that they should use "all the power which God has given them" to protect themselves.

Historian #2: Howard Zinn's View (Class Motivation Interpretation)

It seemed clear to the educated, upper-class colonists that something needed to be done to persuade the lower class to join the revolutionary cause and direct their anger against England. The solution was to find language inspiring to all classes—specific enough in grievances to fill people with anger against the British, but vague enough to avoid class conflict, and stirring enough to build patriotic feelings. Everything the Declaration was about—popular control over governments, the right of rebellion, fury at political tyranny, economic burdens, and military attacks—was well suited to unite large numbers of colonists and persuade even those who had grievances against one another to turn against England.
