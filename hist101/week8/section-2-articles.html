Section 2: Articles of Confederation

A Revolution Afraid of Power

ACT I: The Articles—A Revolution Afraid of Power

After fighting an empire controlled by a single person with too much power, Americans were terrified of centralized authority. They had just experienced what happens when one person—a king—can do whatever he wants. So when they created a national government, they deliberately made it weak.

The Articles of Confederation (adopted 1781, in effect until 1789) were designed to prevent tyranny. But this fear created a paradox that we're still grappling with today: How do you protect liberty WITHOUT making government so weak it can't function?

⚙️

What the Articles Actually Did

The Articles of Confederation Was Designed to PREVENT Tyranny But Also Prevented Effective Governing:
  • No real executive (no president to declare laws)
  • No national judiciary (no courts to enforce laws)
  • NO POWER TO TAX (could only request money from states—usually got ignored)
  • States retained full sovereignty (they were essentially independent countries loosely allied)
  • Congress cannot regulate interstate commerce (states fought each other over trade)
  • Amendments require unanimous consent from all 13 states (impossible to change anything)
  • Each state had one vote (big states angry about equal power)

Think about this: The government that won the Revolution couldn't pay soldiers, couldn't pay debts to other countries, couldn't stop states from fighting each other over trade, and couldn't collect taxes. By design. Because Americans were more afraid of a strong government than of chaos.

The Central Tension: Liberty vs. Capacity

The Paradox of Revolutionary Government

Liberty (Freedom from Tyranny)

The Founders wanted to prevent a strong executive from dominating. They wanted to make sure government couldn't impose taxes without consent. They wanted to protect individual rights.

Solution: Make government weak. Divide power. Require agreement from many people before anything happens.

Capacity (Ability to Function)

But governments need SOME power to collect taxes (to pay for defense and roads), regulate commerce (so states don't destroy each other's economies), and enforce laws (so contracts mean something).

Problem: If you make government too weak, it can't do anything. And an impotent government is useless.

🤔 Guiding Question: Is the problem of the 1780s too MUCH liberty (leading to chaos) or too LITTLE governmental capacity (leading to dysfunction)?

ACT II: Postwar Reality (Why the Articles Failed)

National Level Crisis

  • Continental currency depreciated (money worthless)
  • Soldiers remained unpaid for years
  • Foreign debts to France and other countries went unpaid
  • States were financially broken and refused to contribute

Local Reality: Massachusetts Example

Farmers in Massachusetts faced a concrete crisis. They were cash-poor from the war. They were in debt. And now courts were enforcing collection on those debts. Creditors were taking their land. By 1786, poor farmers in western Massachusetts began a rebellion (Shays' Rebellion) against foreclosure courts.

The rebellion was crushed, but the terrified property owners in the state—and across America—realized the Articles government was too weak to maintain order. This fear of chaos (and fear of mob rule) would drive the Constitutional Convention of 1787.

🤔 Key Insight: Was Shays' Rebellion proof that Americans had TOO MUCH liberty and needed a stronger government? Or was it proof that poor farmers had too LITTLE power and needed representation? This is the debate we're about to see in the next section.
