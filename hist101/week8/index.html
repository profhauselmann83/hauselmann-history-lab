Section 2: Declaration of Independence Activity

Opening Frame

Before we look at what governments they built, we need to understand what they were fighting against and what they believed in. Let's examine the Declaration—not just as a statement of independence, but as a clue to what kind of government they would create afterward.

Part A: Two Historians' Interpretations

Historian #1: Bernard Bailyn's View (Ideological Interpretation)

The Declaration of Independence represents the colonists' deepest fears and beliefs. The colonists believed they saw a clear pattern: an evil and deliberate conspiracy to crush liberty in America. They saw evidence in the Stamp Act and the Coercive Acts. They also believed that America was destined to play a special role in history—to become "the foundation of a great and mighty empire...founded on such principles of liberty and freedom, both civil and religious." The colonists believed England was trying to enslave them, and that they should use "all the power which God has given them" to protect themselves.

Historian #2: Howard Zinn's View (Class Motivation Interpretation)

It seemed clear to the educated, upper-class colonists that something needed to be done to persuade the lower class to join the revolutionary cause and direct their anger against England. The solution was to find language inspiring to all classes—specific enough in grievances to fill people with anger against the British, but vague enough to avoid class conflict, and stirring enough to build patriotic feelings. Everything the Declaration was about—popular control over governments, the right of rebellion, fury at political tyranny, economic burdens, and military attacks—was well suited to unite large numbers of colonists and persuade even those who had grievances against one another to turn against England.

Bridge: From Declaration to Constitutional Design

Now here's the critical insight: When the Founders sat down to write state constitutions and a national government, they were writing against these grievances. They were saying: "How do we make sure a government can NEVER do these things again?"

For example:

  • King imposed taxes without consent → Founders created governments where people or their representatives had to consent to taxes
  • King made judges dependent on his will → Founders made judges independent and difficult to remove
  • King dissolved legislatures → Founders protected the legislature's right to meet
  • King kept standing armies → Founders feared executive power and limited it

So the question becomes: Which historian was right—Bailyn or Zinn? And will your answer change as you see what kind of government the Founders actually created?

Section 4: Document Comparison

You've now learned about three documents: the Declaration of Independence (which listed grievances), the Articles of Confederation (the national government), and state constitutions (state governments). Now let's compare them to see what Americans actually feared and what they tried to create.

Comparison Questions

Question 1: Executive Power

The Declaration complained about a king with too much power. Look at what the Articles of Confederation and state constitutions said about executive power (the governor or leader). What power did they give to executives? What power did they take away? Why do you think they made these choices?

Question 2: Taxation

Grievance 17 complained: "For imposing taxes on us without our consent." How did the state constitutions and Articles of Confederation handle taxation? Who had the power to tax? Who had to agree?

Question 3: The Central Tension

The Founders were terrified of concentrated power (after what they'd experienced with the King). But governments need some power to function. Based on what you've learned about the Articles and state constitutions, were the Founders more afraid of too much government power, or too little? What evidence supports your answer?

Question 4: Synthesis - Historians Revisited

Now that you've seen what kind of governments the Founders actually created, do you think Bailyn (ideological) or Zinn (class-based motivation) was more right about why they wrote the Declaration? What does their choice to create weak governments tell us about their real priorities?
