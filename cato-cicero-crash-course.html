Start here

A fast but serious entry into Roman republican thought
What this page is for: a guided, self-paced crash course that helps you read (1) a morally conservative Roman voice and (2) a philosophical-rhetorical defender of republican legality—then compare what each thinks “keeps a republic alive.”

Your working questions

  • What is the relationship between private behavior and public stability?
  • Can a republic survive without shared norms and virtue?
  • When institutions fail, do you reach for discipline (Cato) or persuasion & law (Cicero)?

How to read (recommended)

  • Read 10–20 minutes.
  • Extract one claim and one fear from the text.
  • Write 6–10 sentences answering: “What kind of political world does this author assume?”
Suggested schedule (30–45 minutes, 4–5 days/week)
  • Mon–Tue: primary text excerpt + 10-minute reflection
  • Wed: scholarly orientation piece
  • Thu: return to primary text with “what changed in my understanding?”
  • Fri (optional): synthesis note (½ page)
Note on “Cato”: This hub focuses on Cato the Elder (the 2nd-century BCE censor and author of On Agriculture). Cicero also writes about Cato the Younger later; that’s a different figure.

Week 1 — Frame the Roman Republic

Goal: build a baseline for how Romans linked institutions, norms, and stability.

Week 1 readings (orientation)
  • Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — “Cicero”
  • Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy — “Cicero”
  • SEP — “Ancient Political Philosophy” (broader frame)
Reflection prompt (copy into Notes)

What does a “republic” require besides laws and offices? List (1) a habit, (2) a value, (3) a fear.

Week 2 — Cato: virtue, austerity, and the moral policing of elites

Goal: read On Agriculture as a political-moral text: management, hierarchy, discipline, and cultural anxiety.

Primary text (open)
  • Cato, De Agri Cultura (LacusCurtius)
  • Perseus Catalog record for De Agricultura (editions/translations)
High-yield questions for Cato
  • Where does “morality” show up as an administrative practice?
  • Is Cato protecting republican freedom—or narrowing it?
  • What does Cato’s hostility to “luxury” really target: consumption, status competition, foreignness, or elite impunity?

Week 3 — Cicero: duty, law, rhetoric, and legitimacy

Goal: pair On Duties (ethical framework) with a crisis speech (political practice).

Primary texts (open)
  • Cicero, On Moral Duties (De Officiis) (Online Library of Liberty)
  • Perseus Catalog — De Officiis (Miller translation record)
  • Cicero, In Catilinam (Scaife/Perseus viewer, English)
High-yield questions for Cicero
  • Does Cicero treat rhetoric as a civic duty or a political weapon—or both?
  • What is the relationship between “law” and “force” in a collapsing republic?
  • Where do you see Cicero trying to preserve norms by describing their violation?

Week 4 — Compare & integrate: two strategies for saving a republic

Goal: build a durable framework you can reuse when thinking about democratic erosion, norm collapse, and moral politics.

Optional “scholarly map” for Cicero (excellent next step)
  • The Cambridge Companion to Cicero (publisher page)
  • Cambridge page with table of contents
Reusable framework: When a polity feels unstable, reformers often argue for discipline (moral enforcement) or legitimacy (norms + persuasion + law). This month trains you to see both logics clearly—and where each breaks.
