Activity Setup

Purpose

Students experience the shift from authority-based reasoning to evidence- and reason-based reasoning by inhabiting different early modern worldviews‚Äîand discovering that they do not resolve disputes the same way.

This is not about "getting the right answer."
It is about how decisions are justified.

  • Decide whether to use secret objectives (increases drama)
  • Prepare to write key words on board (tradition, evidence, fear, order, conscience, reform)
  • Review the 5 cases and select 2-4 to use (witchcraft + torture recommended if short on time)
  • Print or project role descriptions for students

Case 1: A Confession Obtained Under Torture

A man is accused of a serious crime‚Äîtreason, heresy, or murder. There are rumors, but no physical evidence. After several days of imprisonment, he is subjected to prolonged torture. Eventually, he confesses in detail. He names accomplices. He begs forgiveness. He says what the authorities want to hear.

The court records note that the confession was "freely given"‚Äîafter the torture ended. There is no other evidence. But the confession brings closure. The crowd is reassured. The authorities can move on.

Prompt Questions:

  • Is the confession valid?
  • Does torture reveal truth or produce obedience?
  • What matters more: certainty or stability?

Role Positions

üü• Traditional Authority: A confession obtained under torture is valid because it restores order. Better a resolved injustice than a society unraveling from doubt.
üü® Religious Conscience: Torture corrupts truth and offends God. A confession wrung from pain is not testimony‚Äîit is desperation. An unjust verdict endangers not just bodies, but salvation.
üü¶ Enlightenment Rationalist: A confession under torture proves nothing. Pain produces obedience, not truth. Truth cannot be beaten out of a human being.
üü™ Political Authority: The confession is useful. It ends unrest. Whether it is true matters less than whether the matter is settled. The court cannot afford paralysis.

Case 2: A Law Justified by Tradition

A harsh punishment is enforced for a minor offense‚Äîpublic shaming, mutilation, or execution. When questioned, officials respond simply: this is how it has always been done. The law is old. Older than anyone alive. Older than memory.

Some argue it is cruel. Others say it deters crime. Most people obey it without thinking. No one can remember who wrote the law. But everyone knows what happens if it is broken.

Prompt Questions:

  • Does age equal legitimacy?
  • Who benefits from this law continuing?
  • What happens if laws must justify themselves?

Role Positions

üü• Traditional Authority: A law that has endured generations carries legitimacy by its very survival. What has endured has proven its worth.
üü® Religious Conscience: Tradition alone cannot justify injustice. If a law violates God's moral order, its age only deepens the sin. God does not bless cruelty simply because it is old.
üü¶ Enlightenment Rationalist: A law must serve human well-being. Custom explains laws; reason must justify them.
üü™ Political Authority: The law exists because it works‚Äîor at least has not caused collapse. Stability is a public good.

Case 3: A Ruler Claiming Divine Right

A king faces growing resistance. Taxes are unpopular. Religious tensions are rising. Critics claim the king is abusing power. In response, the ruler makes a clear statement:

"My authority comes directly from God. To obey me is to obey divine will. To resist me is rebellion against heaven."

Supporters nod. Dissenters grow quiet. But not everyone is convinced. Some believe God would never endorse injustice. Others fear that disagreement itself invites chaos.

Prompt Questions:

  • Can authority exist without accountability?
  • Who decides whether the ruler is legitimate?
  • What happens if subjects disagree?

Role Positions

üü• Traditional Authority: The ruler's authority comes from God, and questioning it invites disorder. Without obedience, society dissolves.
üü® Religious Conscience: A ruler who violates God's law forfeits divine legitimacy. No earthly crown outranks divine judgment.
üü¶ Enlightenment Rationalist: Authority must be justified by consent and reason, not divine claims that cannot be verified. Claims beyond examination invite abuse.
üü™ Political Authority: Public challenges to authority destabilize the state. Legitimacy matters less than control.

Case 4: A Witchcraft Accusation

A woman is accused of witchcraft. No one saw her cast a spell. But neighbors testify that livestock died after she passed by. A child fell ill. Someone dreamed she was present. The community is frightened. The harvest has failed. Something feels wrong.

The accused denies everything. There is no physical proof. But fear is spreading.

Some argue disbelief risks God's wrath. Others worry the accusation itself is dangerous.

Prompt Questions:

  • What counts as evidence?
  • Is fear itself a danger?
  • Is disbelief more destabilizing than belief?

Role Positions

üü• Traditional Authority: Communal testimony reflects collective wisdom. Fear itself signals danger. Inaction is itself a threat.
üü® Religious Conscience: False accusations offend God. But ignoring witchcraft invites divine wrath. We must fear God more than rumor.
üü¶ Enlightenment Rationalist: There is no empirical evidence for witchcraft. Dreams and misfortune prove nothing. A society ruled by fear is already unjust.
üü™ Political Authority: Public fear must be managed. Ignoring accusations risks unrest. Fear spreads faster than truth.

Case 5: A Scientific Claim Contradicted by Evidence

A respected authority‚Äîa scholar, cleric, or court-appointed expert‚Äîteaches a well-established truth. It appears in books. It has been taught for generations. It fits scripture and tradition.

But new observations contradict it. Measurements don't align. Experiments suggest something else is happening.

The authority dismisses the data. The challenger is younger. Less powerful. But the evidence persists.

Prompt Questions:

  • Does expertise outweigh data?
  • Who gets to challenge whom?
  • What happens if everyone questions everything?

Role Positions

üü• Traditional Authority: Established knowledge exists for a reason. If everyone questions everything, authority collapses. Doubt is contagious.
üü® Religious Conscience: Truth comes from God, but human interpretations can err. Reason must serve moral truth.
üü¶ Enlightenment Rationalist: Evidence outweighs authority. Knowledge advances through questioning. Progress requires doubt.
üü™ Political Authority: Public challenges to authority destabilize institutions. New ideas must be managed carefully. Truth at the wrong moment can be dangerous.

Debrief & Discussion

‚úÖ The Enlightenment Problem: By the final case, students should feel this clearly:
If authority, tradition, religion, fear, and expertise can all be questioned... what holds society together?

Escalation Logic

  • Case 1 (Torture): Feels procedural and concrete ‚Üí justice system
  • Case 2 (Tradition): Challenges legitimacy of law itself
  • Case 3 (Divine Right): Attacks the foundation of political authority
  • Case 4 (Witchcraft): Exposes fear as a social force
  • Case 5 (Science): Destabilizes knowledge itself

1. Connection to Course Content

  • How did these different worldviews show up in the Reformation?
  • Where do we see traditional authority vs. conscience in the religious conflicts we've studied?
  • How does this activity help explain why the Enlightenment led to political revolutions?

2. Decision-Making Process

  • What was hardest about staying in your role's worldview?
  • When did you want to "break character" and use modern reasoning?
  • Did any case make you sympathize with a worldview you initially disagreed with?

3. Historical Thinking

  • Why might reasonable people in the past have supported torture, traditional laws, or witchcraft trials?
  • What were they afraid of losing if they changed their approach?
  • How does understanding their fears change how we judge their actions?

4. Modern Connections

  • Do we still see conflicts between these worldviews today?
  • When do we prioritize stability over change? Evidence over tradition?
  • Are there modern equivalents to "witchcraft accusations"‚Äîfears that spread despite lack of evidence?

Key Takeaway

The Enlightenment did not simply replace one set of answers with better ones.

It transformed how answers were justified‚Äîand in doing so, it made the political revolutions that followed both possible and inevitable.
