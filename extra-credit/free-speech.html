Overview

This assignment invites you to reflect on the principles of free speech and academic freedom explored in Marie McMullan's presentation. By examining the history of free speech protections, the challenges facing them on modern college campuses, and your own experiences with freedom of expression, you'll deepen your understanding of what it means to protect the rights of others—even when we disagree with what they're saying.

Choose Your Option

Option 1: Speech We Hate & the Marketplace of Ideas

Marie emphasized that free speech protections sometimes mean defending "speech we hate." She explained the "marketplace of ideas" theory: the belief that truth will emerge when ideas compete freely, rather than when the government silences speech it dislikes. Reflect on this tension:

  • Can you think of speech or ideas you find hateful, hurtful, or wrong? How does the marketplace of ideas theory address the harm that speech can cause?
  • What's the danger of allowing governments (or institutions) to decide which speech is "too harmful" to protect?
  • How do you balance protecting vulnerable communities from harmful speech with protecting everyone's freedom to speak?

Option 2: Free Speech on Campus & Your Rights

Marie explained that the First Amendment applies with full force to public colleges and universities—and that campuses are places "where the vigilant protection of constitutional freedoms is nowhere more vital." She also noted that censorship on campus often takes forms like denying funding to groups, punishing students for social media posts, or creating "free speech zones." Reflect on free speech on your campus:

  • Have you witnessed or experienced limitations on free speech at MJC or other educational institutions? What happened, and what was the justification given?
  • Marie said the "antidote to bad speech is more speech." What does this mean, and do you think it's realistic in a college environment?
  • How would you balance free speech protections with creating a campus community where all students feel safe and respected?

Additional Reflection Prompts

If you find yourself stuck or want to deepen your reflection, consider these additional questions:

  1. Marie traced the history of free speech from John Milton in the 1600s to modern times. What surprised you most about this history?
  2. She talked about different categories of unprotected speech (incitement, fighting words, true threats, etc.). Why is it important that these categories are narrowly defined?
  3. Marie emphasized the importance of protecting speech across the political spectrum. How do we practice this on our own campus?
  4. What role do student journalists play in defending free speech on campus?
  5. How does free speech connect to the larger question of the series: "Who gets to define freedom, and at what cost?"
