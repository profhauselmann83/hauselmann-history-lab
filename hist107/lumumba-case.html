YOUR ASSIGNMENT

You are a historical investigator examining the assassination of Patrice Lumumba — the first democratically elected Prime Minister of the Democratic Republic of Congo. He took office on June 30, 1960. He was dead by January 17, 1961. Seven months.

You have been given access to five documents. Each one comes from a different source with a different perspective and a different stake in what happened. Your job is not just to read what each document says — it is to ask who produced it, when, and why, and use those questions to evaluate the evidence.

After working through all five documents, you will complete an Evidence Board and write a Final Claim answering the central question: Why was Patrice Lumumba assassinated?

BACKGROUND: THE CASE

Belgium had colonized the Congo since 1884. At independence in 1960, there were fewer than ten university-educated Congolese citizens in a country the size of Western Europe. Belgium left fast and left nothing behind.

Lumumba's party, the MNC, won the most votes in the first election. He became Prime Minister. Within days of independence, the Congolese army mutinied, and the mineral-rich province of Katanga — containing copper, gold, and uranium — declared secession with Belgian backing. Lumumba was dismissed from office in September 1960. He was arrested, beaten, and transferred to Katanga. He was executed on January 17, 1961.

THE SUSPECTS

BELGIUM
FORMER COLONIAL POWER
Maintained mining interests in Katanga. Many Belgian citizens and companies still operating in Congo at independence. Relations with Lumumba strained from independence day. Backed Katanga's secession.
UNITED STATES (CIA)
COLD WAR SUPERPOWER
Feared Soviet expansion in Africa. Filed internal reports labeling Lumumba a communist threat before independence. Church Committee (1975) found CIA was "actively involved" in assassination plots.
JOSEPH MOBUTU
CONGOLESE MILITARY COLONEL
Staged military coup in September 1960. Had ties to Western intelligence. Ordered Lumumba's transfer to Katanga. Later became dictator of Congo (renamed Zaire) and ruled for 32 years.
KATANGESE AUTHORITIES
SECESSIONIST PROVINCE
Controlled the province where Lumumba was held and killed. Led by Moise Tshombe. Backed by Belgian troops and mining interests. Firing squad was composed of Katangese forces.

📜 DOCUMENT A TEXTBOOK ACCOUNT
Document A — World History Textbook
Published: 1999  |  Source: Holt, Rinehart and Winston
Type: Secondary Source  |  Audience: High school students
🔍 Investigator's Note: This is the kind of source you encounter most often. A textbook is a secondary source — written later, for an educational audience. Notice what it includes, what it leaves out, and how certain questions go unanswered.

Like the Portuguese, the Belgians had never really considered preparing Africans in the huge Congo for self-government, much less for independence. As a Congolese nationalist movement emerged after World War II, however, the Belgian government agreed that it should prepare the colony for self-government. Believing that the Congolese had not developed the institutions or acquired the experience needed to manage a modern state, Belgians supported a 30-year timetable to prepare them for independence. Distrusting the Belgians, African nationalists demanded immediate self-government. Consequently, in 1960 Belgium suddenly announced that it would withdraw completely within a year.

With little preparation, many political parties representing different ethnic groups, geographical regions and political beliefs participated in the first elections ever held in the new Democratic Republic of the Congo. Former postal clerk Patrice Lumumba became prime minister, while his rival and political enemy Joseph Kasavubu became president. Lumumba remained opposed to European influence. Angered by his stance, Belgian technicians and experts left the Congo in droves. This proved to be a major blow to the new country's economy.

Soon, the army mutinied and the copper-rich province of Katanga seceded. The country was plunged into a crisis. An assassin killed Lumumba in 1961, and Kasavubu assumed full power. The chaos and violence continued.

Source: World History: Continuity and Change. Austin, Texas: Holt, Rinehart and Winston, 1999, pp. 776-777.

Document B — Lumumba's Independence Day Speech
Date: June 30, 1960  |  Delivered at independence ceremony
Speaker: Patrice Lumumba  |  Type: Primary Source
EXHIBIT B
🔍 Investigator's Note: Lumumba was NOT on the program. The King of Belgium had just given a speech praising the "peaceful" and "friendly" transfer of power. Lumumba took the podium unannounced. Belgian officials and Congolese citizens were both in the audience.

Although this independence of the Congo is being proclaimed today by agreement with Belgium, an amicable country, with which we are on equal terms, no Congolese will ever forget that independence was won in struggle, a persevering and inspired struggle carried on from day to day.

We have experienced forced labor in exchange for pay that did not allow us to satisfy our hunger, to clothe ourselves, to have decent lodgings or to bring up our children as dearly loved ones. Morning, noon and night we were subjected to jeers, insults and blows because we were "Negroes."...

We have not forgotten that the law was never the same for the white and the black, that it was lenient to the ones, and cruel and inhuman to the others. We have not forgotten that in the cities the mansions were for the whites and the tumbledown huts for the blacks...

All that, my brothers, brought us untold suffering. But...we tell you that henceforth all that is finished with. The Republic of the Congo has been proclaimed and our beloved country's future is now in the hands of its own people.

Source: Lumumba's Independence Day Speech, June 30, 1960.

TOP SECRET
🔍 Investigator's Note: This document was never meant for public consumption. It is an internal CIA brief — the kind of document written for a small circle of American intelligence officials. Notice how it describes Lumumba. Notice what it does NOT say. Notice what filing it under "the Communist Bloc" reveals about how the U.S. was framing the situation.

Lumumba Moves to Consolidate Power in the Congo: The government formed by Congo Premier-designate Patrice Lumumba is "weak" and has a "strong leftist tinge" according to the consulate general in Leopoldville. Members of opposition parties named to the cabinet are described as little known and likely to be dominated by Lumumba. Lumumba allocated to himself the key post of defense minister, which carries with it control over the Congo's 24,000-man security force, whose Belgian officers are expected to remain after the Congo's independence on 30 June.

Among ten secretaries of state, however, five—including secretary for economic coordination and planning—are Communist inclined. Anti-Lumumba leaders continue to emphasize Lumumba's Communist contacts, and in conversation with US officials have deplored the failure of the United States to check "Communist forces" in the Congo. Available evidence suggests that Lumumba is a leftist-oriented "neutralist" who has accepted financial aid from Communist sources.

Source: CIA Daily Brief, June 27, 1960.

📰 DOCUMENT D NEW YORK TIMES · AUGUST 1960
Document D — New York Times, August 6, 1960
Author: Thomas F. Brady  |  Date: August 6, 1960
Type: Newspaper Report  |  Audience: General public
PRESS
🔍 Investigator's Note: By August, the crisis has escalated fast. The Congolese army mutinied. Katanga — rich in copper, gold, and uranium — has declared secession with Belgian troops backing it. Lumumba, having been turned away by the U.S. and the UN, is now telegraphing the UN Secretary-General directly and making a very specific accusation about Belgium.

Premier Patrice Lumumba of the Congo telegraphed Secretary General Dag Hammarskjöld today.... Mr. Lumumba accused the Belgian government of "provoking" the secession of Katanga and maintaining troops there "to consolidate" this action. He said the moves of the dissident premier of Katanga, Moise Tshombe, were "dictated" by his Belgian military advisers.

After disclosing the text of the telegram to Mr. Hammarskjöld, Mr. Lumumba added that he firmly approved of his Government's decision to force the Belgian Ambassador to leave the Congo by Monday. He said his Government had taken the decision to break off all relations with Belgium on July 14. He said the decision...was taken after Belgium's "flagrant violation of the friendship treaty by instigation of the secession of Katanga."

Source: Thomas F. Brady, "Congolese Premier Threatens to 'Revise Position' Unless U.N. Sends Troops Now," The New York Times, August 6, 1960.

⚖️ DOCUMENT E BELGIAN COMMISSION · 2001
Document E — Belgian Parliamentary Commission Report
Date: 2001  |  Forty years after the assassination
Author: Belgian Parliamentary Enquiry Committee  |  Purpose: Official investigation
OFFICIAL
🔍 Investigator's Note: This is the strangest document in the file. In 1999 — almost forty years after Lumumba's death — the Belgian parliament established a commission to investigate its own country's possible role. This is their conclusion. Read carefully: note what they admit, and note the careful language they choose.

The different speeches of 30 June 1960 confirmed the mutual distrust between Lumumba and the Belgian government, which undoubtedly influenced their reactions to the subsequent events. From the beginning of the crisis, it was clear that it led to a fundamental split between Lumumba and the Belgian government and that gradually, not only the Belgian government, but also many other governments and many layers of Belgian and Congolese society were campaigning...to bring about Lumumba's downfall politically.

As indicated, the Belgian government found itself pressured by public opinion which was very harshly opposed to the events in the Congo after independence.... It was hard for the government to justify a laissez-faire attitude regarding the tens of thousands of Europeans in the Congo and it was also worried about the financial and economic losses the Congo crisis could cause. The stakes were high for the Belgian financial groups in the Congo. Repeatedly, this was brought to the attention of the government.

The committee concluded that Belgium was "morally responsible for the circumstances leading to the death of Lumumba."

Source: "The Conclusions of the Enquiry Committee," Belgian Parliamentary Commission, 2001.
