YOUR ASSIGNMENT

You are a historical investigator examining the assassination of Patrice Lumumba — the first democratically elected Prime Minister of the Democratic Republic of Congo. He took office June 30, 1960. He was dead by January 17, 1961. Seven months.

You have five documents from five different sources. Your job is not just to read what each says — it is to ask who produced it, when, and why. After all five documents, you will complete an Evidence Board and write a Final Claim answering the central question.

BACKGROUND

Belgium colonized the Congo since 1884. At independence in 1960, there were fewer than ten university-educated Congolese citizens in a country the size of Western Europe. Lumumba’s party won the first election. He became Prime Minister. Within days the army mutinied and the mineral-rich province of Katanga — copper, gold, uranium — declared secession with Belgian backing. Lumumba was arrested, beaten, transferred to Katanga, and executed January 17, 1961.

THE SUSPECTS

BELGIUM
FORMER COLONIAL POWER
Maintained mining interests in Katanga. Relations with Lumumba strained from independence day. Backed Katanga’s secession. Belgian commission (2001) admitted “moral responsibility.”
UNITED STATES / CIA
COLD WAR SUPERPOWER
Feared Soviet expansion. Filed reports labeling Lumumba a communist threat before independence. Church Committee (1975) found CIA “actively involved” in assassination plots.
JOSEPH MOBUTU
CONGOLESE MILITARY COLONEL
Staged coup September 1960. Ordered Lumumba’s transfer to Katanga. Later became dictator of Congo (renamed Zaire) for 32 years.
KATANGESE AUTHORITIES
SECESSIONIST PROVINCE
Controlled the province where Lumumba was held and killed. Backed by Belgian troops and mining interests. Firing squad was Katangese forces.

📜 DOCUMENT A TEXTBOOK · 1999
Document A — World History Textbook
Published: 1999 | Holt, Rinehart and Winston | Secondary Source | Audience: High school students
Investigator’s Note: This is the most familiar kind of source. Notice what it includes, what it leaves out, and how the assassination itself is handled.

Like the Portuguese, the Belgians had never really considered preparing Africans in the huge Congo for self-government, much less for independence. As a Congolese nationalist movement emerged after World War II, however, the Belgian government agreed that it should prepare the colony for self-government. Belgians supported a 30-year timetable. Distrusting the Belgians, African nationalists demanded immediate self-government. Consequently, in 1960 Belgium suddenly announced it would withdraw completely within a year.

With little preparation, many political parties participated in the first elections ever held in the new Democratic Republic of the Congo. Former postal clerk Patrice Lumumba became prime minister, while his rival Joseph Kasavubu became president. Lumumba remained opposed to European influence. Angered by his stance, Belgian technicians and experts left the Congo in droves. This proved to be a major blow to the new country’s economy.

Soon, the army mutinied and the copper-rich province of Katanga seceded. The country was plunged into a crisis. An assassin killed Lumumba in 1961, and Kasavubu assumed full power. The chaos and violence continued.

Source: World History: Continuity and Change. Holt, Rinehart and Winston, 1999, pp. 776-777.

🎙 DOCUMENT B INDEPENDENCE SPEECH · 1960
Document B — Lumumba’s Independence Day Speech EXHIBIT B
Date: June 30, 1960 | Speaker: Patrice Lumumba | Primary Source | Public ceremony
Investigator’s Note: Lumumba was NOT on the program. The King of Belgium had just praised the “peaceful” transfer of power. Lumumba took the podium unannounced. Both Belgian officials and Congolese citizens were in the audience.

Although this independence of the Congo is being proclaimed today by agreement with Belgium, an amicable country, with which we are on equal terms, no Congolese will ever forget that independence was won in struggle, a persevering and inspired struggle carried on from day to day.

We have experienced forced labor in exchange for pay that did not allow us to satisfy our hunger, to clothe ourselves, to have decent lodgings or to bring up our children as dearly loved ones. Morning, noon and night we were subjected to jeers, insults and blows because we were “Negroes.”...

We have not forgotten that the law was never the same for the white and the black, that it was lenient to the ones, and cruel and inhuman to the others. We have not forgotten that in the cities the mansions were for the whites and the tumbledown huts for the blacks...

All that, my brothers, brought us untold suffering. But...we tell you that henceforth all that is finished with. The Republic of the Congo has been proclaimed and our beloved country’s future is now in the hands of its own people.

Source: Lumumba’s Independence Day Speech, June 30, 1960.

🔒 DOCUMENT C TOP SECRET · CIA
Document C — CIA Daily Brief TOP SECRET
Date: June 27, 1960 — three days before independence | Filed under: “The Communist Bloc”
Investigator’s Note: This was never meant for public consumption — an internal brief for a small circle of American intelligence officials. Notice how it describes Lumumba. Notice what filing it under “the Communist Bloc” reveals.

Lumumba Moves to Consolidate Power in the Congo: The government formed by Congo Premier-designate Patrice Lumumba is “weak” and has a “strong leftist tinge.” Lumumba allocated to himself the key post of defense minister, which carries control over the Congo’s 24,000-man security force.

Among ten secretaries of state, five — including secretary for economic coordination and planning — are Communist inclined. Anti-Lumumba leaders continue to emphasize Lumumba’s Communist contacts, and in conversation with US officials have deplored the failure of the United States to check “Communist forces” in the Congo. Available evidence suggests that Lumumba is a leftist-oriented “neutralist” who has accepted financial aid from Communist sources.

Source: CIA Daily Brief, June 27, 1960.

📰 DOCUMENT D NEW YORK TIMES · AUG 1960
Document D — New York Times, August 6, 1960 PRESS
Author: Thomas F. Brady | Date: August 6, 1960 | Newspaper | General public audience
Investigator’s Note: By August the crisis has escalated rapidly. Katanga — rich in copper, gold, and uranium — has declared secession with Belgian troops. Lumumba, turned away by the US and UN, is now telegraphing the UN Secretary-General and making a specific accusation about Belgium.

Premier Patrice Lumumba of the Congo telegraphed Secretary General Dag Hammarskjöld today.... Mr. Lumumba accused the Belgian government of “provoking” the secession of Katanga and maintaining troops there “to consolidate” this action. He said the moves of the dissident premier of Katanga, Moise Tshombe, were “dictated” by his Belgian military advisers.

After disclosing the text of the telegram to Mr. Hammarskjöld, Mr. Lumumba added that he firmly approved of his Government’s decision to force the Belgian Ambassador to leave the Congo by Monday. He said the decision was taken after Belgium’s “flagrant violation of the friendship treaty by instigation of the secession of Katanga.”

Source: Thomas F. Brady, New York Times, August 6, 1960.

⚖ DOCUMENT E BELGIAN COMMISSION · 2001
Document E — Belgian Parliamentary Commission Report OFFICIAL
Date: 2001 — forty years after the assassination | Belgian Parliamentary Enquiry Committee
Investigator’s Note: The strangest document in the file. In 1999 the Belgian parliament established a commission to investigate its own country’s possible role. This is their conclusion. Note what they admit — and note the precise language they choose.

The different speeches of 30 June 1960 confirmed the mutual distrust between Lumumba and the Belgian government, which undoubtedly influenced their reactions to the subsequent events. From the beginning of the crisis, it was clear that it led to a fundamental split between Lumumba and the Belgian government and that gradually, many governments and many layers of Belgian and Congolese society were campaigning...to bring about Lumumba’s downfall politically.

As indicated, the Belgian government found itself pressured by public opinion which was very harshly opposed to the events in the Congo after independence.... It was also worried about the financial and economic losses the Congo crisis could cause. The stakes were high for the Belgian financial groups in the Congo. Repeatedly, this was brought to the attention of the government.

The committee concluded that Belgium was “morally responsible for the circumstances leading to the death of Lumumba.”

Source: “The Conclusions of the Enquiry Committee,” Belgian Parliamentary Commission, 2001.
