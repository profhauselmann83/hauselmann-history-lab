üóúÔ∏è Part 2 ‚Äî Primary Sources Documents, Images & Records

Primary sources are original materials created at the time ‚Äî letters, government reports, newspaper articles, photographs, trade statistics, maps, and more. Including at least one primary source in your archive shows you can work with evidence, not just read about it.

‚öíÔ∏è What Counts as a Primary Source for This Project?

  • Government and colonial records ‚Äî trade reports, census data, parliamentary debates about tariffs or labor
  • Newspapers and periodicals ‚Äî contemporary accounts of commodity production, labor conditions, or market prices
  • Company records and charters ‚Äî documents from trading companies like the East India Company or United Fruit
  • Photographs and maps ‚Äî plantation photographs, trade route maps, port scenes
  • Personal accounts ‚Äî letters, diaries, or testimony from workers, traders, or colonizers
  • Political and legal documents ‚Äî treaties, tariff schedules, labor contracts, anti-slavery petitions

‚úÖ Tips for Annotating Your Sources

  • For primary sources: Describe who created it, when, and why. What does it reveal? What does it leave out? Whose perspective is represented ‚Äî and whose is absent?
  • For books from the catalog: Note the author's argument, not just the topic. A strong source makes a claim; background information alone is not enough.
  • When in doubt, Ask a Librarian: MJC librarians offer live chat, drop-in help, and scheduled research appointments ‚Äî all free.
