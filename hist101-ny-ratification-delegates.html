Raising the Eleventh Pillar
The Ratification Debate of 1788

New York State Ratifying Convention · Poughkeepsie · June–July 1788

Federalists
Federalist
Chancellor Robert R. Livingston
New York City and County
Role:Party Leader
Born:November 27, 1746

Leader of the Federalist party at Poughkeepsie. Head of the illustrious Livingston family, first Chancellor of New York, and one of the five-member committee that drafted the Declaration of Independence. His purpose is to secure New York's ratification of the Constitution.

Born to one of New York's wealthiest and most powerful families, he inherited Clermont Manor (which the British burned in 1777). Educated at King's College, admitted to the bar in 1768. Co-wrote the New York State Constitution with John Jay and Gouverneur Morris (1777).

Special role: If the instructor rates the Constitution as Antifederalist in character after all debate, Livingston is directed to threaten the secession of New York, Richmond, Kings, and Westchester counties from the state.

Federalist
Alexander Hamilton
New York City and County
Role:Party Lieutenant
Born:c. 1755, Nevis, British West Indies

Federalist party lieutenant at the convention. Born in the Caribbean, he rose through the Revolutionary War as Washington's aide-de-camp and led the famous nighttime bayonet charge at Yorktown. Co-author of The Federalist Papers with Madison and Jay.

After the war he studied law, opened a Wall Street practice, co-founded the Bank of New York, and helped found the New York Manumission Society. Married Elizabeth Schuyler, daughter of General Philip Schuyler.

Federalist
John Jay
New York City and County
Born:December 23, 1745, New York City

One of the most distinguished statesmen in America. Served as president of the Continental Congress, minister to Spain, and co-negotiated the Treaty of Paris (1783). Currently serving as Secretary of Foreign Affairs.

Co-authored The Federalist with Hamilton and Madison. Just before the convention, published An Address to the People of the State of New York under the pseudonym "A Citizen of New York," which many consider more persuasive than the Federalist itself.

Federalist
James Duane
New York City and County
Born:February 6, 1733

Mayor of New York City and state senator. A prominent lawyer with vast landholdings — including a township named after him, Duanesburg. Served in both the First and Second Continental Congresses and signed the Articles of Confederation for New York.

Presided over Rutgers v. Waddington (1784), where Hamilton argued for judicial review. Long-time Federalist, committed to increased national power.

Federalist
John Sloss Hobart
New York City and County
Born:May 6, 1738, Fairfield, CT

Former Sons of Liberty member and prominent jurist. Appointed associate justice of the Supreme Court of Judicature in 1777, serving alongside Chief Justice John Jay. Yale graduate (1757) with deep roots in Suffolk County, Long Island.

Served in all four Provincial Congresses and participated in numerous key committees, including those that drafted the resolution approving the Declaration of Independence and the new state constitution.

Federalist
Isaac Roosevelt
New York City and County
Born:December 19, 1726

Great-great-grandfather of President Franklin D. Roosevelt. A prosperous merchant and sugar refiner who helped found the Bank of New York with Hamilton (1784) and now serves as its president. Of Dutch Reformed heritage, he laid the cornerstone of the North Dutch Church (1767).

Served in all four Provincial Congresses. Moved his family to Dutchess County during the British occupation, where he served in the militia and helped convert British currency.

Federalist
Richard Morris
New York City and County
Born:August 15, 1730

Chief Justice of New York (appointed 1779, replacing John Jay). Brother of Lewis Morris (who signed the Declaration of Independence) and half-brother of Gouverneur Morris (who drafted the Constitution). Yale graduate (1748).

His family was divided by the Revolution — while he chose the patriot cause, his stepmother remained loyalist and his brother Staats Long served in the British army. Lost a bid to be convention chair to Henry Oothoudt.

Federalist
Lewis Morris
Westchester County
Born:April 8, 1726

Third lord of Morrisania Manor. Signer of the Declaration of Independence — famously replied to warnings against signing, "Damn the consequences. Give me the pen!" Half-brother (26 years his senior) of Gouverneur Morris, who drafted the Constitution.

Yale graduate (1746). Served in the Continental Congress and as major general in the state militia. Devoted postwar efforts to restoring Morrisania, which the British ransacked.

Federalist
Philip Van Cortlandt
Westchester County
Born:September 1, 1749, New York City

Brigadier general (mustered out 1783) with a distinguished war record. Fought at Saratoga, Monmouth Courthouse, and Yorktown, where he commanded the rear guard of the Continental march. Son of Lieutenant Governor Pierre Van Cortlandt.

Educated as a surveyor and civil engineer. Assisted General Sullivan's campaign against Mohawk raids. Charter member of the Society of the Cincinnati, serving on the committee that created the bylaws for the New York chapter.

Federalist
Peter Lefferts
Kings County
Born:December 27, 1753

One of Brooklyn's most prominent citizens, of Dutch ancestry. Fought at the Battle of Brooklyn as a first lieutenant in the Kings County militia. The Lefferts family home was destroyed in the British advance; he rebuilt it by 1783.

Now owns 240 acres in Flatbush and serves as a judge on the New York Court of Sessions and Court of Common Pleas. Kings County is Federalist territory.

Federalist
Abraham Bancker
Richmond County
Born:1760

A Washington spy during the Revolution! Operating under the pseudonym "Amicus Republicae," he relayed intelligence from patriot sympathizers inside British lines to Washington's headquarters. Most active and at risk during the campaigns of 1780. Washington awarded him a ceremonial sword after the war.

Attended King's College (studies interrupted by the war, like Hamilton). Served as county clerk of Richmond County (1781–84), member of the board of regents, and assemblyman.

Antifederalists
Antifederalist
Governor George Clinton
Ulster County
Role:Party Leader & Convention President
Born:July 26, 1739

New York's only governor since 1777 and the leader of the Antifederalists. Commands a large majority of like-minded delegates at the convention. His goal: defeat the Constitution or secure amendments to limit national power.

Feared that the Constitution's unlimited taxing power would undermine New York's state impost — the foundation of his political popularity (low taxes, redistributed loyalist lands). A brigadier general in the Continental Army, he shared command at the Battle of the Clintons (1777) with his brother James.

Special role: If the Constitution is rated Federalist in character, Clinton is directed to threaten to send a circular letter to other governors demanding a second convention.

Antifederalist
Melancton Smith
Dutchess County
Role:Party Lieutenant
Born:May 7, 1744, Jamaica, Long Island

One of the country's foremost Antifederalist thinkers. Likely the author behind the pseudonyms "Brutus," "The Federal Farmer," and/or "A Plebeian" — though he guards his anonymity carefully. Assists Governor Clinton as convention lieutenant.

A Poughkeepsie merchant with no college training. Served in the first New York Provincial Congress and as Dutchess County sheriff throughout the war. Moved to New York City in 1785; co-founded the New York Manumission Society. Ran from Dutchess County because New York City was solidly Federalist.

Antifederalist
John Lansing Jr.
Albany City and County
Born:January 30, 1754, Albany

Current mayor of Albany and speaker of the state assembly. A loyal follower of Governor Clinton, who arranged his election to the Constitutional Convention along with Robert Yates. He and Yates left the Philadelphia convention early in July, appalled that it was writing a new government rather than amending the Articles of Confederation.

Received the highest number of votes in Albany County for the ratifying convention. A formidable Antifederalist voice.

Antifederalist
Robert Yates
Albany City and County
Born:January 27, 1738, Schenectady

Justice of the New York Supreme Court (future chief justice). Left the Constitutional Convention with Lansing in early July. Kept careful notes of the proceedings — nearly as thorough as Madison's. Some say he is the author of the "Brutus" essays; the question remains open.

Published Political Papers Addressed to the Advocates for a Congressional Review (1786), defending the Articles of Confederation. His uncle Abraham Yates is a major force in Antifederalist politics; his principal patron is Governor Clinton.

Antifederalist
General James Clinton
Ulster County
Born:August 9, 1736

Elder brother of Governor George Clinton. A brigadier general of the Continental Army. Fought in the French and Indian War, the assault on Quebec, the Saratoga campaign, the Battle of Newtown (1779), and Yorktown. His son DeWitt Clinton will later become governor of New York.

Shared command with Governor Clinton at the Battle of the Clintons (October 1777), where Forts Clinton and Montgomery fell but Burgoyne was not reinforced — contributing to the decisive American victory at Saratoga.

Antifederalist
Gilbert Livingston
Dutchess County
Born:December 17, 1742, Dutchess County

A lesser branch of the great Livingston family — a "twig," as he would say. Grew up in Poughkeepsie, studied at King's College (left due to a smallpox epidemic). Ran a small legal practice (partner: the future jurist James Kent), a general store, and a shipyard.

His Clinton connection came through his shipbuilding associate, who was the governor's brother-in-law. Served in the state assembly (1777–78) and as surrogate court judge (1778–85). Supports Clinton and Melancton Smith.

Antifederalist
John Haring
Orange County
Born:September 28, 1739, Tappan

One of the most experienced politicians at the convention, having served at the county, state, and national levels in all three branches of government. Represented New York at the First Continental Congress; co-drafted the Orangetown Resolutions; served seven consecutive terms as state senator.

A successful lawyer who advanced to county judge. Firm in his Antifederalist principles.

Antifederalist
Henry Wisner
Orange County
Born:1720, Orange County

Of Swiss ancestry — his grandfather fought under the Duke of Marlborough. A real estate investor and owner of a grist mill in Goshen. Served in the colonial assembly for eleven consecutive years. During the war, built three gunpowder mills that delivered 1,000 lbs. of powder per week to Washington's army.

Also financed cannon construction and helped study the feasibility of stretching chains across the Hudson. Served in the Continental Congress (1775–76); founded an academy in Goshen (1784); appointed to the first board of regents of the University of the State of New York.

Antifederalist
Thomas Tredwell
Suffolk County
Born:February 6, 1743, Smithtown, L.I.

Princeton graduate (1764). A man who likes to move — from Long Island to upstate Plattsburgh (where he began his law practice), back to Suffolk County, and eventually back to Plattsburgh in his later years ("treading well" until the very end, as his role sheet wryly notes).

Served six consecutive terms in the state assembly (1777–83) representing Suffolk County. Longtime judge on the probate court and now the surrogate court of Suffolk County. Confirmed Antifederalist.

Antifederalist
Zephaniah Platt
Dutchess County
Born:May 27, 1735, Huntington, L.I.

Lawyer and judge who co-founded the city of Plattsburgh, New York — which was named after him (not his first name, thankfully). Served in the Provincial Congress, the convention that ratified the state constitution, successive terms in the state senate, and one term in the Continental Congress.

Served continuously as a Dutchess County judge since 1781. Father of fourteen children by two wives. A confirmed Antifederalist attending as a Dutchess County delegate.

Antifederalist
John Williams
Washington County
Born:September 1752, Barnstable, England

An immigrant who arrived only in 1773, yet rose quickly to prominence. Studied medicine and surgery at St. Thomas Hospital, London; served as a surgeon's mate on an English man-of-war before settling in Salem, Washington County. Set up a medical practice and within two years was elected to the Provincial Congress.

Served as surgeon for the state militia and as colonel of the Charlotte County regiment. State senator in 1777–78; brigadier general of the militia by 1786. A man of local feelings and committed Antifederalist.

Moderate · Convention Chair
Henry Oothoudt
Albany City and County · Pronunciation: OAT-howt
Born:January 6, 1739

Your unpronounceable surname is a sure tip-off that you are of Dutch ancestry. You are Henry Oothoudt [OAT-howt], born January 6, 1739, to Volkert Oothoudt. Little is known of your private life except that you married Eleanor "Neeltje" Van Bergen Oothoudt and had one daughter, Catherina, by her. You live in Catskill, Albany County.

Your political career has been mainly at the local level, with service on various town and county committees. You have been elected to the state senate, representing the interests of Albany County. This past spring, the voters of Albany County sent you as their delegate to the New York State Ratifying Convention, where you ran on the Antifederalist ticket. Yet you are soft in your Antifederalist commitment and would not be entirely opposed to ratification with amendments. The Federalists ran Richard Morris for convention chair; your election over him was easy.

You are the convention chair, presiding over the convention as it meets in "committee of the whole" — which is how it operates throughout the game. Moderate · Federalist Liaison
Jonathan N. Havens
Suffolk County
Born:June 18, 1757, Shelter Island

You are Jonathan Nicoll Havens, born June 18, 1757, on Shelter Island, Suffolk County, New York. Shelter Island has an interesting colonial history — part of the original Plymouth Company grant from King James I (1620), it passed through several owners before Nathaniel Sylvester, a Barbados sugar merchant, became its first white settler. You are a descendant of two of Shelter Island's earliest settler families, William Nicoll and George Havens.

Your father, James Nicoll Havens, was the first town supervisor on the island. After graduating from Yale College in 1777, you served for four years as Shelter Island town clerk (1783–87). Beginning in 1786 you have been a member of the state assembly, reelected to the office this spring.

The voters of Suffolk County elected you to the New York State Ratifying Convention. You once declared your indifference about the convention, saying that you cared "not a fig" if you attended. But you feared the ridicule of being left off the list of Federalist candidates due to suspicion of your having Antifederalist sympathies. In any event, you were elected on the "anti" ticket. People are uncertain about your affiliation, and by all appearances you are a Moderate. But in truth you are more a Federalist plant inside the Moderate camp and will serve as the liaison to the Federalist faction.

Game Play

Consult the Moderate role sheet for your primary instructions.

You are the Moderate liaison who cooperates with the Federalist party, led by Robert Livingston and Alexander Hamilton. As liaison, you are more set in your views than the other Moderates, who are fully indeterminate and free to vote as they see fit. You, by contrast, will vote with the Federalists, including voting to ratify the Constitution, if rated Federalist by the instructor. Your job is twofold: (1) to recruit Moderates to the Federalist side and (2) to persuade your recruits to join in the debates that follow formal speechmaking with questions, comments, or declarations supportive of Federalist positions. Those speaking in debate double their votes on the issues to which they speak. You double your vote for the final vote if the Constitution is rated Federalist by the instructor.

It is paramount that the Moderates (excepting you) operate as fair-minded judges of the speeches and proposals offered by others, honest brokers without interests or objectives of their own. Thus your only tool of persuasion is persuasion itself — no deals, bribes, or threats. Take your arguments from An Address to the People of the State of New York by "A Citizen of New York," generally known to be John Jay.

Because you don't write and deliver speeches (not enough time), you instead take a short exam, before the start of the game, based on the Gordon Wood reading found in the game book. The exam winner(s) casts an extra vote throughout the game, including the final vote to ratify or reject the Constitution.

Moderate · Antifederalist Liaison
Jacobus Swartwout
Dutchess County
Born:November 5, 1734, Fishkill, NY

You are Jacobus Swartwout, born November 5, 1734, in Fishkill, Dutchess County, New York. Your Dutch Reformed ancestors have been in New York since the early colonial period. When the Revolution came, you threw yourself into the patriot cause. You led the Committee for Detecting and Defeating Conspiracies in Dutchess County (1776), rooting out Tories and loyalist sympathizers. Your regiment — informally known as the Swartwout Regiment — saw action at the battles of Brooklyn, Fort Washington, Fort Independence, and White Plains. You rose to the rank of brigadier general of the militia.

In postwar politics you have served in the state assembly. This past spring the voters of Dutchess County elected you a delegate to the state ratifying convention on the Antifederalist ticket. In truth, however, you are embedded among the Moderates as a liaison to the Antifederalist faction — your Antifederalist sympathies are real, but you will work within the Moderate camp to move the undecided delegates toward rejection of the Constitution.

Moderate
David Hedges
Suffolk County
Born:June 15, 1744, East Hampton, Long Island

You are David Hedges — "Deacon" Hedges to your neighbors — born June 15, 1744, in East Hampton, Long Island. You are a farmer and deacon of the local church, known for great physical endurance. When the regular minister fled during the Revolution, you conducted church services yourself. On July 2, 1775, twenty-one men enlisted in the Continental Army at your church door. You once rode 100 miles home from market in a single day, though you noted that the horse "was long disabled" afterward.

You have been supervisor of Southampton town for twenty years. This past spring, the voters of Suffolk County sent you as a delegate to the state ratifying convention. You ran on the Antifederalist ticket, but you are nevertheless a Moderate, open to the idea of ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union.

Game Play

Consult the Moderate role sheet for your primary instructions.

Moderates as a group are intended to be fair-minded judges of the speeches and proposals offered by others, honest brokers without interests or objectives of their own. In fact, the power lies with you to determine if the Constitution is ratified or rejected. The formal factions, Federalists and Antifederalists, are equal in number and will largely cancel each other out.

But you may divide into two parties, because among you are two delegates resolved to move the Moderates into the Federalist or Antifederalist camp — Jonathan N. Havens for the Federalists and Jacobus Swartwout for the Antifederalists. Listen to the liaisons and listen to the arguments. Then cast your votes, issue by issue, favoring what you believe to be the better arguments. You may go back and forth; strength on one issue might not carry over to another. Your best judgment is what the game is asking of you.

Also your participation in debate: you double your vote on any issue to which you make a substantive contribution. Needless to say, participation will improve your grade.

Because you don't write and deliver speeches (not enough time), you instead take a short exam before the start of the game, based on the Gordon Wood reading found in the game book. The exam winner(s) casts an extra vote throughout the game, including the final vote to ratify or reject the Constitution.

Moderate
Jesse Woodhull
Orange County
Born:February 10, 1735, Setauket, Long Island

You are Jesse Woodhull, born February 10, 1735, in Setauket, Long Island. Your family later moved to Orange County, where you became a farmer and community leader. When the Revolution began, you accepted a colonel's commission and commanded the Cornwall Regiment of the Fourth Brigade under General George Clinton. When Fort Montgomery fell to the British on October 6, 1777, the fort's purpose — preventing Burgoyne from being reinforced — was achieved, though you were among those caught up in the British assault.

After the war you served four consecutive terms in the state senate (1777–81) and twice on the governor's council of appointment. This past spring, the voters of Orange County elected you to the state ratifying convention; you received the most votes of any Orange County delegate. You ran on the Antifederalist ticket but are nevertheless a Moderate, open to ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union.

Moderate
John Cantine
Ulster County
Born:October 20, 1735, Marbletown, Ulster County

You are John Cantine, born October 20, 1735, in Marbletown, Ulster County, to Peter Cantine and Cornelia DuBois Cantine. You have been nearly continuously elected to represent Ulster County in the state assembly since 1777 — eight separate terms in all. Ulster County is Governor George Clinton's home territory and perhaps the most staunchly Antifederalist county in the state; you had to run on the Antifederalist ticket to have any chance of success. But you are less adamant in your opposition than your fellow delegates and are open to the idea of ratification with amendments.

Your brothers Matthew and Peter Cantine also enjoyed modest political careers, with service in the state convention and assembly. You go to Poughkeepsie as a Moderate, a fair-minded judge of the arguments you will hear, frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the newly formed union of states.

Moderate
Cornelius C. Schoonmaker
Ulster County
Born:1745, Shawangunk, Ulster County

You are Cornelius C. Schoonmaker, born in 1745 in Shawangunk, Ulster County. You are a farmer, surveyor, agitator, patriot, and politician. You served on the local committee of vigilance during the Revolution, ferreting out loyalists and enforcing nonimportation boycotts. You have been continuously reelected to the state assembly since 1777. The name Schoonmaker suggests an ancestor who "made schoons, or schooners" — flatbottom boats used on Dutch inland waterways — though you are a farmer, not a shipwright.

This past spring, the voters of Ulster County sent you as a delegate to the state ratifying convention on the Antifederalist ticket. You received the second-fewest votes of any Ulster delegate, barely edging out James Clinton, the governor's own brother. You are open to ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union. You go to Poughkeepsie as a Moderate.

Moderate
Dirck Swart
Albany City and County
Born:May 17, 1734, Dutchess County

You are Dirck Swart — and you will be the first to admit that it is "not the loveliest of names." You were born May 17, 1734, in Dutchess County, to Teunis Swart and Marytje Van Keuren. Your Dutch ancestry is unmistakable. You moved to Stillwater in Albany County, where you built a house to surprise your wife Jannetje Vanderzee — the house still stands, and you are more famous for it than for anything else you have done.

Your political career has been checkered. You signed up with the Albany Sons of Liberty in 1766; then, perhaps for practical reasons, you accepted a loyalist justice-of-the-peace post in 1770; then you switched back to the patriot cause in 1775. You served in the Provincial Congress. During the war you provided your home as headquarters for General Philip Schuyler. You have largely stayed out of elective politics until this year's convention election, when the voters of Albany County sent you to the state ratifying convention on the Antifederalist ticket. You are nevertheless a Moderate, open to ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union.

Peter Vrooman
Albany City and County
Born:March 20, 1736

You are Colonel Peter Vrooman (sometimes "Vroman," and sometimes with a "Van"), born March 20, 1736. Two tales are told of you and your family.

Early in the war, probably 1775, you were a member of the committee of safety, and a meeting of the committee was scheduled at your residence. Tory loyalists had offered a reward for your capture, and a small party of abductors had secreted themselves around the house, expecting to seize you after the others had left. But you luckily left with them, and the plot was foiled.

More tragically was the massacre of your extended family in the summer of 1780. The Vrooman clan settled in and around Schoharie, in the Mohawk River Valley. On August 10 a band of seventy-three Indians and five Tories descended on the valley's scattered farms. You chanced to be at Middle Fort that day. Had you been where you were then residing, at the home of a relative, you would not have survived — those outside the forts were tomahawked and scalped; those spared were captured and taken to Niagara and then Montreal. A year later the survivors returned on foot. Because you were inside the safety of a fort when the attack occurred, your reputation suffered with the locals, but not with the soldiers of the Fifteenth Albany Regiment, who lauded your courage and decisiveness.

You have repeatedly been elected to the state assembly and state senate. Albany is Antifederalist territory and you ran on the Antifederalist ticket. But you are nevertheless a Moderate, open to ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union.

Nicholas Low
New York City and County
Born:March 30, 1739, Raritan Landing, New Jersey

You are a merchant mainly, not a soldier or politician. You are Nicholas Low, born March 30, 1739, to Cornelius Low Jr. and Johanna Gouverneur Low in Raritan Landing, New Jersey. Your two sisters married two brothers, Hugh and Alexander Wallace, and with them you established Low & Wallace in New York City, an import–export business dealing in dry goods, salt, gunpowder, wine, and farm produce (1774). Your older brother Isaac was more successful in prerevolutionary politics, but he declined to stand for election to the Second Continental Congress because independence was the issue and his interests and loyalties tied him to England. He became a loyalist and stayed in the city during the British occupation. When the British departed, he went with them, as did your brothers-in-law, the Wallaces.

After the war, as the only Low remaining, you came into your own — with sufficient wealth to buy stock in the Bank of New York and sufficient prestige to serve on its board of directors (starting in 1784). You entered the political arena only this year. The voters of New York County elected you simultaneously to the state assembly and to the state ratifying convention. New York is Federalist territory, and you ran on the Federalist ticket. But you received the fewest votes of any Federalist candidate, perhaps because you are less fervent than the other eight. Indeed, you are a Moderate, respectful of Antifederalists who want to condition ratification on amendments to the Constitution.

Samuel Jones
Queens County
Born:July 26, 1734, Oyster Bay, Long Island

You are Samuel Jones, born July 26, 1734, in Oyster Bay, Long Island. You read law under noted attorney William Smith and were admitted to the bar in 1760. In the mid-1780s you and Alexander Hamilton represented New York in its border dispute with Massachusetts. And in 1786, you and Richard Varick produced a compendium of all New York state laws then in existence — a major contribution to legal clarity in the young state. Also in that year, you were first elected to the state assembly representing Queens County.

This past spring, the voters of Queens favored you with election to the New York State Ratifying Convention meeting in Poughkeepsie. You received 517 votes, the second-most of the four-person delegation. Queens is Antifederalist territory, and you ran on the Antifederalist ticket. But you are nevertheless a Moderate, open to the idea of ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union.

Peter Van Ness
Columbia County
Born:November 30, 1734, Kinderhook

You are Judge Peter Van Ness of Kinderhook, Columbia County. Born November 30, 1734, you are the son of William Gronesbeck Van Ness and Gertrude Hogeboom. Your sister Jane is the wife of Robert Yates, prominent jurist from Schenectady, former delegate to the Constitutional Convention, and faithful follower of Governor George Clinton. That said, it was Yates who married up.

As a colonel in command of the Ninth Regiment of the Albany County Militia, you saw action at Saratoga in October 1777 and were present for the surrender of British General Burgoyne. You were the first judge of Columbia County and were a member of the state senate. This spring you were elected to the New York State Ratifying Convention. Columbia is Antifederalist territory, and you ran on the Antifederalist ticket, garnering the fewest votes of any of the successful candidates. The reason may be that you are more nationally minded than the standard Antifederalist. Indeed, you are a Moderate, open to the idea of ratification and frightened at the prospect of New York's remaining outside the union.

Thaddeus Crane
Westchester County
Born:March 27, 1728, Norwalk, Connecticut

You are Thaddeus Crane, born March 27, 1728, to Joseph Crane and Mary Crouch Crane in Norwalk, Fairfield County, Connecticut. You moved to North Salem, Westchester County, New York, where you married first Sarah Paddock Crane and then Lydia Bell Reed Crane, and had a whopping eighteen children by them.

Your main exploits, besides siring countless offspring, came as a militia officer in the Revolutionary War, rising from captain to major to colonel. As a company commander, you fought at the Battle of Ridgefield in Connecticut (April 1777). You were part of a force hastily assembled to repel a British amphibious assault on the supply depot at Danbury. The depot could not be saved, but British troops returning to the coast were properly harassed, with the main fighting taking place at the village of Ridgefield. General Benedict Arnold commanded American forces; former royal governor William Tryon commanded the British. You were seriously wounded during the engagement, shot through the hip. You remained in the army and, after your convalescence, were promoted to lieutenant colonel of the Fourth Regiment of the Westchester County militia.

Your political career has consisted of service in the state assembly. This past spring, your neighbors honored you with election to the New York State Ratifying Convention. Westchester County is Federalist territory, and you ran on the Federalist ticket. But you garnered the second-fewest votes among the Federalist slate of candidates, and you now describe yourself as moderate in your views.
