Faction Overview & Seating

Assign roles before class. Distribute proportionally based on enrollment. Below is the recommended layout and each faction's core ideology.

🔴 KPD — Communist Party
  • Seated: Far left
  • Goal: Overthrow Weimar, establish Soviet-style republic
  • Strategy: Expose SPD as "social fascists"; build worker solidarity
  • Win by: Revolutionary speeches, worker mobilization points, defeating budget
🌹 SPD — Social Democrats
  • Seated: Center-left
  • Goal: Defend Weimar democracy; pass social reform legislation
  • Strategy: Build coalitions with Center; keep KPD and NSDAP out of power
  • Win by: Successful coalition votes, reform bills passed, government survival
✝ Zentrum — Catholic Center Party
  • Seated: Center
  • Goal: Protect Catholic interests; keep government stable
  • Strategy: Swing bloc — may support SPD or DNVP depending on issue
  • Win by: Key bills that protect church/education, coalition participation
⚖ DDP — German Democratic Party
  • Seated: Center
  • Goal: Liberal democracy, rule of law, Weimar Constitution
  • Strategy: Bridge left-center coalitions; defend constitutional norms
  • Win by: Passing liberal legislation, preventing authoritarian outcomes
🏛 DVP — German People's Party
  • Seated: Center-right
  • Goal: Business interests, pragmatic conservatism, Stresemann's legacy
  • Strategy: Flexible; opposes KPD; may negotiate with SPD or DNVP
  • Win by: Economic policy wins, coalition participation
🦅 DNVP — German Nationalists
  • Seated: Center-right to far right
  • Goal: Restore conservative monarchy or authoritarian order; reverse Versailles
  • Strategy: Reject Weimar; may collaborate with NSDAP against Republic
  • Win by: Defeating government, passing nationalist legislation
⚡ NSDAP — National Socialist German Workers' Party
  • Seated: Far right — these students play the most volatile role; remind them it is a historical simulation
  • Goal: Destroy Weimar democracy from within; seize control of the Reichstag
  • Strategy: Populist speeches, emotional appeals, exploit crises (unemployment, humiliation)
  • Win by: Reichstag plurality, failed government formations, emergency decrees, Chancellor appointment

Three-Session Overview

Session 1 — Day 1
Establishing the Republic · The Stab-in-the-Back & Versailles Crisis
Introduce the simulation. Characters take their seats. The Reichstag convenes. Debate the Versailles Treaty and the legitimacy of the Weimar Constitution. Mandatory agenda: opening speeches, first legislative vote (ratification of Weimar or call for revision).
Session 2 — Day 2
Crisis & Coalition · Hyperinflation, Unemployment, the Depression
The Republic faces its greatest economic tests. Budget and emergency powers debates. Coalition formation attempts. The mandatory agenda item: vote on emergency economic legislation (Article 48 trigger). Points awarded for successful coalition-building or for bringing down a government.
Session 3 — Day 3
The Final Crisis · Structured Debate & Historical Reckoning
Hitler's appointment as Chancellor looms. Structured debate format: each faction makes a final argument for their vision of Germany. Vote on enabling act or final government formation. Historical outcome revealed.

Key Instructor Principles

Stay in the Game Master role. You are the Reichstag President (chair). You do not advocate for any faction. You enforce parliamentary procedure, award points, rule on challenges, and control the clock. If students break character badly or a debate becomes unproductive, call "out of session" and reset.
Tip — Let It Get Messy. The whole point is for students to experience the difficulty of coalition-building and the fragility of democratic norms under pressure. Resist the impulse to smooth things over too quickly. Chaos is pedagogically useful here.
NSDAP Role Sensitivity. Before Session 1, speak privately or briefly to students playing NSDAP roles. Clarify that they are playing a historical character, not expressing personal views. Remind all students that the simulation is an academic exercise in understanding how democracy failed — not an endorsement of any ideology.
Session 1 — Establishing the Republic
Day 1 · 50 minutes · The Reichstag convenes for the first time. Introduce the game, seat delegates, run opening speeches, and reach the first mandatory vote.

Minute-by-Minute Plan

0:00 – 5:00
Opening — Enter the Republic
You assume the role of Reichstag President. Set the historical scene: November 1919. Germany has just signed the Versailles Treaty. The Kaiser is gone. What kind of Germany will this be?
5:00 – 20:00
Opening Speeches (Mandatory Agenda Item 1)
Each faction delivers a 1–2 minute opening statement. Start with SPD (defenders of the Republic), then KPD, Zentrum, DDP, DVP, DNVP, NSDAP. Award participation points as noted below.
20:00 – 35:00
Open Debate — Versailles & Weimar's Legitimacy
Free debate. Factions argue their position. You moderate: call on speakers, enforce 90-second limits, award bonus points for strong historical arguments and primary source citations.
35:00 – 45:00
Mandatory Vote — Article 48 Standing Rule
Vote: Should the new Weimar government retain Article 48 (presidential emergency powers) as written, or demand revision? Each faction votes; you record and announce result. Award outcome points.
45:00 – 50:00
Session Close & Prep for Session 2
Record cumulative points. Announce Session 2 topic. Remind students: read their faction's core texts before next class.

Opening Script

Students enter and find faction seating. Wait for class to settle. Stand at front.
"The Reichstag is now in session."

"It is November 1919. The Great War is over. But the peace is bitter. The Versailles Treaty has stripped Germany of territory, imposed crushing reparations, and placed sole blame for the war on Germany's shoulders. The Kaiser has abdicated. A new republic has been declared — the Weimar Republic.

Is this republic legitimate? Can it survive? That is what we are here to decide — not as historians looking backward, but as the political actors who were there. You are members of the Reichstag. The fate of German democracy is literally in your hands.

I am the President of the Reichstag. I will recognize speakers, call votes, and maintain order. When I say the Reichstag is in session, we are in session. When I say we are out of session, we step back and I speak to you as your instructor.

We will begin with opening statements from each party. SPD — you have the floor."

Mandatory Vote — Article 48

"We come now to our first vote. The question before the Reichstag: Article 48 of the Weimar Constitution grants the President emergency powers to rule by decree if the government cannot function. Should we retain Article 48 as written — or demand it be revised or eliminated?

YES — Retain Article 48 as written.
NO — Revise or eliminate Article 48.

Each faction will declare its vote. Record on the board."

Historical Note to Share After Vote: Article 48 was used over 250 times between 1919–1933. Hindenburg used it to govern by decree after 1930, effectively bypassing the Reichstag — the first step toward Hitler's appointment as Chancellor.
Session 2 — Crisis & Coalition
Day 2 · 50 minutes · The Weimar Republic faces economic catastrophe. Coalition politics, emergency legislation, and the limits of democracy under pressure.

Minute-by-Minute Plan

0:00 – 5:00
Session Open — Crisis Announcement
You announce the economic crisis (hyperinflation 1923 or Depression 1930, your choice). Set the stakes. Review cumulative points from Session 1.
5:00 – 20:00
Coalition Negotiations (Mandatory Agenda Item 2)
Factions have 10 minutes of "lobby time" — they can move around the room, make deals, and negotiate coalition agreements. You observe and take notes. Then reconvene for coalition announcements.
20:00 – 35:00
Legislative Debate — Emergency Economic Bill
Factions debate the proposed emergency economic legislation. SPD will likely introduce it; KPD and NSDAP will likely oppose. Vote determines government survival.
35:00 – 45:00
Government Formation / No-Confidence Vote
Can a majority coalition form a government? If not, the government falls. NSDAP and KPD may call for new elections. You rule on parliamentary procedure and award outcome points.
45:00 – 50:00
Session Close & Preview Session 3
Record cumulative points on board. Announce: "The final crisis is coming." Preview Session 3's structured debate format.

Crisis Announcement Script

Display or announce the crisis scenario you are using this session.
"The Reichstag is now in session. I must inform the delegates of a grave development."
Scenario A — Hyperinflation (1923)
"The German mark has collapsed. This morning, a loaf of bread costs four billion marks. Workers are paid twice a day so they can spend their wages before the money becomes worthless. Foreign debt from Versailles reparations is due. The government must act — or fall."
Scenario B — The Great Depression (1930)
"Six million Germans are unemployed. Banks have collapsed. The American loans that fueled Germany's recovery are gone. The Reichstag cannot form a stable coalition. The President has already begun ruling by decree under Article 48. How long before this Republic collapses entirely?"

Coalition Lobby Time (10 min)

Students stand and move freely. You circulate, observe negotiations, take notes for participation grades.
"You have ten minutes. The Reichstag is briefly recessed. Parties may negotiate. When we reconvene, each faction must announce whether they have joined a coalition and with whom."
Watch For: SPD + Zentrum + DDP coalition (the "Weimar Coalition") is historically the Republic's survival path. DNVP + NSDAP cooperation is the path to authoritarian takeover. DVP is often the swing faction.
Common Problem: Students may refuse to negotiate. Prompt: "What concessions would you need to join a coalition?" or "What does your faction gain by letting the government fall?"

Debate Issue: Emergency Economic Legislation

2
Should the Reichstag pass emergency economic legislation?
Coalition vote · Government survival · Article 48 trigger
▾
Write on board: "Can democratic institutions solve a crisis they did not create?"
🌹 SPD — Pass the Bill
  • Emergency action now or the Republic collapses and something worse takes over
  • Workers need relief: unemployment benefits, wage floors, public works
  • This is why we built the Republic — to serve the people in moments of crisis
🔴 KPD — Defeat the Bill
  • This bill protects banks and bosses, not workers
  • The more desperate the crisis, the closer the revolution
  • Vote NO; let the capitalist government fall
⚡ NSDAP — Defeat the Bill / Demand New Elections
  • This Republic cannot be reformed — it must be replaced
  • Every failed coalition is a gift to us: the people see that democracy doesn't work
  • Demand elections — our numbers grow with every crisis
✝ Zentrum — Negotiate Terms
  • Will support the bill if protections for religious schools are included
  • Stability is a Catholic value; we do not want chaos
  • The swing vote — what concessions can SPD offer?

Moderator Prompts During Debate

  • "KPD — if this bill fails and the government falls, who benefits? Is that your goal?"
  • "NSDAP — what is your alternative? What would you do with emergency powers?"
  • "SPD — why should Zentrum trust you? What are you offering?"
  • "DNVP — is there any version of this bill you would support?"

Session 3 — The Final Crisis
Day 3 · 50 minutes · Structured debate. Every faction makes its final case. The Republic's fate is decided. Historical truth is revealed.
Format Change — Structured Debate. Session 3 uses a structured debate format rather than free parliamentary procedure. Each faction gets an equal speaking slot. This ensures every student speaks and creates a clear dramatic arc toward the final vote.

Minute-by-Minute Plan

0:00 – 5:00
Session Open — January 1933
Announce the historical context. Hindenburg is President. Hitler has been appointed Chancellor this morning. What happens next is in the Reichstag's hands.
5:00 – 30:00
Structured Final Speeches (Mandatory Agenda Item 3)
Each faction delivers a 3–4 minute closing argument. Order: SPD → KPD → DDP → Zentrum → DVP → DNVP → NSDAP (last). You time each speech strictly. Award points per rubric.
30:00 – 40:00
Final Vote — Enabling Act
Vote on granting Hitler's government emergency powers (the historical Enabling Act). SPD opposes; everyone else historically voted yes. Watch what happens in your simulation.
40:00 – 50:00
Out of Session — Historical Reveal & Debrief Setup
Step out of Reichstag President role. Share what actually happened. Announce cumulative point winners. Frame the research paper connection. Optional: show Niemöller quote.

Opening Script — January 30, 1933

Step to center front. Pause for effect. The mood should feel heavy.
"The Reichstag is in session for the last time."

"It is January 30, 1933. This morning, President Paul von Hindenburg appointed Adolf Hitler as Chancellor of Germany. Some of you in this chamber helped make this possible. Some of you fought against it with everything you had. All of you are now living with the consequences.

Before us today is one final question: the Enabling Act. If passed, it would grant Chancellor Hitler's government the power to rule by decree for four years — bypassing this Reichstag entirely. It requires a two-thirds majority.

Before we vote, each party will make its final statement. You have three minutes. Make your case — to history."

⚡ NSDAP — Final Argument

  • The German people have chosen — they chose us in election after election

  • Faction goal: Persuade Zentrum and DVP to vote YES
🔴 KPD — Final Argument

  • Fascism is the last stage of capitalism — this was inevitable
  • We will not dignify this vote by participating (KPD deputies were already arrested by this date)
Historical Note: By March 23, 1933, most KPD deputies had already been arrested. You may have the student deliver the speech but note this reality.
✝ Zentrum — Final Argument

  • Stability for Germany required us to make difficult choices
  • We believe we can manage and moderate this government

Pedagogical Core: The structured debate is where the simulation pays off. Students must articulate, in character, why parties that "should have known better" — Zentrum, DVP, DNVP — voted to end democracy. This is the heart of the research paper question.

Historical Reveal Script

Step out of Reichstag President role. Shift to speaking as instructor.
"We're now out of session. You've experienced — in compressed form — fourteen years of Weimar politics."

"In real life: The Enabling Act passed on March 23, 1933. Only the SPD voted no. 94 deputies in a chamber of 535. Within months, all other political parties were banned. Trade unions were dissolved. The press was censored. Within a year, Germany was a one-party state.

The question your research paper asks is: How did this happen? Not 'why were the Nazis evil' — that's easy. The harder question is: Why did democratic parties fail to stop them? Why did coalitions collapse? Why did parties that believed in democracy vote to end it?

That is what you spent three sessions experiencing. Now you write about it."
"First they came for the Communists, and I did not speak out — because I was not a Communist. Then they came for the Social Democrats... Then they came for the Jews... Then they came for me — and there was no one left to speak for me." — Martin Niemöller (post-war reflection)

Game Outcome Scenarios

Note: Unlike some Reacting to the Past games, the abridged Weimar simulation is not designed to produce a single "winner." The goal is for students to experience the process — coalition collapse, democratic erosion, competing ideologies — regardless of the final vote result. The debrief matters more than the outcome.
Outcome: Republic Survives

Democratic coalition held. Enabling Act defeated. Historical counterfactual — discuss: what would Germany have needed long-term?

Outcome: Republic Falls

Enabling Act passes. Mirrors history. Key debrief question: Which parties enabled this? Why?

Outcome: Left-Wing Collapse

KPD and SPD failed to unite. Historical reality. Discuss: How did left-wing division contribute to Hitler's rise?

Outcome: Governing Deadlock

No majority could form. Also historically common. Discuss: How does parliamentary deadlock feed authoritarianism?

Discussion Questions

  1. How did your faction's experience match or differ from the historical record?
    Look for: coalition failures, ideological rigidity, short-term thinking
  2. Which faction do you think bears the most responsibility for democracy's collapse?
    Push back on easy answers — the NSDAP is obvious; what about Zentrum and DNVP?
  3. What would it have taken to save the Republic?
    Requires counterfactual thinking — useful for research papers
  4. Why did democratic parties vote for the Enabling Act?
    The hardest question — fear, false confidence, miscalculation, self-interest
  5. What parallels, if any, do you see in contemporary democratic systems?
    Handle carefully — the goal is analytical comparison, not partisan commentary

Bridge to Research Paper

"Your research paper asks you to analyze the Weimar Republic as your primary case study — then compare it to one other authoritarian government. You've now lived in the simulation. You felt what it was like to watch coalitions collapse, to have your emergency powers used against you, to be the last faction standing.

The paper asks: How did authoritarianism rise? What democratic mechanisms failed? Your simulation experience gives you an inside view that your sources will now help you analyze rigorously.

The Weimar section is 3 to 4 pages. Your comparative government section is independently researched, 2 to 3 pages. Use your check-in schedule — do not leave this until the week it's due."
Common Paper Mistake 1: Treating the NSDAP as the only cause. The paper should analyze the system — coalitions, Article 48, economic crises, the Versailles legacy — not just Hitler.
Common Paper Mistake 2: Choosing a comparative government that has nothing in common with Weimar. The comparison should illuminate something — not just describe two separate cases.
Remind students: The comparative government section must be independently researched — the Weimar simulation materials do not cover it. Students choose their government; the paper requires them to find their own primary and secondary sources for that section.
