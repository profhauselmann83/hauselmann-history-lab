Overview

This page provides detailed background on each major debate topic you'll encounter in the simulation. Use this to prepare your speeches, understand the historical context, and develop your arguments.

How to Use This Page

  • Before Each Session: Read the relevant debate topics for that session's mandatory items
  • For Your Papers: Use these summaries as starting points, then consult the Core Texts and Supplemental Documents
  • For Your Speeches: Identify the key tensions and your party's position
  • For Negotiations: Understand what other parties want and where compromise might be possible

Remember:

Every debate is linked to the Stability Index. Your choices will either strengthen or weaken democracy. There are no "right answers"—only historical consequences.

Foreign Relations (Foreign Ministry)

Foreign policy debates revolve around Germany's relationship to the Versailles Treaty system. All German parties—from Communists to Nazis—agree that Versailles is unjust. But they disagree violently on how to respond.

Two competing strategies:

Erfüllungspolitik (Policy of Fulfillment)

Advocates: SPD, Centre, DDP, DVP (sometimes)

Strategy: Comply with Versailles while negotiating gradual revisions

Logic: Germany has no military power. Defiance will bring French/British intervention. Cooperation earns goodwill and leads to treaty erosion over time.

Evidence: Locarno Treaties (1925), League admission (1926), Dawes Plan (1924), Young Plan (1929)

Katastrophenpolitik (Policy of Catastrophe)

Advocates: DNVP, NSDAP, some Reichswehr officers

Strategy: Refuse all cooperation; provoke confrontation; reject Versailles entirely

Logic: Compliance legitimizes the treaty. Only defiance will force renegotiation. Germany must accept short-term pain to win long-term freedom.

Evidence: 1923 hyperinflation disaster (attempted Katastrophenpolitik backfired)

For full historical context, see Historical Background: Foreign Relations

The Freedom Law

Mandatory Item: Session 1

Presented by: Alfred Hugenberg (DNVP)

Portfolio: Foreign Ministry

What Is the Freedom Law?

The Law against the Enslavement of the German People (nicknamed the Freedom Law) is a proposed bill sponsored by the DNVP and NSDAP that would:

  1. Renounce the Treaty of Versailles entirely
  2. Criminalize cooperation with Versailles - Any government official who negotiates reparations, acknowledges war guilt, or recognizes Versailles borders would be guilty of treason
  3. Reject all reparations payments immediately

Historical Context

In 1928, the DNVP suffered major electoral losses. Desperate to regain support, party leader Alfred Hugenberg allied with the marginal NSDAP and ultranationalist groups to sponsor the Freedom Law. The goal was to find a wedge issue that would split the ruling coalition and energize the nationalist base.

The Young Plan (see below) was the perfect target. Hugenberg argued that any German who supported the Young Plan was a traitor validating the "enslavement" of Germany.

The Stakes

If the Freedom Law Passes:

  • The government cannot negotiate with the Allies without committing a crime
  • France and Belgium have threatened military intervention if Germany renounces Versailles
  • The Young Plan (which reduces reparations) would be dead
  • Germany would face international isolation
  • The Republic would be in open defiance of the world order

If the Freedom Law Fails:

  • The DNVP and NSDAP claim the Reichstag is "enslaved to foreign powers"
  • Nationalists paint moderates as "November criminals"
  • The government can proceed with Erfüllungspolitik
  • Germany maintains international goodwill

Questions to Consider

  • Does German honor require rejecting Versailles, even at the cost of invasion?
  • Is the Freedom Law sincere patriotism or cynical political opportunism?
  • Can Germany risk war in its current weakened state?
  • Does cooperation with Versailles legitimize an unjust treaty?
  • What would France do if Germany passed this law?

Impact on Stability Index

If passed: MASSIVE decrease (international crisis, possible intervention)

If defeated narrowly: Moderate decrease (nationalists energized)

If defeated overwhelmingly: Small increase (Republic shows strength)

The Young Plan

Mandatory Item: Session 2

Presented by: Chancellor Hermann Müller (SPD)

Portfolio: Foreign Ministry

What Is the Young Plan?

The Young Plan (1929) is a renegotiated reparations agreement that would:

  1. Reduce total reparations from 132 billion gold marks to 112 billion
  2. Lower annual payments and extend the payment schedule to 59 years (until 1988)
  3. Remove Allied supervision of German finances
  4. Evacuate the Rhineland by 1930 (five years ahead of schedule)
  5. Restore German financial sovereignty

It is named after American banker Owen D. Young, who chaired the committee.

Historical Context

The Young Plan is the latest success of Foreign Minister Stresemann's Erfüllungspolitik:

  • 1924: Dawes Plan reduced initial reparations burden
  • 1925: Locarno Treaties normalized relations with France
  • 1926: Germany admitted to League of Nations
  • 1929: Young Plan further reduces payments and ends occupation

Stresemann died in October 1929, just as the plan awaited Reichstag ratification. Almost all observers—including the Allies and the German cabinet—see the Young Plan as a major diplomatic victory.

But Stresemann's death leaves the plan without its champion at a critical moment.

The Opposition

The DNVP and NSDAP violently oppose the Young Plan because:

  • It accepts the principle of reparations (validates war guilt)
  • It extends payments to 1988 - "enslaving future generations"
  • It is linked to the Liquidation Treaty with Poland (see below)
  • Signing it would violate the Freedom Law (if that passes)

The KPD also opposes it as a "capitalist scheme" that benefits international bankers.

The Stakes

If the Young Plan Passes:

  • Reparations reduced significantly
  • Rhineland evacuated by 1930
  • Germany regains financial sovereignty
  • International goodwill strengthened
  • BUT: Nationalists claim government "legitimized enslavement"
  • BUT: Payments continue until 1988

If the Young Plan Fails:

  • Rhineland occupation continues indefinitely
  • Allied financial supervision remains
  • Higher reparations under Dawes Plan continue
  • International crisis - France may increase pressure
  • Possible return to 1923-style confrontation

Questions to Consider

  • Does accepting reduced payments legitimize an unjust treaty?
  • Is 59 years of payments "enslavement" or pragmatic compromise?
  • Can Germany afford to reject international goodwill?
  • What happens if Germany defaults again?
  • Is early Rhineland evacuation worth continued reparations?
  • Can the government survive if the Young Plan fails?

Impact on Stability Index

If passed: Small to moderate increase (diplomatic success, but nationalists angry)

If defeated: Large decrease (international crisis, government likely collapses)

The Liquidation Treaty with Poland

Status: Discretionary Item (can be added to agenda by Speaker)

Portfolio: Foreign Ministry

What Is the Liquidation Treaty?

The Liquidation Treaty is a proposed agreement with Poland to resolve the ongoing crisis over German property in Polish-annexed territories. It would:

  1. End the trade war between Germany and Poland (ongoing since 1924)
  2. Limit Polish "liquidation" of German-owned property
  3. Provide compensation for Germans whose land was confiscated
  4. Regulate treatment of German minorities in Poland
  5. NOT alter borders - the Polish Corridor and other annexations remain

The treaty is financially linked to certain provisions in the Young Plan.

Historical Context: The Polish Question

The creation of Poland by Versailles is the single most hated provision of the treaty for most Germans. See Historical Background: Poland and the Eastern Border for full context.

Key facts:

  • The Polish Corridor separated East Prussia from Germany, creating an exclave
  • Poland annexed West Prussia, Posen, and parts of Upper Silesia
  • Germany lost 13% of its territory and 10% of its population
  • 800,000 ethnic Germans fled or were expelled from Polish territories by 1923
  • Poland began "liquidating" German property - forcing sales at below-market prices
  • Germans traveling through the Corridor faced harassment; in 1925 a train derailment killed 25 Germans (suspected sabotage)

No German party accepts these borders. Not one.

The Trade War (1924-1926)

Unable to challenge Poland militarily, Germany launched an economic war:

Poland must be mortally wounded after the trade war. With her blood her strength will flow away as well, and finally her independence.

— Frankfurter Zeitung (liberal newspaper), 1924

The trade war devastated Poland's economy (Poland depended heavily on German trade) and helped trigger a coup that brought General Piłsudski to power in 1926. Poland retaliated by accelerating liquidation of German property.

The Dilemma

By 1929, the trade war has failed. Poland won't collapse. German refugees keep arriving. The situation is unsustainable.

The Liquidation Treaty would:

Benefits

  • End economic warfare
  • Protect remaining Germans in Poland
  • Provide compensation for confiscated property
  • Reduce tensions (lowers risk of war)
  • Linked to Young Plan benefits

Costs

  • Implicitly recognizes Polish borders
  • Appears to accept permanent loss of German territory
  • Abandons Germans still in Poland
  • Violates pan-German principle (all Germans in one state)
  • Nationalist fury: "Betrayal of the East!"

The Stakes

If the Treaty Passes:

  • Crisis with Poland de-escalates
  • Trade resumes, benefiting both economies
  • German minorities somewhat protected
  • BUT: Nationalists call it "acceptance of Polish theft"
  • BUT: Borders remain unchanged - Polish Corridor still exists
  • Linked to Young Plan - rejecting one may doom the other

If the Treaty Fails:

  • Trade war continues (hurts both sides)
  • Liquidation of German property accelerates
  • More German refugees
  • Rising tensions - risk of war
  • Polish corridor remains major flashpoint
  • May jeopardize Young Plan

Questions to Consider

  • Can Germany negotiate with Poland without legitimizing territorial theft?
  • Does protecting Germans still in Poland justify recognizing Polish borders?
  • Is the Polish Corridor negotiable, or must it be recovered by force someday?
  • What is Germany's moral obligation to ethnic Germans in Poland?
  • Can the Republic afford another confrontation in the East?
  • Would rejecting this treaty make future border revision easier or harder?

Impact on Stability Index

If passed: Moderate decrease (nationalists furious about "accepting Polish theft")

If defeated: Moderate decrease (crisis with Poland intensifies)

Either way, this issue destabilizes the Republic.

The Naval Bills (Armored Cruisers)

Naval Bill #1: Mandatory Item, Session 1

Naval Bills #2 and #3: Discretionary Items (must be fought for)

Presented by: Defense Minister Kurt von Schleicher

Portfolio: Defense Ministry

What Are the Naval Bills?

The Reichswehr proposes building three Deutschland-class armored cruisers (derisively called "pocket battleships" by the British press). Each bill funds one ship.

The first bill must pass in Session 1. If it fails, the program dies.

Bills #2 and #3 are annual expenditures that must be approved in later sessions to continue funding. Missing even one means the program stops.

Technical Details

  • Cost: Extremely expensive - drains budget during economic crisis
  • Purpose: Coastal defense (officially); power projection (actually)
  • Versailles compliance: Technically legal under treaty limits, but Allies object
  • Design: Heavily armed but faster than battleships - ideal for commerce raiding

Historical Context

The Reichswehr delayed proposing these ships until after the 1928 election, hoping for a conservative majority. Instead, the SPD won. The issue became a major campaign flashpoint:

SPD campaign slogan: "Food for children, not armored cruisers."

After the SPD victory, the party faced a dilemma: Liberal coalition partners (DVP, DDP) threatened to bring down the government if the ships weren't funded. A majority in the Reichstag supported construction.

The SPD reluctantly agreed to the first ship. The KPD called a referendum to block it - the referendum failed.

The Dilemma

Arguments FOR the Naval Bills

  • National defense: Germany needs a navy to protect coastline and trade
  • International prestige: A modern navy signals Germany's return to Great Power status
  • Jobs: Shipbuilding employs thousands of workers
  • Versailles compliance: The ships are technically legal
  • Deterrence: Shows Germany won't be pushed around
  • Coalition stability: Liberals will collapse government if ships aren't funded

Arguments AGAINST the Naval Bills

  • "Guns or butter": Cannot afford warships during mass unemployment
  • Budget crisis: Every mark for ships is a mark not spent on unemployment benefits
  • Allied anger: Britain and France see this as rearmament, risking intervention
  • Militarism: Feeding the Reichswehr's autonomy and power
  • Wrong priorities: People are starving - this is obscene
  • Hypocrisy: How can you cut social spending while funding battleships?

The Stakes

If Naval Bill #1 Passes:

  • First ship funded
  • Coalition holds together (for now)
  • SPD alienates core working-class supporters
  • Bills #2 and #3 must be fought for later
  • Budget further strained

If Naval Bill #1 Fails:

  • Entire program dies
  • Liberal parties (DVP, DDP) may collapse the coalition
  • Reichswehr furious - relationship with government worsens
  • Conservatives claim Republic is "weak"
  • More money available for social spending

Questions to Consider

  • Can a modern state exist without a modern military?
  • Is national defense more important than social welfare?
  • Does funding warships during economic crisis show wrong priorities?
  • Will the Allies intervene if Germany rearms?
  • Should the Reichswehr get what it wants to keep it loyal to the Republic?
  • Is the coalition worth saving if it requires betraying workers?

Impact on Stability Index

If Bill #1 passes: Small decrease (SPD base angry, but coalition stable)

If Bill #1 fails: Moderate decrease (coalition crisis, Reichswehr alienated)

Banning Paramilitaries

Status: Discretionary Item

Portfolio: Defense Ministry or Interior Ministry

What Is the Issue?

Should the Reichstag ban political paramilitaries? If so, which ones?

Options:

  1. Ban all paramilitaries (requires 2/3 majority - constitutional amendment)
  2. Ban specific paramilitaries (simple majority - uses Law for Protection of Republic)
  3. Ban none (maintain status quo)

The Paramilitaries

See Historical Background: Paramilitary Violence for full context.

Organization Party Size Status
SA (Storm Troopers) NSDAP ~60,000 Legal, openly violent
Red Front KPD ~130,000 BANNED May 1929
Reichsbanner SPD/Centre/DDP ~3,000,000 Legal, "respectable"
Stahlhelm DNVP ~500,000 Legal, "respectable"

Combined, these groups dwarf the 100,000-man Reichswehr.

The Red Front Ban (May 1929)

In May 1929, the SPD interior minister banned the KPD's Red Front paramilitary after violent clashes on May Day. The SPD-led police killed 30+ demonstrators.

The Red Front ban:

  • Confiscated all assets
  • Banned successor organizations
  • Left the SA legal

The KPD calls this hypocrisy: Why ban the Red Front but not the SA, which is equally violent?

The SPD's response: The Red Front explicitly opposed the Republic. The SA claims to support legality (even if that's a lie).

The Dilemma

Arguments FOR Banning Paramilitaries

  • Public safety: Street violence is escalating - people are dying
  • Democracy: Private armies undermine the state's monopoly on force
  • Preventing civil war: If these groups keep growing, war is inevitable
  • Rule of law: Political violence must have consequences
  • Hypocrisy: How can you ban the Red Front but not the SA?

Arguments AGAINST Banning Paramilitaries

  • Freedom of association: Constitutional right (Article 124)
  • Self-defense: Parties need protection from each other
  • Reichswehr opposition: The military sees the SA as a threat but won't support banning Stahlhelm
  • Enforcement: How would you enforce a ban? They'll just go underground
  • Selective banning: Unfair to ban some but not all
  • Constitutional crisis: Total ban requires 2/3 majority (impossible)

Specific Proposals

Option 1: Ban All Paramilitaries

Requires: 2/3 majority (constitutional amendment)

Effect: SA, Reichsbanner, Stahlhelm all banned

Problem: Nearly impossible to achieve politically

Option 2: Ban the SA

Requires: Simple majority

Effect: NSDAP's street fighters illegal

Problem: NSDAP will claim persecution; SA will go underground; enforcing ban is difficult

Option 3: Lift the Red Front Ban

Requires: Simple majority

Effect: KPD paramilitary re-legalized

Problem: Conservatives will be furious; validates Communist street violence

Option 4: Status Quo

Requires: Nothing (just don't act)

Effect: Red Front banned, all others legal

Problem: Street violence continues and worsens

Questions to Consider

  • Can democracy tolerate private armies?
  • Is selective banning (Red Front yes, SA no) defensible or hypocritical?
  • Would banning the SA make the NSDAP more or less dangerous?
  • Does freedom of association include the right to organize paramilitaries?
  • Can a ban be enforced, or will they just go underground?
  • What would the Reichswehr do if you tried to ban the Stahlhelm?

Impact on Stability Index

If SA banned: Moderate decrease (NSDAP claims martyrdom, street violence continues underground)

If all banned: Large increase (Republic shows strength) BUT nearly impossible politically

If Red Front re-legalized: Large decrease (validates communist violence)

If status quo maintained: Small decrease (violence continues)

Antisemitism and the "Jewish Question"

Mandatory Item: Session 4

Portfolio: Interior Ministry

What Is the Issue?

No legislation restricting the rights of Jews has been formally proposed. However, antisemitism pervades public discourse, and völkisch parties regularly demand action on the "Jewish Question."

The Reichstag must decide: Should it take a clear stance on antisemitism and Jewish rights?

Possible actions:

  • Condemn antisemitism and reaffirm equal citizenship (Article 135)
  • Strengthen anti-defamation laws to criminalize racial (not just religious) attacks
  • Restrict Jewish participation in certain professions or institutions
  • Do nothing and maintain the ambiguous status quo

Historical Context: Jews in Germany

See Historical Background: The "Jewish Question" for comprehensive context.

Key facts:

  • Jews comprise less than 1% of the population (564,000 out of 60 million)
  • Thoroughly assimilated, largely secular, overwhelmingly patriotic
  • Article 135 of the constitution guarantees full citizenship regardless of religion
  • 5 of 9 German Nobel Prize winners are Jewish (including Einstein)
  • Jewish Germans served loyally in WWI (a 1916 "Jewish census" proved this, but the military suppressed the results)

Four Positions on Jews in Germany

Position Advocates View
Equal Citizenship SPD, KPD, DDP Jews are citizens with equal rights. Antisemitism is antidemocratic bigotry.
Religious Anti-Judaism Some Catholics, Protestants Religious differences exist, but Jews who convert are no longer Jews. No legal restrictions justified.
Cultural Anti-Judaism Most Conservatives, DNVP Jewish values conflict with German ideals. Socially acceptable prejudice. Assimilation possible but difficult.
Racial Antisemitism NSDAP, Pan-German League Jews are a biologically destructive race. Cannot be assimilated. Must be isolated/removed from Germany.

The Legal Situation

Current law (Penal Code §166): Forbids defamation based on beliefs (religion), not race.

The loophole: Antisemites claim they're attacking Jews as a race, not a religion, so §166 doesn't apply.

Example: Joseph Goebbels (NSDAP) has been prosecuted twice for antisemitic attacks on Berlin police vice president Bernhard Weiss (a Jew). Goebbels argued he was attacking Weiss's race, not his religion.

Antisemitic Conspiracy Theories

Antisemites promote conspiracy theories that Jews created:

  • Democracy to destroy traditional authority
  • Marxism to destroy capitalism
  • International finance to manipulate nations into war
  • The November Revolution to "stab Germany in the back"

These theories draw on The Protocols of the Elders of Zion, a fabricated text claiming to expose a Jewish plot for world domination. (It was created by the Russian secret police, but many Germans believe it's real.)

The Stab-in-the-Back Myth

Antisemitism fuses with the stab-in-the-back myth: Germany wasn't defeated militarily—it was betrayed by Jews, Marxists, and democrats on the home front.

This is a lie. Germany's military situation in October 1918 was hopeless. General Ludendorff himself demanded the armistice. But the myth allows nationalists to blame Jews for Germany's defeat.

The Dilemma

Arguments FOR Condemning Antisemitism

  • Constitutional duty: Article 135 guarantees equal rights
  • Moral imperative: Antisemitism is racist bigotry
  • National unity: Jewish Germans are patriots—scapegoating them divides the nation
  • International reputation: The world is watching
  • Slippery slope: If antisemitism is acceptable today, what restrictions come tomorrow?
  • Strengthen democracy: Show the Republic defends all citizens

Arguments AGAINST Condemning Antisemitism

  • Freedom of speech: People have a right to their opinions
  • Political backlash: Condemning antisemitism will energize the NSDAP and DNVP
  • Cultural conflict: Many Germans genuinely believe Jews are "different"
  • Disproportionate influence: Jews are overrepresented in certain professions (banking, law, medicine, journalism)
  • Why now?: No violence against Jews is occurring—why provoke conflict?
  • Unenforceable: You can't legislate away prejudice

Possible Proposals

Option 1: Strengthen Anti-Defamation Laws

Proposal: Amend Penal Code §166 to criminalize racial (not just religious) defamation

Effect: Antisemitic attacks on Jews as a "race" would be illegal

Problem: NSDAP/DNVP claim this violates free speech; enforcement would be difficult

Option 2: Reichstag Resolution Condemning Antisemitism

Proposal: Non-binding resolution affirming equal citizenship and condemning racial prejudice

Effect: Symbolic statement; no legal force

Problem: NSDAP/DNVP will vote against it and claim persecution

Option 3: Restrict Jewish Participation

Proposal: Quotas or restrictions in certain professions (proposed by NSDAP/some DNVP)

Effect: Violates Article 135; unprecedented violation of civil rights

Problem: Where does it stop? Likely unconstitutional.

Option 4: Status Quo

Proposal: Do nothing; maintain current ambiguity

Effect: Antisemitism continues unchallenged

Problem: Failure to defend Jewish citizens emboldens extremists

Questions to Consider

  • Does the constitution's guarantee of equal rights require active defense of minorities?
  • Is antisemitism a legitimate cultural concern or racist bigotry?
  • Can you condemn antisemitism without provoking a backlash that strengthens the NSDAP?
  • Are Jews "overrepresented" in certain fields, and if so, does that justify restrictions?
  • Is the "Jewish Question" even a real issue, or a manufactured crisis?
  • What happens if the Republic fails to defend its most vulnerable citizens?

Impact on Stability Index

If antisemitism condemned: Small increase (Republic shows moral courage) BUT moderate nationalist backlash

If restrictions imposed on Jews: MASSIVE decrease (constitutional crisis, international condemnation, moral catastrophe)

If status quo maintained: Small decrease (failure to defend citizens emboldens extremists)

Censorship and All Quiet on the Western Front

Status: Discretionary Item

Portfolio: Interior Ministry

What Is the Issue?

Should the government censor All Quiet on the Western Front?

More broadly: What limits, if any, should exist on freedom of expression in the Republic?

Article 118 of the constitution: "Censorship is forbidden."

But can the government restrict content that "defames the military" or "undermines national unity"?

What Is All Quiet on the Western Front?

All Quiet on the Western Front (Im Westen nichts Neues) is a 1929 novel by Erich Maria Remarque about German soldiers in World War I.

The book:

  • Published January 1929
  • Sold 1.5 million copies in Germany within months
  • International bestseller (translated into dozens of languages)
  • Depicts the horror, futility, and psychological trauma of war
  • Written from the perspective of ordinary German soldiers

The film:

  • American film adaptation (1930) using new sound technology
  • Scheduled for release in Germany in late 1930
  • Conservatives demanding it be banned before it even arrives

Why Is It Controversial?

See Historical Background: Censorship and All Quiet for full context.

All Quiet forces Germans to confront the meaning of the war and the Republic that emerged from it. Every political faction reads it through their own lens:

Competing Interpretations

Political Group Reading
SPD & Pacifists Antiwar novel that legitimizes the Republic and condemns imperial militarism
KPD (Communists) Liberal sentimentalism that fails to indict capitalism as the cause of war
Most Conservatives Insult to the German military, defames doctors/clergy/teachers, unpatriotic
Some Conservatives Honest portrayal of soldiers' suffering; validates soldierly comradeship
NSDAP (Far Right) Treasonous propaganda by a Marxist Jew (Remarque is neither) who should be shot

The Attacks on Remarque

The NSDAP and DNVP launched a vicious campaign against Remarque:

  • Accused him of being a Marxist (he's not)
  • Accused him of being a Jew (he's not)
  • Claimed he never served in the war (he did - he was wounded)
  • Called for him to be tried for treason
  • Disrupted distribution of the book
  • Demanded the film be banned before release

The Legal Question

Article 118: "Censorship is forbidden."

But the Supreme Court has indicated that the government can restrict content that:

  • Constitutes blasphemy (religious offense)
  • Defames the military or state institutions
  • Threatens public order

Does All Quiet meet any of these criteria?

The Dilemma

Arguments FOR Censorship

  • National honor: The book defames German soldiers and the military
  • Public order: The film will provoke riots (NSDAP has threatened to disrupt screenings)
  • Morale: Antiwar sentiment undermines national defense
  • Veterans: Many veterans find the book offensive and disrespectful
  • Foreign propaganda: The book gives Germany's enemies ammunition
  • Precedent: Other countries have restricted it

Arguments AGAINST Censorship

  • Article 118: "Censorship is forbidden" - the constitution is clear
  • Freedom of expression: Democracy requires tolerating offensive speech
  • Truth: Remarque is a veteran telling the truth about war
  • Slippery slope: If you ban this, what's next? Where does censorship end?
  • Streisand effect: Banning it will only make it more popular
  • Hypocrisy: The NSDAP wants to ban it because it makes war look bad - that's the point!
  • Authoritarian precedent: Censorship is what dictatorships do

Possible Proposals

Option 1: Ban the Book and Film

Proposal: Use public order laws to restrict All Quiet

Effect: Book removed from stores, film banned from theaters

Problem: Violates Article 118; creates martyrdom; likely unenforceable

Option 2: Ban Only the Film

Proposal: Allow the book but ban the film (to prevent riots)

Effect: Film doesn't screen in Germany

Problem: Arbitrary distinction; still violates free expression

Option 3: Restrict to Adults Only

Proposal: Age restrictions (18+ only)

Effect: Limits youth exposure

Problem: Still a form of censorship; who decides what's "appropriate"?

Option 4: Defend Freedom of Expression

Proposal: Explicitly protect All Quiet and condemn censorship attempts

Effect: Upholds Article 118; book/film freely available

Problem: Conservatives furious; NSDAP will disrupt screenings anyway

Option 5: Status Quo

Proposal: Do nothing; let local authorities decide

Effect: Patchwork of local bans and permissions

Problem: Ambiguity allows harassment of theaters and bookstores

Questions to Consider

  • Does freedom of expression include the right to criticize the military?
  • Is All Quiet unpatriotic or truthful?
  • Can the Republic tolerate art that undermines national myths?
  • Where should the line be drawn between free speech and public order?
  • Is censorship ever justified in a democracy?
  • What message does banning this book send about the Republic's values?
  • If the NSDAP threatens violence, should the government give in to their demands?

Impact on Stability Index

If banned: Large decrease (Republic abandons free expression, validates NSDAP tactics)

If defended: Small increase (Republic shows commitment to freedom) BUT conservative backlash

If restricted (adults only): Moderate decrease (halfway censorship satisfies no one)

If status quo: Small decrease (ambiguity allows continued harassment)

Austerity and Unemployment Insurance

Mandatory Item: Session 2

Presented by: Economic Minister Eduard Dingeldey (DVP)

Portfolio: Economics Ministry

What Is the Issue?

The budget is in crisis. Unemployment is soaring. Should the government cut unemployment benefits to balance the budget?

This is THE issue that threatens to break the Grand Coalition.

The Economic Crisis

By late 1929, Germany faces a perfect storm:

  • Wall Street Crash (October 1929): American loans recalled; credit dries up
  • Unemployment: Rising from ~1.3 million (1928) to ~3 million (winter 1929-30)
  • State liquidity crisis: Tax revenues falling as unemployment insurance costs soar
  • Budget deficits since 1925
  • Trade imbalance: Imports exceed exports

The Unemployment Insurance Law (1927)

In 1927, during the boom years, the Reichstag passed compulsory unemployment insurance:

  • All workers insured
  • Employer and worker contributions (equal split)
  • Benefits guaranteed if unemployed "through no fault of their own"
  • Fixed benefit levels set by law

The problem: The law assumed continued economic growth. Instead, unemployment tripled. The insurance fund is bankrupt. The state must cover the shortfall.

The Austerity Debate

See Historical Background: Austerity for full context.

The liberal/conservative position (DVP, DDP, Centre, DNVP):

Deflation and Austerity

Cut state spending drastically to:

  1. Prevent hyperinflation (1923 must never happen again)
  2. Lower prices (deflation makes German goods competitive)
  3. Restore creditworthiness (balanced budget = investor confidence)
  4. Force reparations renegotiation (show Allies Germany can't pay)

Short-term pain for long-term gain. Most economists support this approach.

The socialist position (SPD, KPD):

Maintain Social Spending

Cutting unemployment benefits during mass unemployment is:

  • Cruel: Workers didn't cause the crisis - why should they pay?
  • Economically counterproductive: Austerity deepens recession
  • Politically suicidal: The SPD cannot betray its core constituency
  • A betrayal: Unemployment insurance is a right, not a privilege

Alternative: Tax the wealthy, cut military spending, maintain benefits

The Coalition-Breaking Issue

SPD Position

"We will not betray the working class."

The SPD cannot vote to cut unemployment benefits. It would destroy the party's credibility with workers and drive them to the KPD.

If forced to choose: The SPD will leave the coalition rather than betray workers

DVP Position

"We will not bankrupt Germany."

The DVP (representing business interests) cannot accept deficit spending. It violates economic orthodoxy and risks hyperinflation.

If forced to choose: The DVP will leave the coalition rather than accept fiscal irresponsibility

⚠️ THE Coalition Crisis

This is the issue that historically destroyed the Grand Coalition in 1930. The SPD and DVP cannot both get what they want.

If the coalition collapses, the president can:

  • Appoint a minority government
  • Rule by emergency decree (25/48/53)
  • Call new elections

Any of these options weakens parliamentary democracy.

Possible Proposals

Option 1: Cut Unemployment Benefits

Proposal: Reduce benefit levels and/or eligibility

Effect: Budget balanced, DVP satisfied

Problem: SPD cannot accept; workers suffer; coalition likely collapses

Option 2: Maintain Benefits, Raise Taxes

Proposal: Increase taxes on wealth/capital to fund benefits

Effect: Budget balanced, SPD satisfied

Problem: DVP cannot accept; seen as socialist confiscation; coalition likely collapses

Option 3: Compromise (Small Cuts + Small Tax Increases)

Proposal: Minor benefit reduction + minor tax increase

Effect: Neither side happy, but coalition survives

Problem: May not satisfy either party's base; "splitting the difference" may not actually solve the budget crisis

Option 4: Deficit Spending

Proposal: Borrow to cover shortfall; maintain benefits

Effect: SPD satisfied, immediate crisis delayed

Problem: DVP/Centre/DNVP furious; seen as economic irresponsibility; violates orthodox economics

Option 5: Presidential Decree

Proposal: Pass the issue to the president to resolve by emergency decree

Effect: Reichstag avoids responsibility

Problem: Strengthens presidential government; weakens parliamentary democracy

Questions to Consider

  • Who should bear the burden of economic crisis - workers or the wealthy?
  • Is preventing hyperinflation worth imposing austerity during mass unemployment?
  • Are unemployment benefits a right or a privilege?
  • Can the economy recover through austerity, or does cutting spending deepen recession?
  • Is the coalition worth saving if it requires betraying your core constituency?
  • What happens to democracy if parliamentary government cannot solve this crisis?

Impact on Stability Index

If benefits cut: LARGE decrease (workers radicalized, SPD discredited, KPD gains)

If benefits maintained via deficit: Moderate decrease (economic crisis worsens, business community alienated)

If compromise reached: Small decrease (neither side satisfied, but coalition holds)

If coalition collapses: MASSIVE decrease (parliamentary democracy fails, opens door to presidential government)

Nationalization of the Banks

Status: Discretionary Item

Portfolio: Economics Ministry

What Is the Issue?

Should the state take over the banks?

More specifically:

  • Should private banks be nationalized (state ownership)?
  • Should banks be "Aryanized" (Jews removed from ownership/management)?
  • Should the state regulate banks more heavily without nationalizing them?

Historical Context

See Historical Background: Nationalization for full context.

Nationalization has precedent:

  • The Kaiserreich nationalized the post office and railways
  • During WWI, the state regulated essential industries
  • The Socialization Law of 1919 promised state control of key industries
  • The Soviet Union fully nationalized industry (though this is controversial)

Why banks? Many blame banks for:

  • The Wall Street crash
  • Calling in loans that triggered the German crisis
  • Profiting from war (especially reparations)
  • Speculating with depositors' money
  • Foreclosing on farms and small businesses

The Antisemitic Angle

Conservatives routinely accuse Jews of controlling banks and using them to oppress Germany:

  • Jews are "internationalists" running a world conspiracy based on finance
  • Jewish bankers started the war for profit
  • Jewish bankers profit from reparations
  • Jewish bankers charge usurious rates
  • Jewish bankers foreclose on German farms

This leads to calls for "Aryanization": Replace Jewish bank owners/managers with "Aryans," but keep banks private.

Reality: Most banks are not Jewish-owned. But the stereotype persists.

The Dilemma

Arguments FOR Nationalization

  • Economic stability: State control prevents reckless speculation
  • Public good: Banking is too important to leave to private profit
  • Social control: Banks would serve workers and farmers, not speculators
  • Prevent foreclosures: State banks wouldn't kick farmers off their land
  • Reparations: Show Allies that Germany's economy is collapsing
  • Socialist principle: Key industries should be publicly owned

Arguments AGAINST Nationalization

  • Property rights: Confiscation violates private property
  • Bolshevism: Nationalization = communism
  • Economic disaster: State cannot run banks efficiently
  • International reaction: Foreign investment will flee; credit will dry up
  • Slippery slope: If banks today, what industry tomorrow?
  • Free market: Competition improves efficiency; state monopoly doesn't

The "Aryanization" Question

Arguments FOR Aryanization

  • Jewish conspiracy: International Jewish bankers control finance
  • National interest: German institutions should be run by Germans
  • Popular demand: Many Germans believe this
  • Compromise: Keep banks private but remove Jewish influence

Arguments AGAINST Aryanization

  • Antisemitic scapegoating: Jews don't control banks - this is a conspiracy theory
  • Violates Article 135: Equal citizenship regardless of religion/race
  • Arbitrary confiscation: Theft based on race
  • Where does it end?: If banks today, what profession tomorrow?
  • Moral catastrophe: State-sanctioned racial discrimination

Possible Proposals

Option 1: Full Nationalization

Proposal: State takes over all major banks

Effect: Banking becomes a public utility

Problem: Seen as Bolshevism; international credit dries up; conservatives/liberals furious

Option 2: Partial Nationalization

Proposal: State takes over only the largest banks; small banks remain private

Effect: State controls major credit but preserves some private banking

Problem: Still seen as socialist confiscation; may not actually solve crisis

Option 3: Heavy Regulation (No Nationalization)

Proposal: Banks remain private but under strict state oversight

Effect: Prevents reckless behavior without confiscation

Problem: Doesn't satisfy those who want state control; banks resist regulation

Option 4: Aryanization

Proposal: Remove Jews from bank ownership/management; banks remain private

Effect: Antisemitic policy; violates Article 135

Problem: State-sanctioned racial discrimination; likely unconstitutional; international condemnation

Option 5: Status Quo

Proposal: Leave banks as they are

Effect: Crisis continues; banks continue risky behavior

Problem: Public anger at banks unaddressed; more foreclosures; deepening crisis

Questions to Consider

  • Are banks public utilities or private businesses?
  • Is nationalization socialist reform or Bolshevik revolution?
  • Can the state run banks more efficiently than the private sector?
  • Do Jews control international finance, or is this antisemitic conspiracy theory?
  • Would Aryanization solve economic problems or just scapegoat a minority?
  • What would international investors do if Germany nationalized banks?

Impact on Stability Index

If fully nationalized: LARGE decrease (international crisis, capital flight, "Bolshevism" fears)

If partially nationalized: Moderate decrease (economic uncertainty, conservative backlash)

If heavily regulated: Small decrease (banks resist, neither side satisfied)

If Aryanized: MASSIVE decrease (constitutional crisis, international condemnation, moral catastrophe)

If status quo: Small decrease (crisis continues, public anger grows)

Agricultural Affairs (Food Ministry)

Agricultural debates confront the deep crisis in the countryside: Should the state protect large estates or small farmers? Should food be cheap for workers or expensive to support agriculture? Who owns the land?

For full historical context, see Historical Background: Agricultural Affairs

Grain Tariffs

Mandatory Item: Session 3

Portfolio: Food Ministry

What Is the Issue?

Should Germany maintain high tariffs on imported grain?

This pits urban workers (who want cheap food) against Junker estate owners (who want protection from competition) against small farmers (who get little benefit from tariffs but suffer from high costs).

What Are Grain Tariffs?

Tariffs are taxes on imports. High grain tariffs make foreign grain expensive, protecting German producers from international competition.

The problem: These tariffs primarily benefit large Junker estates (which produce most of Germany's grain), not small farmers. And they raise food prices for urban workers.

Historical Context: The Junker Class

See Historical Background: The Junkers for full context.

Who are the Junkers?

  • Protestant landowning aristocratic elite of eastern Prussia
  • Dominated state and military in the Kaiserreich
  • Socially conservative, monarchist, anti-democratic
  • Own massive grain-producing estates
  • President von Hindenburg is a Junker

The Junker estates are drowning in debt. Due to:

  • Postwar collapse in grain prices
  • Failure to modernize
  • Cutting off of East Prussia from Germany (Polish Corridor)
  • Cheap grain imports from America, Russia, Argentina

Without high tariffs and state subsidies (Osthilfe), many estates would go bankrupt.

The Arguments

Arguments FOR High Tariffs

  • National security: Germany must be self-sufficient in food (autarky)
  • 1914-1918 lesson: The British blockade starved Germany - never again
  • Protect domestic production: Without tariffs, German agriculture collapses
  • Patriotism: International grain market controlled by Jewish bankers (antisemitic trope)
  • Rural preservation: Agriculture is morally superior to urban decadence
  • Political power: Junkers are powerful - angering them is dangerous

Arguments AGAINST High Tariffs

  • Hurt workers: Tariffs raise food prices, hurting urban poor
  • Help only Junkers: Small farmers get little benefit
  • Economic inefficiency: Protectionism distorts markets
  • Free trade: Lower tariffs = lower prices = higher standard of living
  • Drain state coffers: Tariffs + Osthilfe subsidies cost taxpayers
  • Unfair: Why should workers pay more for food to protect aristocratic estates?

The Small Farmer Problem

Small farmers are caught in the middle:

  • Tariffs don't help them much (they produce for local markets, not export)
  • But tariffs raise their costs (equipment, fertilizer, feed are more expensive)
  • They face debt, foreclosure, bankruptcy
  • They get no sympathy from urban workers or liberals

Result: Rural radicalization. Small farmers turning to NSDAP and Rural People's Movement.

Autarky and National Security

Junkers and nationalists link tariffs to:

  • Autarky (self-sufficiency): Germany must produce its own food
  • Military preparedness: You can't fight a war if you depend on imports
  • Anti-Versailles patriotism: International markets are controlled by enemies
  • Antisemitism: Jewish bankers control grain trade

The counterargument: Even with tariffs, Germany cannot feed itself. The goal is impossible. Tariffs just make food expensive.

The Stakes

If High Tariffs Maintained:

  • Junker estates protected
  • Food prices remain high
  • Workers pay more for bread
  • Small farmers get little benefit
  • State continues subsidies (Osthilfe)
  • Hindenburg/Junker class satisfied

If Tariffs Reduced or Eliminated:

  • Food prices drop
  • Workers benefit
  • Junker estates face bankruptcy
  • Small farmers may benefit (lower costs) or may suffer (can't compete with cheap imports)
  • Hindenburg/Junker class furious
  • Conservatives claim "betrayal of German agriculture"

Questions to Consider

  • Should urban workers pay more for food to protect aristocratic estates?
  • Is agricultural self-sufficiency (autarky) achievable or desirable?
  • Do tariffs actually help small farmers, or only Junkers?
  • Is free trade economically efficient but politically dangerous?
  • What happens if you anger President von Hindenburg (himself a Junker)?
  • Can Germany feed itself without imports?

Impact on Stability Index

If tariffs maintained: Small decrease (workers angry, food expensive)

If tariffs reduced: Moderate decrease (Junker/conservative fury, Hindenburg alienated)

If tariffs eliminated: LARGE decrease (agricultural crisis, Hindenburg may intervene)

Small Farmers' Relief

Status: Discretionary Item

Portfolio: Food Ministry

What Is the Issue?

Should the state provide relief to small farmers facing foreclosure?

Options include:

  • Debt relief: Forgive or restructure farm debts
  • Subsidized loans: Cheap credit for small farmers
  • Land reform: Confiscate unproductive Junker estates and redistribute to small farmers
  • Protection from foreclosure: Legal barriers preventing banks from seizing farms

The Small Farmer Crisis

See Historical Background: The Small Farmer's Plight for full context.

What small farmers face:

  • Hyperinflation (1923) destroyed their capital
  • Tight credit prevents modernization
  • Cheap imports undercut prices
  • Rising taxes
  • Falling demand (urban workers tighten belts)
  • Result: Bankruptcies, foreclosures, desperation

What they want:

  • Debt relief or forgiveness
  • Protection from foreclosure
  • Cheaper credit
  • Lower taxes
  • Higher food prices (but workers want lower prices!)

Land Reform: The Radical Solution

Many demand land reform: Confiscate indebted or unproductive Junker estates and redistribute to small farmers.

Precedent: The Settlement Law of 1919 authorized land redistribution but was never seriously implemented.

Arguments FOR Land Reform

  • Social justice: Land should belong to those who work it
  • Economic efficiency: Small farms are more productive than Junker estates
  • Lower food prices: More efficient production = cheaper food
  • Rural employment: More small farms = more jobs
  • Autarky: More farmers = more food security
  • Political: Break Junker power

Arguments AGAINST Land Reform

  • Property rights: Confiscation violates private property
  • Bolshevism: This is agrarian communism
  • Hindenburg's land: The President is a Junker landowner
  • Social order: Destroys traditional hierarchy
  • Practical: Who decides what's "unproductive"?
  • Dangerous precedent: If estates today, what property tomorrow?

The Green Front (1929)

See Historical Background: The Green Front for context.

In February 1929, agricultural groups formed the Green Front to unite farmers. Led by Martin Schiele (CNBP), the Green Front demands:

  • Exclude socialists from government
  • Address small farmer needs
  • Protect large farmers (Junkers)
  • Implement austerity
  • Make cabinet ministers independent of parties

In effect: Undermine parliamentary democracy while expressing rural needs.

If their demands aren't met, the Green Front threatens to become a powerful anti-system force.

The Rural People's Movement

Since 1928, desperate small farmers have launched:

  • Local riots
  • Protests
  • Terrorist attacks (bombing tax offices, etc.)

The countryside is radicalizing. Many farmers turning to NSDAP.

The Dilemma

Arguments FOR Small Farmer Relief

  • Humanitarian: Farmers are suffering
  • Political stability: Prevent rural radicalization
  • Food security: Need small farmers to produce food
  • Economic stimulus: Debt relief allows farmers to spend/invest
  • Social justice: Farmers didn't cause the crisis

Arguments AGAINST Small Farmer Relief

  • Budget crisis: State has no money
  • Moral hazard: Rewarding bad management
  • Free market: Let inefficient farms fail
  • Urban workers first: Why help farmers when workers are unemployed?
  • Slippery slope: Debt relief for farmers today, everyone tomorrow
  • Precedent: Violates liberal economic principles

Possible Proposals

Option 1: Full Debt Relief

Proposal: Forgive farm debts

Effect: Farmers saved from foreclosure

Problem: Massively expensive; seen as rewarding failure; where does money come from?

Option 2: Subsidized Loans

Proposal: State provides cheap credit

Effect: Farmers can refinance debt, invest in modernization

Problem: Still expensive; may not help farmers who can't service any debt

Option 3: Land Reform

Proposal: Confiscate unproductive Junker estates, redistribute to small farmers

Effect: More small farms, breaks Junker power

Problem: Hindenburg/Junkers furious; seen as Bolshevism; constitutional crisis

Option 4: Protection from Foreclosure

Proposal: Legal barriers preventing bank seizures

Effect: Farmers keep land

Problem: Violates property rights; banks furious; doesn't solve debt problem

Option 5: Status Quo

Proposal: Let market forces work

Effect: Foreclosures continue

Problem: Rural radicalization intensifies; Green Front becomes more powerful; violence increases

Questions to Consider

  • Does the state have an obligation to protect farmers from foreclosure?
  • Is debt relief fair when urban workers also suffer?
  • Should inefficient farms be allowed to fail?
  • Would land reform solve the agrarian crisis or create a political crisis?
  • Can the Republic survive rural radicalization?
  • What happens if the countryside turns against democracy?

Impact on Stability Index

If debt relief provided: Small increase (rural anger decreases) BUT budget crisis worsens

If land reform enacted: Moderate decrease (Hindenburg/Junkers furious, constitutional crisis)

If status quo maintained: LARGE decrease (rural radicalization accelerates, violence increases)
