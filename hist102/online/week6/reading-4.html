Chapter 31  ·  Section 3

The 1990s: Prosperity & Permanent Conservatism

Clinton, neoliberalism, and the institutionalization of the right

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Clinton's “Third Way” and how he benefited from and inherited Reagan era policies
  • Analyze the technology boom, NAFTA, and economic expansion of the 1990s
  • Trace how conservative institutions became permanent power centers
  • Evaluate the long-term consequences of asymmetric institutional power

Bill Clinton, elected president in 1992, represented a “Third Way”—a centrist approach that accepted much of the Reagan era framework. He embraced neoliberalism: free trade, deregulation, and a smaller federal role, while maintaining social programs. He signed the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) in 1994, which eliminated tariffs among the United States, Canada, and Mexico, creating a common market of 425 million people.

The 1990s were an era of economic expansion. The tech boom created a speculative bubble centered on startups with no profits. The stock market soared; unemployment fell to 4 percent; the economy grew at 4.5 percent average annually from 1992 to 2000. The federal government ran budget surpluses ($230 billion by 2000) for the first time since 1969. Wealth inequality continued to increase. The top 1 percent's share of national income rose from 13 percent in 1980 to 23 percent by 2000. Yet optimism was widespread. The internet, emerging as a public resource through the 1990s, promised a world of abundance and democracy. The White House created its first website in 1994. Americans, particularly younger Americans, believed the future would be better than the past.

Bill Clinton & Congress
1993–2001
Clinton's presidency coincided with Republican-controlled Congress (1995–2001). Despite partisan division, Clinton and the Republicans often agreed on economic policy: NAFTA, deregulation, deficit reduction. This agreement on neoliberal economics obscured deeper ideological rifts.

Chapter 31  ·  Section 4

The Permanent Conservative Majority

Heritage Foundation, Fox News, and asymmetric polarization

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how conservative think tanks and media became permanent power centers
  • Analyze the rise of asymmetric partisanship and why Democrats did not build equivalent institutions
  • Trace the consequences for American democracy and polarization

Even as the Cold War ended and prosperity seemed assured, conservative institutions consolidated permanent power. The Heritage Foundation, AEI, and other conservative organizations had built vast networks of intellectuals, journalists, and policymakers. Fox News, launched in 1996, became the dominant voice for American conservatism. Conservative radio hosts like Rush Limbaugh reached millions. Conservative intellectuals embedded themselves in universities, law schools, and policy circles. The asymmetry was striking: liberals and progressives had no equivalent institutional apparatus. No equivalent think tank, no equivalent media network, no equivalent ability to staff administrations with ideologically aligned personnel.

This asymmetry would prove consequential. Democratic administrations often appointed centrists and moderates to key positions. Republican administrations appointed ideological conservatives who saw government as a tool to advance conservative goals. Conservative institutions trained lawyers, judges, and academics who would reshape law for decades. When conservative justices gained a Supreme Court majority in 2005, they had been cultivated by Federalist Society networks—a conservative legal organization founded in 1982. When Republican administrations cut taxes, slashed regulations, or appointed conservative judges, they were implementing policies developed by Heritage Foundation and AEI researchers. The conservatives had built a permanent infrastructure of power that outlasted electoral cycles.

“Conservatism had lost a great many of its potential leaders to the demands of business and commerce. Those who were not called to make their fortunes were deterred from public service by the higher incomes available in law and scholarship. Yet it is precisely those talents which the conservative movement needs. If the young men and women now in college have the freedom to choose, an important obstacle to conservatism will have been overcome.” — William F. Buckley Jr., founding National Review, 1955

Week 6 Comprehensive Synthesis

From Cold War to permanent conservatism: 1945–2000

This week's readings traced a 55-year arc from the end of World War II to the year 2000. We began with demobilization, the GI Bill's promise and its racial limitations, and the emergence of the Cold War. We traced the McCarthyism and loyalty boards that criminalized dissent. We examined the Civil Rights Movement's victories and the backlash that produced Nixon's Southern Strategy. We saw the counterculture challenge Cold War conformity, only to be met by conservative institutionalization. We analyzed Reaganomics, the Religious Right, and the War on Drugs' disproportionate impact on Black Americans. And we ended with the 1990s prosperity and the consolidation of conservative institutional power that would define American politics for the next two decades.

Throughout this arc, we saw three patterns: (1) prosperity's racial boundaries—the GI Bill built White middle class while excluding Black veterans; (2) the use of fear and security rhetoric to expand state power—Cold War fears became McCarthyism and the War on Drugs; (3) the institutionalization of conservative power—think tanks, media networks, legal organizations that outlasted electoral cycles. Understanding these patterns is crucial for understanding contemporary American politics, where inequality has deepened, partisan polarization has intensified, and institutions have been weaponized by ideological movements.
