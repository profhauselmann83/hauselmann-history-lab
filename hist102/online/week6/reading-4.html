Heritage Foundation & Conservative Movement
The Conservative Turn

In 1973, Edwin Feulner and Paul Weyrich founded the Heritage Foundation with funding from Joseph Coors. They wanted to challenge what they saw as liberal intellectual dominance in universities, think tanks, media, and government. Liberals had Brookings Institution and the Ford Foundation. Conservatives had nothing comparable.

Feulner and Weyrich's innovation was the "briefcase test": they produced policy papers short enough to fit in a briefcase that busy policymakers could actually read. This contrasted with academic tomes. They weren't interested in theoretical debates—they wanted practical policy proposals that could be implemented immediately.

By the 1980s, conservatives controlled the presidency. The Heritage Foundation provided policy ideas directly to Reagan's administration. Reagan incorporated Heritage recommendations into his legislative agenda. For a moment, conservatism seemed to be winning—reshaping American politics around free-market ideology and military strength.

My Notes — Strategy Shift
0 words✓ Saved

Yet success brought disillusionment. When Reagan moderated his positions before his 1984 reelection, many conservatives felt betrayed. When George H.W. Bush agreed to a tax increase (breaking his "no new taxes" pledge), conservatives erupted. They had hoped for a revolution; instead they got pragmatism. This fueled a new conservatism: less focused on changing minds, more focused on winning power and obstructing the opposition.

My Notes — Ideas vs. Power

Conservative distrust of Republican leaders deepened. By the 1990s, many conservatives embraced obstructionism and procedural warfare as primary tactics. They couldn't transform the government alone, but they could block the other side from governing. This mindset would shape American politics for decades.
