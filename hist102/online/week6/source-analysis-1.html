Overarching Question

Who was primarily responsible for the Cold War, the Soviet Union or the United States?

History is not neutral. Different observers interpret the same events differently based on their assumptions, values, and access to information. Below are four primary sources from 1946—a crucial moment when the Soviet-American alliance was disintegrating. Read them carefully, noting how each speaker frames intentions, responsibilities, and threats.

Document A: Winston Churchill's "Iron Curtain" Speech (March 1946)

Document A
The "Sinews of Peace" Speech
Winston Churchill, former British Prime Minister, speaking at Westminster College in Fulton, Missouri, with President Truman present, March 5, 1946.
"From Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic, an iron curtain has descended across the continent. Behind that line lie all the capitals of the ancient states of Central and Eastern Europe… The Russian-dominated Polish Government has been encouraged to make enormous and wrongful inroads upon Germany… All these famous historic states and peoples of Europe have to accept the new Soviet control… It is not our duty at this time when difficulties are so numerous to interfere forcibly in the internal affairs of countries which we have not been able to save from the Soviet spell. But the Russians, in their sphere, have done everything they could to promote communism throughout the world."
Question A1
Winston Churchill's "Iron Curtain" speech and Henry Wallace's letter to Truman (July 1946) present starkly different diagnoses of Soviet intentions. According to Churchill, what does the Soviet Union want? According to Wallace, what has the United States done that might make the Soviets distrust American motives? Whose interpretation do you find more convincing based on the evidence, and why?

Document B: Truman Doctrine Speech (March 1947)

Document B
Address to Congress on the Truman Doctrine
President Harry Truman, addressing Congress to request military and economic aid for Greece and Turkey, March 12, 1947.
"I believe that it must be the policy of the United States to support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation by armed minorities or by external pressures… The free peoples of the world look to us for support in maintaining their freedoms… Should we fail to aid Greece and Turkey in this fateful hour, the effect will be far reaching to the West as well as to the East… I am fully aware of the broad implications involved… This is no more than a frank recognition that totalitarianism may assume a variety of forms and that today we have the freedom to choose between different futures."

Document C: Soviet Ambassador Novikov's Telegram (September 1946)

Document C
Telegram to Soviet Foreign Ministry
Soviet Ambassador to the United States Nikolai Novikov, reporting to Soviet Foreign Minister V.M. Molotov on American intentions, September 27, 1946.
"The foreign policy of the United States, which reflects the imperialist tendencies of American monopolistic capital, is characterized… by a striving for world supremacy… American monopolistic capital… is trying to utilize the force of the entire American state apparatus to achieve its goals of world domination… The principal immediate clients of American imperialism in Europe and Asia are Great Britain and the countries under its control… American strategic plans for a future war have been developed and are being implemented… [The U.S.] seeks to reduce Russia to a secondary power… The U.S. is pursuing… the construction of military-strategic bases all over the world… There is in the minds of American leaders… a firm conviction that the future belongs to them."
Question A3
Compare how the Truman Doctrine speech and Novikov's telegram each use language about security, threat, and responsibility. For example, Truman says "the free peoples of the world look to us," while Novikov describes American "imperialist tendencies." How do these rhetorical choices reveal each side's worldview? What would someone reading only one of these documents conclude about who started the Cold War?

Document D: Henry Wallace Letter to Truman (July 1946)

Document D
Private Letter to President Truman
Henry Wallace, former Vice President and Secretary of Commerce, writing privately to President Truman to warn against aggressive Soviet policy, July 23, 1946.
"How do American actions since V-J Day appear to other nations? I mean we have appeared to them to be behaving as the "have" nations of the world and to have either lined up the "have not" nations behind the United States, for the United States to do with those nations as we wish, or else we have started to adopt the policy of complete isolation… I think we should make an effort to prevent an arms race… I believe that the common people of Russia want peace and normal relations with the United States more than anything else… An atomic bomb in the hands of either the United States or Russia constitutes a threat to world peace and to the very existence of civilization itself."
Question A4
Henry Wallace argues that an "armaments race does not lead to peace but to war" and that the United States should "allay any reasonable Russian grounds for fear." Evaluate this argument. Reflection

These four sources were all written in 1946—at a critical moment when the Soviet-American alliance was breaking down. Notice how each speaker claims to be responding *defensively* to threats from the other side. This is how Cold Wars develop: through mutual misunderstanding and escalation. History isn't inevitable—it's the result of choices made by leaders, each believing their own side is right.
