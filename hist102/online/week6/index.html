Week 6  ·  April 13 – 19

The Cold War and Beyond

From Containment to Reaction: The Long Struggle for American Power and Purpose

Apr 13 – 19 60 pts Final PDF due Sunday 11:59 PM

This week spans forty years of American history: from the hopeful moment when World War II ended and the atomic age began, through the ideological struggles of the Cold War, the social upheaval of the 1960s, the constitutional crisis of Watergate, and the conservative backlash of the 1980s. You’ll encounter four distinct political visions—Truman’s containment, Nixon’s pragmatism, Carter’s moral leadership, and Reagan’s revolution—and ask which succeeded, which failed, and why Americans kept changing course.

The materials span two major source analyses: one exploring the origins of Cold War mistrust through competing testimonies (Churchill, Truman, Soviet Ambassador Novikov, and Henry Wallace), and one examining the Watergate scandal through primary evidence (Nixon’s speech, Dean’s testimony, Haldeman’s account, and the White House tapes themselves). These sources teach you how to read across contradictory accounts and evaluate reliability based on evidence, motive, and consequence.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain how the policy of containment shaped U.S. foreign policy and Cold War strategy
  • Analyze the sources of Cold War mistrust by reading multiple perspectives
  • Evaluate the outcomes of the Vietnam War, civil rights movements, and Watergate crisis
  • Assess the Reagan Revolution’s economic policies and their long-term consequences
  • Trace the rise of conservatism as a political and intellectual movement
  • Connect historical events to Haass’s Bill of Obligations themes
Central Question

“How did American leaders respond to the Soviet challenge after 1945? What assumptions guided their policies? And when those assumptions failed—or when Americans reacted against them—how did the nation redefine itself?”

Reaction & Remaking: After WWII, Americans initially pursued a Cold War strategy of containment. But Cold War assumptions about permanent ideological conflict created unintended consequences: vast military spending, government secrecy, mistrust of authority, mass incarceration, and environmental damage. By the 1980s, a conservative backlash rejected both Cold War liberalism AND the social experiments of the 1960s.

The question becomes: was conservatism a legitimate correction of liberal excess, or a power grab that exploited legitimate grievances? Your role is to weigh the evidence carefully.
