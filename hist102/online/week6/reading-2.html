Chapter 29  ·  Section 1

The Civil Rights Movement Matures

From Brown v. Board to sit-ins, Freedom Rides, and nonviolent resistance

Learning Objectives

  • Trace legal and direct-action strategies that dismantled segregation
  • Explain how grassroots organizing sustained the movement through violence and backlash
  • Analyze the shift from desegregation to Black Power

The Supreme Court's 1954 decision in Brown v. Board of Education declared segregated public schools unconstitutional. Yet implementation proved glacially slow. The Court mandated desegregation “with all deliberate speed” in 1955—language that allowed massive resistance. In 1957, when nine Black students attempted to enter Little Rock's Central High School, Governor Orval Faubus called out the state National Guard to block them. President Eisenhower, who privately disagreed with Brown, was forced to send federal troops. It was the first time since Reconstruction that federal troops protected Black rights in the South.

White resistance was fierce and multifaceted. Some withdrew children to newly created all-White private academies. Many Southern districts closed public schools entirely rather than integrate—one Virginia county kept schools closed five years. Southern segregationists filed lawsuits to bankrupt the NAACP. 101 Congress members signed the “Southern Manifesto” accusing the Supreme Court of abusing its power. Violence was constant. When Emmett Till, a 14-year-old from Chicago, was murdered while visiting relatives in Mississippi, his mother insisted on an open-casket funeral. The acquittal of his killers shocked the nation.

Rosa Parks
1913–2005
NAACP member from Montgomery, Alabama, who refused to give up her seat on December 1, 1955. Her arrest sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott (381 days), which brought national attention to segregation and elevated MLK to national prominence.

Inspired by Till's death, Rosa Parks, an NAACP member from Montgomery, Alabama, refused to give up her seat to a White man on December 1, 1955. Her arrest sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott, which lasted 381 days. A young minister named Martin Luther King Jr. helped lead and sustain it. Black Montgomery residents walked, organized carpools, and refused to ride buses, facing harassment and violence. In June 1956, a federal court found the segregation ordinance unconstitutional.

The movement shifted tactics in the early 1960s. On February 1, 1960, four Black college students sat down at a segregated lunch counter in Greensboro, North Carolina. Their refusal to leave sparked the sit-in movement, which spread to hundreds of cities. Participants endured abuse—scalding coffee poured on them, White mobs attacked them—but remained nonviolent. In 1961, Freedom Riders rode integrated buses through the South, testing whether segregation was actually enforced. White mobs attacked them in Mississippi; the rides ultimately led to federal enforcement of desegregation.

The movement culminated in the 1963 March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, where over 250,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial. King's “I Have a Dream” speech became the movement's moral centerpiece. Yet even as this massive demonstration showed the movement's power, resistance hardened. Four months later, a White supremacist bombed the 16th Street Baptist Church in Birmingham, killing four Black girls.

“I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up and live out the true meaning of its creed: 'All men are created equal.'” — Martin Luther King Jr., March on Washington, August 28, 1963

Chapter 29  ·  Section 2

Beyond Civil Rights: Identity & Power

The 1968 election, the American Indian Movement, and Black Power

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the rise of the counterculture and what it reveals about generational values
  • Analyze the American Indian Movement's demands and the systemic poverty Native Americans faced
  • Describe the causes and consequences of the 1968 Democratic collapse
  • Trace how Nixon's Southern Strategy exploited White backlash and abandoned civil rights

The 1960s witnessed the rise of the counterculture—a youth rebellion against mainstream values. Young people rejected materialism, militarism, and sexual repression. They created communes, listened to rock music, and experimented with LSD. The counterculture was heavily associated with opposition to the Vietnam War—a war increasingly unpopular as television showed its brutal reality.

The Vietnam War dominated the 1968 election. In January 1968, the Tet Offensive shocked America. Though a military failure for the North, it was a psychological and political victory: trusted CBS anchor Walter Cronkite declared the war unwinnable. President Lyndon B. Johnson announced he would not seek reelection. Senator Eugene McCarthy's anti-war campaign scored an upset victory in New Hampshire. Robert F. Kennedy, attorney general under his brother JFK, entered the race as the leading anti-war candidate. Then, on June 5, 1968, an assassin killed Kennedy. The Democratic National Convention descended into chaos. Police riots broke out outside the convention hall; inside, the party nominated Hubert Humphrey, the sitting vice president and an establishment figure most associated with the war.

The Tet Offensive
January–February 1968
A coordinated North Vietnamese and Viet Cong assault on South Vietnamese cities. Though militarily repelled, it devastated American confidence in the war. Walter Cronkite declared the war unwinnable; LBJ announced he would not seek reelection.

Richard Nixon, the Republican nominee, executed the Southern Strategy—appealing to White voters alienated by the Democratic Party's embrace of civil rights. He used rhetoric of “law and order” to exploit White fear of urban unrest and Black power. He promised to appoint strict constitutionalists to the Supreme Court. Nixon won with 43.4 percent of the vote, while segregationist George Wallace won 13.5 percent.

Meanwhile, the American Indian Movement (AIM), founded in 1968, organized protests against Native American poverty and government oppression. Native Americans faced life expectancy of 46 compared to 69 nationally; reservation unemployment exceeded 50 percent; suicide rates were five times higher. AIM members occupied Alcatraz Island for 19 months (1969–1971). In December 1973, AIM occupied Wounded Knee, South Dakota—site of the 1890 massacre of Lakota people—in a dramatic 71-day standoff. The occupation drew international media attention to Native American grievances.

The civil rights movement's legal gains—the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965—had not translated into economic equality or political power. As frustration mounted, the movement shifted toward Black Power and away from integration-focused civil rights. The Black Panther Party, founded in 1966, armed itself for self-defense and created community programs. The shift alarmed White Americans, but it reflected the movement's recognition that legal equality meant little without economic power.

“The problem is not that Black Power advocates are radical. The problem is that America itself is radical—it has taken radical action to keep Black people oppressed.” — Black Power movement rhetoric, 1960s

Week 6  ·  Synthesis

Connections Across the Reading

Civil rights, backlash, and the fractured 1960s

The 1960s were a decade of extraordinary social change and equally extraordinary resistance. The civil rights movement won legal victories—Brown, the Civil Rights Act, the Voting Rights Act—that transformed law if not yet society. Simultaneously, the counterculture challenged Cold War conformity, Vietnam sparked generational conflict, and the American Indian Movement demanded sovereignty. Yet these movements provoked a White backlash that Nixon channeled into a new political coalition. Understanding this decade means seeing both the movements' moral clarity and their ultimate inability to prevent the conservative counterrevolution that would dominate the next forty years.
