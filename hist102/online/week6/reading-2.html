Chapter 30 · Sections 1–5
The Sixties & The Unraveling

The 1960s began with optimism—a young president, economic growth, hopes for progress. But by decade's end, the nation was fractured. The counterculture rejected middle-class values, civil rights activists demanded equal citizenship, and millions protested the Vietnam War. The Kennedy assassination in 1963, the murder of Martin Luther King Jr. in 1968, and Robert Kennedy's assassination that same year shook national confidence.

The American Indian Movement (AIM) used dramatic protests to draw national attention. In 1969, activists occupied Alcatraz Island, declaring it Indian land under terms of old treaties. The Alcatraz Proclamation used satire and irony: they "offered" to purchase the island from the federal government, mimicking how the U.S. had purchased Native lands centuries before. At Wounded Knee in 1973, AIM members occupied the site of the 1890 massacre, demanding recognition of treaty rights and sovereignty.

Richard Nixon's "southern strategy" appealed to white Southern voters who felt abandoned by the Democratic Party's support for civil rights. Nixon spoke of restoring "law and order" and the "silent majority"—coded language that attracted voters alarmed by social change and urban unrest. Yet Nixon's actual policies were more pragmatic than his rhetoric suggested.

In the early 1970s, the American economy encountered an unfamiliar problem: "stagflation"—simultaneous unemployment and inflation. The Vietnam War had driven up government spending. Oil embargoes caused energy crises. Traditional economic remedies (expanding or contracting spending) seemed powerless. Families suffered wage stagnation while prices soared. This economic crisis shook confidence in government expertise and Keynesian economics.

By 1980, Americans felt the nation was in decline—a feeling that would fuel the Reagan revolution.
