Overview

In 1980, Ronald Reagan won the presidency with a radical platform: cut taxes, deregulate business, weaken unions, and confront the Soviet Union militarily. This reading explores how Reagan assembled a winning coalition, how his economic policies actually played out, and the human costs of his social policies, particularly regarding the AIDS crisis, the War on Drugs, and mass incarceration.

Chapter 31 · Sections 1 & 2
The Reagan Revolution

Reagan won in a landslide, carrying 44 of 50 states. His coalition united three groups: evangelicals concerned about abortion and family values, wealthy business leaders opposed to taxes and regulation, and working- and middle-class voters angry about inflation, taxes, and cultural change. He promised to restore American greatness and defeated Jimmy Carter's message of limits and decline.

Reagan's economic program, called Reaganomics, rested on supply-side economics. The theory was simple: cut taxes on corporations and the wealthy, and their increased investment would stimulate economic growth, creating jobs and generating enough new tax revenue to offset the tax cuts. Deregulation would unleash business innovation. The result, supposedly, would be growth without inflation.

Reagan's rhetorical conservatism was aggressive, but his actions as California governor had been pragmatic. He had raised taxes and increased spending when necessary. As president, he again balanced rhetoric with flexibility, though his basic direction remained conservative. He cut the top marginal tax rate from 70 percent to 28 percent. He also increased military spending dramatically, creating massive budget deficits.

The AIDS epidemic of the 1980s revealed a moral crisis. Reagan barely mentioned the disease publicly for years. Religious conservatives blamed gay people for the epidemic. People with AIDS faced discrimination in housing, employment, and healthcare. Community organizations like ACT UP had to force the government and media to treat AIDS as a urgent public health crisis. Activist Ryan White, an innocent teenager infected through contaminated blood transfusions, became a symbol of the crisis—and of the tragedy when religious bigotry blocked compassion.

The War on Drugs, initiated under Reagan, targeted drug dealers and users with mandatory minimum sentences and aggressive law enforcement. Street-level drug enforcement exploded. Police practices, like stop-and-frisk, targeted predominantly Black neighborhoods. The consequence was catastrophic: mass incarceration, with African Americans imprisoned at rates 5–7 times higher than whites, despite similar drug-use rates. Children grew up with incarcerated parents. Communities were hollowed out.
