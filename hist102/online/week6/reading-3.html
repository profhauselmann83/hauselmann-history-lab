Chapter 31  ·  Section 1

The Reagan Revolution

Supply-side economics, the New Right, and the turn against civil rights

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Reagan's political transformation and appeal to diverse constituencies
  • Analyze Reaganomics (supply-side economics) and its actual economic outcomes
  • Describe the rise of the Religious Right and the New Right coalition
  • Trace the War on Drugs and its disproportionate impact on Black Americans

Ronald Reagan, a former Hollywood actor and liberal Democrat who had migrated rightward during the 1960s, won the 1980 presidential election with 51 percent of the popular vote. He assembled a New Right coalition of fiscal conservatives, social conservatives and evangelical Christians, foreign policy hawks, and Southern Democrats alienated by civil rights. Reagan promised to roll back the Great Society, cut taxes and federal spending, increase military spending, and take a hardline against communism.

Reaganomics—based on supply-side economics—promised that cutting taxes would stimulate the economy. Congress cut taxes by 25 percent over three years (1981–1984). Unemployment reached 10 percent in 1982, the highest since the Great Depression. Yet average growth from 1983 to 1989 reached 4.5 percent annually, below historical averages. The national debt tripled, from approximately $1 trillion to $3 trillion, as spending on defense and entitlements exceeded tax revenue. Wealthy Americans saw their share of national wealth increase; the median household income for ordinary Americans remained flat.

Jerry Falwell
1933–2007
Baptist minister who founded the Moral Majority (1979), an organization mobilizing evangelical Christians for political action. Opposed abortion, homosexuality, and secular education. Falwell's ability to deliver evangelical voters to Reagan proved crucial to the New Right coalition's success.

The Religious Right played a crucial role in the New Right coalition. Evangelical Christians, who had largely remained outside politics since the Scopes trial of the 1920s, mobilized in massive numbers. The Moral Majority, founded by Jerry Falwell, claimed three million members and delivered evangelicals to Reagan. The Religious Right opposed abortion, LGBTQ+ rights, and what they viewed as secular indoctrination in public schools. Their political emergence marked a significant realignment: the party of Lincoln, which had supported civil rights, was becoming the party of Southern conservatism.

Reagan's administration cut federal funding for social programs while expanding police powers. The War on Drugs led to mandatory minimum sentencing for drug possession. The disparity was stark: possession of 5 grams of crack cocaine (associated with Black Americans) carried a mandatory five-year sentence, while possession of 500 grams of powder cocaine (associated with White Americans) carried the same sentence. This 100-to-1 sentencing disparity reflected and reinforced racial inequality. Between 1980 and 1990, the U.S. prison population doubled. By 1990, Black Americans constituted 12 percent of the national population but 43 percent of the prison population.

“A rising tide lifts all boats.” — Reagan's defense of supply-side economics, 1981

Chapter 31  ·  Section 2

Cold War's End & Permanent Institutions

Gorbachev, the fall of communism, and the institutionalization of conservatism

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Reagan's Cold War rhetoric and eventual thaw with Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev
  • Describe the rapid collapse of communist regimes and the Soviet Union's dissolution
  • Analyze how conservative think tanks became permanent institutional power centers
  • Evaluate the long-term consequences of conservative institutional dominance

Reagan's rhetoric toward the Soviet Union was initially hardline. He called the USSR the “evil empire” and supported anti-communist rebels in Afghanistan, Nicaragua, and Africa. Yet by the mid-1980s, a new Soviet leader offered an opening. Mikhail Gorbachev, who became Soviet premier in 1985, initiated perestroika (restructuring) and glasnost (openness). Reagan and Gorbachev negotiated arms reductions and a more cooperative relationship. In 1989, the Berlin Wall fell—symbolizing the Cold War's end. The Soviet Union formally dissolved on December 26, 1991.

Yet even as the Cold War ended, conservative institutions that had grown up during it became permanent features of American politics. The Heritage Foundation, the American Enterprise Institute (AEI), and other conservative organizations had built networks of intellectuals, journalists, and policymakers. Fox News, launched in 1996, created a permanent conservative media apparatus. These institutions would outlast Reagan and become pillars of Republican politics for decades.

Mikhail Gorbachev
1931–2022
Soviet premier who initiated perestroika and glasnost, reforms intended to strengthen the Soviet system. Instead, they accelerated the USSR's collapse. His negotiation of arms reduction with Reagan helped ease Cold War tensions.
“The genius of Reagan was not that he ended the Cold War. It's that he enabled conservative institutions to survive and flourish after the Cold War ended.” — Political scientist, reflecting on Reagan's legacy

Week 6  ·  Synthesis

Connections Across the Reading

Reagan, inequality, and the institutionalization of conservatism

The Reagan era reshaped American politics by consolidating conservative power not just through electoral victory but through the creation of permanent institutional structures. Reaganomics promised a rising tide that would lift all boats, yet wealth inequality increased. The War on Drugs targeted drug abuse but devastated Black communities through mandatory sentencing and mass incarceration. The Religious Right brought evangelical Christians into politics, shifting the Republican Party toward social conservatism. And even as the Cold War ended, conservative think tanks and Fox News ensured that conservative ideology would remain dominant in American public discourse.
