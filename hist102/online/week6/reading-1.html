Chapter 28 · Section 1
The Challenges of Peacetime
Demobilization, the GI Bill, and Truman's Fair Deal

After World War II, American servicemen, their families, and policymakers faced an enormous challenge: transitioning twelve million soldiers back to civilian life. The pressure was intense. Servicemen, labeled the "Ohio boys" (Over the Hill in October), threatened to vote Republican if they weren't home by Christmas 1946. Congress responded to popular pressure and cut the military from 12 million to 1.5 million between 1945 and 1947—still more troops than America had ever kept in peacetime.

Millions of women who had worked defense jobs were dismissed as soldiers returned, despite surveys showing 75–90 percent of women workers wanted to keep their jobs. The government and media propaganda encouraged women to "go home," but women continued to make up one-third of the labor force throughout the 1950s. For returning veterans, adjustment was painful. The Army estimated 20 percent of casualties were psychological. Veterans worried about finding work, feared their families had changed, and some struggled with what it meant to resume "normal" life after combat.

The GI Bill and American Opportunity

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act, known as the GI Bill, was one of the most significant pieces of legislation in American history. It gave every honorably discharged veteran a year's unemployment compensation, preventing millions of jobless soldiers from flooding the labor market. More importantly, it paid for college or vocational school tuition and provided a living stipend.

The results were transformative. In 1940, only 5.5 percent of American men had a college degree. By 1950, that number had jumped to 7.3 percent, as more than two million servicemen used the benefit. The numbers kept growing. College enrollment swelled, creating a well-educated, skilled labor force. Upon graduation, these men entered professions that paved the way to middle-class stability. The bill also offered low-interest loans for homes and small businesses, accelerating the post-war economic boom.

Yet not all veterans benefited equally. African American veterans could use their educational benefits only at schools that accepted Black students. Approximately nine thousand servicemen and women dishonorably discharged because they were gay or lesbian received no benefits. Some Mexican American veterans faced denial or delays.

The Fair Deal

Truman wanted to build on Roosevelt's New Deal. His Fair Deal program proposed a federal minimum wage increase, expanded Social Security and public housing, and prohibition of child labor. He retained some wartime price controls but removed others. In his 1949 inaugural address, he called his agenda the "Fair Deal"—a nod to his predecessor's vision.

Importantly, Truman declared that the Fair Deal should include Americans of color. He became the first president to address the NAACP. He established a Presidential Committee on Civil Rights in 1946 and desegregated the armed forces by executive order in 1948—a bold move many objected to on grounds that the military was not the place for "social experimentation."

Congress, dominated by Republicans and conservative Southern Democrats, rejected much of Truman's agenda. The American Medical Association spent $1.5 million to defeat his healthcare proposal, framing it as "socialized medicine" to appeal to Americans' fear of communism. Congress refused to make lynching a federal crime, wouldn't outlaw the poll tax, and rejected making the Fair Employment Practices Committee permanent. But they passed the conservative Taft-Hartley Act, limiting union power—over Truman's veto.

Chapter 28 · Section 2
The Cold War
From Alliance to Confrontation

As World War II ended, the alliance between the United States and Soviet Union began to crumble. Both had fundamentally incompatible visions for Europe's future. Joseph Stalin wanted to dominate Eastern Europe and spread communist revolution globally. The United States wanted to expand its influence through democratic governments and free-market economies worldwide.

Europe and Asia were devastated. Great Britain, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, and China were reduced to shadows. Only the United States and Soviet Union emerged as superpowers. They quickly found themselves locked in a contest for military, economic, social, technological, and ideological supremacy. This struggle—fought through espionage, propaganda, proxy wars, and an arms race—became known as the Cold War. It would define American foreign policy for forty-five years.

Containment Abroad

In February 1946, George Kennan, a State Department official in Moscow, sent an eight-thousand-word telegram to Washington. In what became known as the "Long Telegram," Kennan argued that Soviet leaders believed the only way to protect the USSR was to destroy "rival" nations and their influence. He advised that the best way to stop Soviet expansion was to contain Soviet influence where it already existed and prevent its expansion into new areas. This policy of "containment" formed the basis for U.S. foreign policy for over thirty years.

As communists came to power elsewhere, American policymakers extended containment into what became known as the "domino theory" under Eisenhower: if one nation fell to communism, neighbors would succumb like toppling dominoes. Winston Churchill in 1946 described an "Iron Curtain" dividing free Western Europe from communist Eastern Europe controlled by Stalin.

To implement containment, the National Security Act of 1947 reorganized the government, creating the National Security Council and the CIA for surveillance and espionage. The Department of Defense was established in 1949.

Chapter 28 · Section 2 (Continued)
Containment at Home

In 1949, two events shook American confidence. On August 29, the Soviet Union exploded its first atomic bomb—no longer did America have a monopoly on nuclear power. Months later, communist forces under Mao Zedong triumphed in China. American policymakers and media immediately suspected communist spies in the State Department had sabotaged American interests.

In February 1950, Wisconsin Senator Joseph McCarthy claimed the State Department harbored communists. Klaus Fuchs, a physicist on the Manhattan Project, was imprisoned for passing nuclear secrets to Soviets. This fueled American fears. The most infamous case was Julius and Ethel Rosenberg, executed in 1953 for espionage despite questionable evidence—later discoveries proved Julius had passed information, but Ethel's guilt remained disputed.

Fears of internal communist threats had existed since the 1930s. Truman signed Executive Order 9835 on March 21, 1947, giving the FBI broad powers to investigate federal employees for "security risks." Loyalty boards proliferated. The House Committee on Un-American Activities (HUAC), established in 1938, investigated suspected communists in business, academia, and media—particularly Hollywood. Witnesses were subpoenaed; refusal meant imprisonment. Those invoking the Fifth Amendment or suspected of communist sympathies lost jobs and were blacklisted. Victims included composer Leonard Bernstein, novelist Dashiell Hammett, playwright Lillian Hellman, actor Paul Robeson, and musician Artie Shaw.

A particularly heinous aspect of this hunt was its targeting of gay men and lesbians employed by government. Anti-communists believed gay people were morally weak and thus likely to betray their country. Many also believed gays could be blackmailed by Soviet agents because homosexuality was classified as a mental illness by psychiatrists.
