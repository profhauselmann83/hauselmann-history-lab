Chapter 28  ·  Section 1

28.1 Demobilization & the GI Bill

Twelve million soldiers return home; postwar American prosperity and inequality take shape

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the challenges of demobilizing 12 million troops after WWII
  • Analyze the GI Bill’s impact on opportunity and its role in deepening racial inequality
  • Describe Truman’s Fair Deal and the obstacles it faced in Congress

The defeat of Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan in 1945 marked the end of World War II but the beginning of a far longer struggle. The United States and the Soviet Union, allies during wartime, possessed fundamentally incompatible visions for the postwar world. Both were exhausted by the war, yet both emerged convinced that their ideology represented the only legitimate path forward. As Joseph Stalin, premier of the Soviet Union, tightened his grip on the countries of Eastern Europe, Americans began to fear that communism would spread globally, transforming newly liberated nations into Soviet satellites. At the same time, Soviet leaders viewed American military bases surrounding their border and American support for anti-communist regimes as evidence of Western hostility and imperialism.

The most immediate task after the war was demobilizing the military and reintegrating twelve million soldiers into civilian life. The challenge was unprecedented in American history. Many servicemen, labeled the “Ohio boys” (Over the Hill in October), threatened to vote Republican if they were not home by Christmas 1946. This grassroots political pressure became irresistible for Congress and the White House. President Harry Truman, still inexperienced in national office, felt politically powerless to resist the pressure despite warnings from military leaders like Secretary of the Navy James Forrestal and Secretary of War Robert P. Patterson that rapid demobilization jeopardized national security. The government reduced the military from 12 million in June 1945 to 1.5 million by June 1947—still a substantial peacetime force, but a dramatic reduction.

The “Ohio Boys”
1945–1946
Servicemen who organized the “Bring Troops Home” campaign, using the slogan “Over the Hill in October” (O.H.I.O.). Their grassroots political organizing forced Congress to accelerate demobilization despite military leaders’ warnings about America’s strategic posture during the emerging Cold War.

Readjustment to postwar life was difficult. The Army estimated that 20 percent of casualties were psychological, not physical wounds. Veterans worried about finding work, feared their families had changed, and struggled with what it meant to resume “normal” life after combat. Doctors warned fiancées and mothers that soldiers might return with psychological problems that would make them difficult to live with. Millions of women who had worked defense jobs were dismissed despite surveys showing 75–90 percent wanted to keep their jobs. Government propaganda encouraged women to “go home,” yet women continued to make up roughly one-third of the labor force throughout the 1950s, often in lower-paying positions than they had held during the war.

Before the war ended, Congress passed the GI Bill (Servicemen's Readjustment Act) to ease the transition. Every honorably discharged veteran received a year of unemployment compensation, preventing millions of jobless soldiers from flooding the labor market. More importantly, the bill paid for college or vocational school tuition and provided living stipends. The results were transformative. In 1940, only 5.5 percent of American men had college degrees. By 1950, that jumped to 7.3 percent, as more than two million servicemen used the benefit. These educated veterans entered skilled professions that paved the way to stable middle-class lives. The bill also offered low-interest mortgages for homes and small business loans, accelerating postwar economic growth and the suburban boom.

Yet not all veterans benefited equally. African American veterans could use educational benefits only at colleges that accepted Black students—effectively limiting their choices to a handful of historically Black colleges. Approximately nine thousand servicemen and women dishonorably discharged because they were gay or lesbian received no benefits at all. Some Mexican American veterans faced denial or bureaucratic delays. These inequities demonstrated that even well-intentioned federal programs could not overcome the deeply embedded racial and sexual discrimination baked into American institutions.

“The GI Bill created the modern American middle class—but a whites-only version of it. Federal housing loans were denied to Black veterans through redlining; prestigious universities rejected Black applicants. The wealth gap today is a direct result.” — Historian Thomas Sugrue

28.2 The Cold War Emerges

Truman's Fair Deal, containment strategy, and McCarthyism

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how the U.S.-Soviet alliance fractured after WWII
  • Analyze George Kennan's containment doctrine and the Truman Doctrine
  • Describe the Marshall Plan and Berlin Blockade as implementations of containment
  • Trace the domestic consequences of the Cold War: McCarthyism and loyalty boards

Truman wanted to build on Roosevelt's New Deal legacy. His Fair Deal program proposed a federal minimum wage increase, expanded Social Security and public housing, and prohibition of child labor. Importantly, Truman declared that the Fair Deal should include Americans of color. He became the first president to address the NAACP and desegregated the armed forces by executive order in 1948. Congress, however, dominated by Republicans and conservative Southern Democrats, rejected much of Truman's agenda. They refused to make lynching a federal crime, wouldn't outlaw the poll tax, and rejected making the Fair Employment Practices Committee permanent. They did pass the conservative Taft-Hartley Act, limiting union power, over Truman's veto.

As World War II drew to a close, the alliance between the United States and Soviet Union began to crumble. Both sides realized their visions for Europe and the world were incompatible. Stalin wished to establish communist governments in Eastern Europe; the United States wanted to install democratic governments open to American trade. The end of the war left Europe and Asia devastated. Only the United States and Soviet Union emerged as superpowers, quickly locked in a contest for military, economic, and ideological supremacy. This struggle—fought through espionage, propaganda, proxy wars, and an arms race—became known as the Cold War.

In February 1946, George Kennan sent an eight-thousand-word message known as the Long Telegram to Washington. He maintained that Soviet leaders believed the only way to protect the Soviet Union was to destroy rival nations. He advised that the best strategy was containment—preventing Soviet expansion into new areas without direct military confrontation. This policy formed the basis for American foreign policy for over thirty years.

As communist governments came to power elsewhere, American policymakers extended containment into the domino theory: neighbors to communist nations would inevitably succumb. Winston Churchill articulated this fear in March 1946 when he referred to an Iron Curtain dividing free Western Europe from communist Eastern Europe.

In March 1947, the United States announced the Truman Doctrine, which offered military assistance to Greece and Turkey to resist communism. Eventually expanded to any state resisting communist takeover, this doctrine became a hallmark of Cold War policy. By 1946, Western Europe was in crisis. Communism was gaining ground in France and Italy. Truman and Secretary of State George Marshall proposed the Marshall Plan, which gave $13 billion in economic aid to European nations from 1948 to 1951. The plan stipulated that European nations had to work together and spend money on American goods, boosting American exports and cultural influence.

The Soviets responded aggressively. In June 1948, Stalin ordered all land and water routes to West Berlin cut off, hoping to starve the western city into submission. The Berlin Blockade tested containment policy. The United States and Britain responded by flying supplies to West Berlin for over a year, delivering approximately two million tons of supplies. On May 12, 1949, the Soviets admitted defeat and ended the blockade. Germany remained divided: West Germany in the west, East Germany in the east, a division lasting forty years.

George Kennan
1904–1985
American diplomat whose "Long Telegram" outlined the strategy of containment. Later regretted that his nuanced arguments about economic containment were oversimplified into a militarized Cold War policy. Spent decades arguing the Cold War had gone too far.

In 1949, two events severely disrupted American confidence. The Soviet Union exploded its first atomic bomb on August 29—the U.S. no longer had a nuclear monopoly. Months later, communist forces under Mao Zedong triumphed in China. American policymakers suspected communist spies in the State Department had sabotaged American interests. In response, Truman signed Executive Order 9835, which gave the FBI broad powers to investigate federal employees for security risks. State and municipal governments created loyalty boards to find and dismiss potentially disloyal workers.

In February 1950, Wisconsin Senator Joseph McCarthy claimed the State Department harbored communists and announced he had a list of 205 names. The accusations were largely false, but they created national panic. The House Committee on Un-American Activities (HUAC), established in 1938, intensified investigations into suspected communists in business, academia, and the media—particularly Hollywood. Witnesses were subpoenaed; refusal meant imprisonment. Those invoking the Fifth Amendment or suspected of communist sympathies lost jobs and were blacklisted. A particularly heinous aspect of this hunt was targeting gay men and lesbians employed by government. Anti-communists believed gay people were morally weak and thus likely to betray their country. Many believed gays could be blackmailed by Soviet agents because homosexuality was classified as a mental illness. Thousands of gay federal employees were fired.

“The McCarthy era taught Americans to fear not just communism but dissent itself. To disagree with official policy could get you fired, blacklisted, or imprisoned. That chilling effect on free speech lasted for years.” — Historian Ellen Schrecker
Reading Notes — Cold War Strategy & McCarthyism
The Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan, and Berlin Blockade all illustrate containment, yet they use different methods. The Doctrine offered military aid; the Marshall Plan offered economic aid; the airlift demonstrated American resolve. What does the variety of containment strategies suggest about American policymakers' understanding of communism's appeal? And how did Cold War anxieties lead to McCarthyism and the persecution of gay Americans?

Week 6  ·  Synthesis

Connections Across the Reading

Demobilization, prosperity, fear, and the architecture of the Cold War

The 1945–1950 period was one of profound contradiction. Economic prosperity was real for many, but prosperity was built on military spending and excluded most Americans of color. The GI Bill created opportunity, but a whites-only version. Containment policy was shaped by genuine concern about Soviet expansion, but it became a tool for expanding American military and intelligence apparatus. Loyalty boards claimed to protect America from communists, but they created a climate of fear that chilled free speech and destroyed innocent lives.

Understanding this reading means holding all of these contradictions at once. Demobilization was politically necessary but strategically worrying. The Fair Deal promised social progress but faced a conservative Congress. Containment strategy was intellectually sophisticated (Kennan's nuanced argument) but became a blunt instrument (military bases worldwide, arms race). The Cold War abroad created the Cold War at home—McCarthyism, loyalty boards, the persecution of gay Americans.
