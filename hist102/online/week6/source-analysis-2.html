Overarching Question

Was President Nixon involved in the Watergate break-in and cover-up?

On June 17, 1972, five men were caught breaking into the Democratic National Committee offices in the Watergate complex in Washington D.C. This event sparked investigations that ultimately led to President Nixon's resignation in 1974. Below are four primary sources: Nixon's own defense, testimony from his legal advisor John Dean, testimony from his chief of staff H.R. Haldeman, and the secret White House tapes themselves. Your task is to evaluate the evidence and judge reliability.

Document A: President Nixon's Speech (April 30, 1973)

Document A
Address to the Nation on Watergate
President Richard Nixon, addressing the American public and announcing his response to Watergate allegations, April 30, 1973.
"I want to talk to you tonight from my heart on a subject of deep concern to every American... I have accepted the resignations of two of my staff members... I can say categorically that his investigation indicates that no one on the White House staff, no one in this administration presently employed, was involved in this very bizarre incident... What I want to do in this entire matter is see that the truth is fully brought out... I had no prior knowledge of the Watergate break-in... I first learned of it from the newspapers, as you did."

Document B: John Dean Testimony (June 25, 1973)

Document B
Senate Watergate Committee Testimony
John Dean, White House Counsel under President Nixon, testifying before the Senate Watergate Committee. Dean is under oath and liable to perjury charges.
"I have felt compelled to come forward and tell the truth. I was made aware of the break-in at the Democratic National Committee on June 17, 1972... Mr. Haldeman told me the President was pleased with the results of the break-in... The President said the whole thing was not as bad as it could have been... In a conversation on March 21, 1973, the President asked me how much it would cost to keep the defendants quiet... The President discussed the use of Executive clemency to get the defendants off the hook... I did not think the President was joking."

Document C: H.R. Haldeman Testimony (August 1, 1973)

Document C
Senate Watergate Committee Testimony
H.R. Haldeman, White House Chief of Staff under President Nixon, testifying before the Senate Watergate Committee. Haldeman is under oath.
"I flatly deny that the President was ever told about raising money for the defendants... The President's position was that the facts must come out, whatever they were... If such a conversation occurred... it would have been hypothetical... The President never said 'get the money and do whatever you have to do'... I have the clearest possible recollection that the President said we should not do anything that would be wrong."

Document D: The White House Tapes (June 23, 1972 & March 21, 1973)

Document D
Secret Audio Recordings
Transcripts from secret recordings made in the Oval Office. Nixon had been recording his conversations to create a historical record. These became crucial evidence in the Watergate investigation.
June 23, 1972 (6 days after break-in):
Haldeman: "The way you've got it set up now, if they don't cooperate... the CIA would have to take the 5th Amendment or claim that the whole thing is a CIA asset."
Nixon: "That's right."

March 21, 1973:
Dean: "We have a cancer close to the Presidency that is growing."
Nixon: "How much do we need?"
Dean: "I would say that these people are going to cost a million dollars over the next... two years."
Nixon: "We could get that... on a cash basis, uh, I mean we could get that."

Historical Outcome

Nixon resigned on August 9, 1974, facing certain impeachment. The White House tapes proved that he had obstructed justice by attempting to block the FBI investigation and by discussing payment to silence the burglars. The Watergate scandal shattered public trust in government, contributed to the impeachment process, and led to major reforms in campaign finance and executive oversight.
