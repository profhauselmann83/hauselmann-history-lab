The Cold War and Beyond — A Historical Overview

~45 min Optional transcript available

What You'll Learn

This lecture contextualizes the week's readings by tracing the arc of American foreign and domestic policy from 1945 to the 1990s. You'll explore how the Truman administration's policy of containment shaped Cold War strategy, how internal security measures (loyalty boards, McCarthyism) created a climate of fear, and how the Vietnam War fractured American confidence in government.

The lecture then examines the cultural and political reaction: the counterculture, the civil rights and gay rights movements, the Watergate scandal, and finally the conservative backlash of the 1980s. Throughout, we ask: what assumptions guided policy? When those assumptions failed, how did Americans redefine national purpose?

Pay special attention to how Cold War thinking shaped both foreign policy (Korea, Vietnam, Nixon's opening to China) and domestic policy (race relations, law-and-order politics, the War on Drugs). The lecture emphasizes that Cold War rivalry was not inevitable—it emerged from choices made by both American and Soviet leaders, based on assumptions about each other's intentions.

Key Themes

Containment & Domino Theory

How did policymakers believe they could limit Soviet expansion? What assumptions underlay the domino theory? And what happened when those assumptions proved wrong in Vietnam?

Cold War at Home

How did fear of communism reshape American institutions? What role did loyalty boards, HUAC, and McCarthyism play in creating a security state?

The Sixties Unraveling

How did Vietnam divide the nation? What did the civil rights and counterculture movements reveal about America's self-understanding?

Conservative Reaction

Why did conservatism gain political power in the 1980s? What was the Heritage Foundation's role in shaping conservative strategy?
