The Progressive Era and Reform

Empire abroad, reform at home, and the long struggle for equality

OpenStax Ch. 19 & 21 150 pts total Mar 23–29

Learning Objectives

  • Explain U.S. imperial expansion through Hawaii and the Spanish-American War, and evaluate the debate it triggered at home
  • Describe the muckraking tradition and how investigative journalism drove legislative reform
  • Analyze Theodore Roosevelt's Square Deal and how it redefined the federal government's role in the economy
  • Trace the women's suffrage movement from the Seneca Falls Convention to the Nineteenth Amendment
  • Compare Woodrow Wilson's New Freedom with Roosevelt's New Nationalism
  • Evaluate the impact of Jacob Riis's photography and writing on public consciousness and policy
  • Compare Booker T. Washington's philosophy of accommodation with W.E.B. Du Bois's demand for full civil equality
  • Explain the founding and significance of the NAACP and the Urban League

Central Question

The Progressive Era is often celebrated as an age of democratic reform — but who was included in "the people" that reformers sought to empower, and who was left out? How do we evaluate a reform movement that expanded democracy for some Americans while actively sustaining white supremacy for others?

Keep this tension in mind as you read. The same era that produced women's suffrage and antitrust law also produced the nadir of American race relations.
