Background & Historical Context
In 1898, the United States formally annexed Hawaii — but were Hawaiians in favor of this? The two newspaper articles below give very different answers. As you read, think carefully about who published each article, who their audience was, and what stake they had in the outcome of annexation. Your goal is not just to summarize what each source says, but to evaluate why they say it.

Annexation of Hawaii — Timeline

  • Jan. 1891Liliʻuokalani became Queen of Hawaii after her brother King Kalākaua died.
  • 1892Queen Liliʻuokalani worked to revise the constitution: she wanted to restore voting rights to native Hawaiians, remove voting rights from non-Hawaiians, and return legislative power to the monarch.
  • Jan. 1893The Hawaiian League led a coup d'état, overthrowing Queen Liliʻuokalani. American businessmen and a U.S. government official immediately recognized the new provisional government.
  • Mar. 1893President Grover Cleveland refused to recognize the provisional government, declared the coup illegal, but left any decision about restoring the Queen to Congress — which took no action.
  • Mar. 1897President William McKinley, who supported annexation, took office. He backed an annexation treaty, but it failed to win the required two-thirds Senate vote.
  • Sep. 1897The Hui Aloha 'Āina held a mass meeting to protest annexation and launched a petition drive against it.
  • Nov. 1897–Feb. 1898Hui Aloha 'Āina representatives and Queen Liliʻuokalani traveled to Washington, D.C. to present the anti-annexation petition to U.S. senators.
  • Feb. 1898The Spanish-American War began. Several senators changed their votes in favor of annexation, wanting to secure a Pacific naval base.
  • Jul. 1898A joint resolution annexing Hawaii passed Congress and was signed by McKinley. (A joint resolution required only a simple majority, unlike the two-thirds needed for a treaty.)
  • Aug. 12, 1898"Annexation Day": the Hawaiian flag was lowered and the American flag raised at the palace in Honolulu.
Document A The Pacific Commercial Advertiser — "Annexation!" (1898)
Publication: The Pacific Commercial Advertiser Date: July 14, 1898 Audience: English-language; primarily the white business community of Honolulu Stance: Pro-annexation
And the star-spangled banner
In triumph shall wave,
O'er the Isles of Hawaii
And the homes of the brave.
— H. M. Whitney

Honolulu, H.I., U.S.A., July 13, 1898, 3:30 p.m. — The Pacific Mail S.S. Coptic signals from off Waikiki that these Islands have been annexed to the United States. Flags are being hoisted everywhere. Thousands flocked to the water front. There are great crowds on the streets evidencing the very delirium of joy.

VOTE AT WASHINGTON. Washington, July 6 — The resolutions which make Hawaii a part of the United States were passed by the Senate this afternoon. [The] will of the people, expressed in the vote of their representatives by 42 to 21, declared that Hawaii must be a part of the Union of States. When the Vice-President announced the vote there was applause from floor and gallery.

Source: "Annexation!" (1898, July 14). The Pacific Commercial Advertiser.

Document B The Independent — "The Memorial" (1897)
Publication: The Independent (English-language, Honolulu) Date: October 16, 1897 Audience: English-language Hawaiian readership; opposed to annexation Document type: Reprint of a resolution adopted at a mass meeting of Hawaiian citizens

To the President, the Congress and the People of the United States of America. This Memorial respectfully represents as follows:

1 — That your memorialists are residents of the Hawaiian Islands; that the majority of them are aboriginal Hawaiians…

3 — That the Government of the Republic of Hawaii has no warrant for its existence in the support of the people of these Islands; that it was instituted without considering the rights and wishes of a great majority of the residents; and that it maintains itself solely by force of arms.

4 — That its Constitution was adopted by a convention, a majority of whose members were self-appointed, and the balance of whose members were elected by a numerically insignificant minority of the white and aboriginal male citizens of these Islands.

7 — That your memorialists have learned with grief and dismay that the President of the United States has submitted for ratification a treaty which proposes to annex our territory to the United States.

9 — That your memorialists humbly but fervently protest against this; and repeat that the consent of the people of the Hawaiian Islands has never been asked.

10 — That Annexation would subvert the rights of the Hawaiian people, and would negate the principles proclaimed in the Declaration of American Independence.

12 — Your memorialists humbly pray that no further steps be taken until the Hawaiian people have the opportunity to express at the ballot box, their wishes as to whether to accept or reject Annexation.

Source: "The Memorial." (1897, October 16). The Independent.
