Chapter 19  ·  Section 1

How the Other Half Lives: Jacob Riis and the Photography of Poverty

When images became arguments — documentary photography and the reform imagination

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how Jacob Riis used photography and journalism to document and publicize urban poverty
  • Describe the conditions of life in New York City tenements in the 1880s and 1890s
  • Analyze the rhetorical strategies Riis used and evaluate the strengths and limitations of his approach
Jacob Riis
1849 – 1914
Danish-born journalist and photographer who arrived in New York as a penniless immigrant in 1870. After years of poverty and near-starvation, he became a police reporter for the New York Tribune. His 1890 book How the Other Half Lives shocked middle-class Americans and inspired a generation of reformers, including the young Theodore Roosevelt.

By 1890, New York City's Lower East Side was the most densely populated place on earth. In some blocks, more than a thousand people were packed into tenement buildings designed for a fraction of that number — buildings with no running water, no indoor plumbing, no ventilation, and no fire escapes. Whole families of six, eight, or ten lived in single rooms barely larger than a modern bathroom. Children slept on fire escapes in summer and on floors in winter. Tuberculosis, typhoid, cholera, and diphtheria moved through these blocks with terrifying speed. In 1870, one in every five children born in New York City died before their first birthday.

Jacob Riis understood that words alone could not convey what he had seen. A Danish immigrant who had himself been homeless and near-starvation in New York in the 1870s, Riis had become a police reporter for the New York Tribune, covering the tenement districts nightly. In 1887, he began using the newly developed flash photography technique — burning magnesium powder that produced a blinding flash of light — to photograph tenement interiors at night, without warning or preparation, capturing the reality of tenement life rather than its presentation for an audience.

The result was How the Other Half Lives (1890), a book of photographs and text that introduced middle-class Americans to a world they had never seen. Riis documented the Five Points neighborhood, the Bend, the Mulberry Street tenements, and the child labor gangs in ruthless detail. He photographed a family of seven in a single airless room, children making artificial flowers in a cellar at midnight, men sleeping on the floor of a flophouse for seven cents a night. The book caused a sensation. Theodore Roosevelt, then a New York City Police Commissioner, tracked Riis to his office: "I have read your book and I have come to help," he told him. The two became lifelong friends and allies.

The Limits of Riis's Vision

Riis's work was pioneering, but it was not without significant limitations that historians have examined carefully. His approach was paternalistic: he documented poverty from above, presenting the poor as objects of middle-class sympathy and reform rather than as subjects with their own agency and voice. His text was laced with ethnic stereotypes that reflected the nativist attitudes of his era — he described Chinese immigrants, Eastern European Jews, and Italian immigrants through caricature, playing on his readers' prejudices even while enlisting their sympathy. His Black subjects received particularly little empathy. And his proposed solutions were ultimately reformist rather than structural: better tenements, better policing, more charities — not challenges to the economic system that produced the poverty he photographed.

Nevertheless, Riis's impact was undeniable. His work contributed directly to the demolition of Mulberry Bend (the city's most notorious block) in 1895, the installation of new parks and playgrounds in tenement districts, and the passage of the Tenement House Act of 1901, which required running water, indoor toilets, and windows in every room of new tenement construction. More broadly, he helped establish the principle that the conditions of the urban poor were a public problem requiring public solutions — a principle that would undergird Progressive reform for the next two decades.

"The boundary line of the Other Half lies through the tenements. The man who has never been inside one of these [tenements] can have no adequate idea of what they are like." — Jacob Riis, How the Other Half Lives, 1890

The Urban Crisis: Immigration, Labor, and the Progressive Response

From tenements to settlement houses — how reformers reimagined urban America

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the conditions faced by immigrants in American industrial cities at the turn of the century
  • Explain how the settlement house movement developed as a response to urban poverty
  • Analyze the role of women reformers in building the institutional infrastructure of Progressivism

Between 1880 and 1920, more than twenty million immigrants arrived in the United States — the great majority from Southern and Eastern Europe. Italians, Poles, Russians, Austro-Hungarians, and Eastern European Jews poured into industrial cities. Unlike the earlier waves of German and Scandinavian immigrants, who had moved toward the agricultural Midwest, the new immigrants overwhelmingly settled in urban industrial centers: New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Detroit. They took the worst jobs, lived in the worst housing, and faced systematic discrimination from native-born Americans who regarded them as racially inferior, criminally inclined, and culturally dangerous.

In the steel mills of Pittsburgh, twelve-hour days and seven-day weeks were standard. Workers in textile mills, garment factories, and meatpacking plants earned wages that could not sustain a family above the poverty line even with every family member working. The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire of March 1911 — which killed 146 garment workers, mostly young immigrant women, because the owners had locked the exits — was not an aberration. It was the visible tip of a vast iceberg of industrial death and injury that killed an estimated 35,000 American workers per year at the century's turn.

Hull House and the Settlement House Movement

Jane Addams
1860 – 1935
Co-founded Hull House in Chicago in 1889. Her residents included Florence Kelley, who drove child labor reform; Julia Lathrop, the first head of the Children's Bureau; and Grace Abbott, who led the Immigrants' Protective League. Hull House was simultaneously a social service agency, a research institute, and a political lobby.

The settlement house movement offered a different model of reform than either charity or muckraking. Where charity organizations typically provided aid at arm's length, settlement house workers actually moved into poor neighborhoods, living alongside the communities they sought to serve. Jane Addams and Ellen Gates Starr founded Hull House in a formerly wealthy Chicago neighborhood in 1889. Within a decade it was a miniature city of thirteen buildings, serving two thousand people per week through dozens of programs: a nursery, kindergarten, night school, job training classes, clubs for youth and adults, a gym, a theater, a public kitchen, and a labor museum.

Hull House was also a research institute and a political lobbying operation. Florence Kelley, who lived at Hull House from 1891, conducted systematic surveys of child labor and sweatshop conditions in Chicago that provided the empirical foundation for Illinois's 1893 child labor law. Julia Lathrop documented conditions in state institutions for the mentally ill and helped establish the nation's first juvenile court. The Children's Bureau, created by Congress in 1912 as the first federal agency devoted to child welfare, was directly inspired by Hull House's research and advocacy. Its first director, Julia Lathrop, was a Hull House alumna.

The settlement houses were also, deliberately or not, training grounds for Progressive reform leadership. An extraordinary proportion of the women who led federal and state reform agencies in the 1910s and 1920s had passed through Hull House or one of its sister institutions. They shared a methodology: systematic documentation of social problems, translation of that documentation into legislative proposals, and sustained lobbying to enact them. This was a new model of political action — empirical, institutional, and gender-integrated in a way that conventional politics was not.

Chapter 21  ·  Section 1

The Nadir: Jim Crow, Disenfranchisement, and the Hardening of White Supremacy

Why the Progressive Era was simultaneously the worst period for African Americans since slavery

Learning Objectives

  • Define Jim Crow laws and explain their mechanisms and purposes
  • Describe how the Supreme Court's Plessy v. Ferguson decision codified segregation
  • Explain why historians call this period the "nadir" of American race relations

The Supreme Court's decision in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) was the constitutional cornerstone of the Jim Crow system. Homer Plessy, who was seven-eighths white and could pass for white, had deliberately boarded a whites-only train car in Louisiana to test the state's Separate Car Act. The Supreme Court ruled, 7-1, that "separate but equal" accommodations did not violate the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause. Justice John Marshall Harlan, the sole dissenter, wrote prophetically: "Our Constitution is color-blind and neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens." The Court would not overturn Plessy for fifty-eight years.

The Jim Crow laws that followed Plessy covered virtually every aspect of public life. Separate schools (invariably with far fewer resources), separate railroad cars, separate waiting rooms, separate water fountains, separate hospitals, separate prisons, separate cemeteries. The system was enforced not only by law but by the constant threat of violence. Lynching — the public murder of Black people, usually by hanging or burning, often as a public spectacle attended by hundreds — reached its peak in the 1890s and early 1900s. Between 1877 and 1950, more than 4,000 Black Americans were lynched, the great majority in the South.

Disenfranchisement completed the system. Southern states used a sophisticated toolkit: literacy tests (applied selectively by white registrars), poll taxes (unaffordable for most Black sharecroppers), white-only primary elections (since winning the Democratic primary was tantamount to winning the general election in the South), and grandfather clauses (exempting from voting requirements anyone whose grandfather had voted — i.e., all white men). In Mississippi, Black voter registration fell from 190,000 in 1890 to fewer than 1,500 by 1900. The effect was complete political exclusion: not a single African American served in Congress from 1901 to 1929.

Reading Notes — Jim Crow

Ferguson decision, the structure of disenfranchisement, and the practice of lynching. Then consider: how does this history complicate or challenge the standard narrative of the Progressive Era as a period of democratic reform and expansion?

Booker T. Washington and the Politics of Accommodation

Industrial education, self-help, and the strategic acceptance of second-class citizenship

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Booker T. Washington's philosophy of racial advancement through industrial education and economic self-sufficiency
  • Analyze the political strategy behind the "Atlanta Compromise" speech
  • Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of Washington's accommodationist approach
Booker T. Washington
1856 – 1915
Born enslaved in Virginia. Founder and president of Tuskegee Institute in Alabama. The most politically powerful African American of his era — TR dined with him at the White House in 1901, triggering a firestorm of Southern protest. He secretly funded civil rights litigation while publicly preaching accommodation.

Born into slavery in Virginia in 1856, Booker T. Washington rose through determination and strategic brilliance to become the most influential African American in the country by 1900. As the founder and president of the Tuskegee Normal and Industrial Institute in Alabama — which he built from virtually nothing into a nationally recognized institution — he articulated a philosophy of racial advancement built on three pillars: industrial education, economic self-sufficiency, and the strategic deferral of demands for political and civil equality.

Washington's defining statement came in his speech at the Atlanta Cotton States and International Exposition in September 1895 — immediately dubbed the Atlanta Compromise. Addressing an audience that included both Black and white Southerners, Washington offered a bargain: African Americans would accept segregation and the effective denial of political rights, focus on vocational and industrial training rather than higher education, and build economic self-sufficiency from within the constraints of the Jim Crow system. In exchange, white Southerners and Northern industrialists would provide the funding, employment, and tolerance necessary for Black economic advancement. His metaphor was direct: "In all things that are purely social we can be as separate as the fingers, yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress."

The speech made Washington the de facto national spokesperson for African Americans, at least in the eyes of white America. Presidents McKinley and Roosevelt consulted with him on federal appointments. Northern philanthropists — Carnegie, Rockefeller, and others — showered Tuskegee with donations. Washington used this influence to build what historians call the Tuskegee Machine: a network of patronage, press outlets, and political connections that he used to reward allies and marginalize critics within the Black community.

Washington's private behavior did not always match his public philosophy. Historians have documented that he secretly funded civil rights lawsuits challenging grandfather clauses and railroad segregation laws, and that he used his connections to lobby quietly against the most egregious forms of Jim Crow. This gap between public accommodation and private activism suggests that Washington was a more complex and conflicted figure than either his admirers or his critics have acknowledged — a man operating within the most constrained possible political environment, making strategic calculations that he could not afford to reveal publicly.

"In all things that are purely social we can be as separate as the fingers, yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress." — Booker T. Washington, Atlanta Exposition Address, September 18, 1895

Chapter 21  ·  Section 3

W.E.B. Du Bois and the Demand for Full Equality

The Talented Tenth, the color line, and the case against accommodation

Learning Objectives

  • Explain W.E.B. Du Bois's critique of Washington's accommodationist philosophy
  • Define the concept of "double consciousness" and explain its significance
  • Describe the Niagara Movement and its role in the founding of the NAACP
W.E.B. Du Bois
1868 – 1963
First African American to earn a Ph.D. from Harvard (1895). Taught at Atlanta University, where he pioneered the sociological study of Black American life. Co-founder of the NAACP in 1909. His The Souls of Black Folk (1903) remains one of the most influential works of American social thought.

William Edward Burghardt Du Bois was born in Great Barrington, Massachusetts in 1868 — three years after the Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery — and educated in an integrated school system. He graduated from Fisk University, attended the University of Berlin, and in 1895 became the first African American to earn a doctoral degree from Harvard. He was, in other words, almost precisely the kind of person that Washington's philosophy of industrial education was designed to prevent: an intellectually ambitious Black man who had been educated in exactly the classical and professional tradition that Tuskegee deliberately did not offer.

Du Bois's most celebrated contribution to American thought was the concept of double consciousness, introduced in The Souls of Black Folk (1903): "It is a peculiar sensation, this double-consciousness, this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others, of measuring one's soul by the tape of a world that looks on in amused contempt and pity." To be Black in America was to experience one's identity through two irreconcilable frameworks simultaneously — the framework of American national identity and the framework of racial exclusion. This psychological burden, Du Bois argued, was itself a form of oppression, not merely its consequence.

The Souls of Black Folk also contained a direct and systematic critique of Washington. Du Bois argued that Washington's program had three fatal flaws. First, it asked Black Americans to accept second-class citizenship permanently rather than temporarily, effectively validating the Jim Crow system it claimed only to be accommodating. Second, by deprecating higher education and political rights, it stripped Black Americans of the very tools — professional leadership, political voice, intellectual culture — they most needed to advance. Third, it was based on a false premise: that economic success was possible without political rights, that a community could thrive commercially while its members could be lynched with impunity, its votes suppressed, and its children excluded from equal schools.

In opposition to Washington's "Talented Tenth" formulation — Washington's term, actually, later adopted by Du Bois — Du Bois argued that Black advancement required the cultivation of a professional and intellectual elite who could provide leadership, advocacy, and role models for the broader community. This was not elitism but strategy: without doctors, lawyers, teachers, ministers, and intellectuals, a community could not sustain the institutions it needed to survive and advance. The goal was not to produce a small privileged class but to build the institutional infrastructure of a free people.

Then explain the concept of "double consciousness" in your own words, and explain why Du Bois believed it was important to name and theorize this experience rather than simply describe it. What did he mean when he said that double consciousness was itself a form of oppression, not merely its consequence?

The NAACP, the Great Migration, and the Limits of Progressive Reform

Building institutions for the long struggle — and asking who the Progressive Era was really for

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the founding of the NAACP and identify its founding principles and strategies
  • Describe the Great Migration and explain its causes and consequences
  • Synthesize the week's readings by evaluating the Progressive Era from the perspective of African Americans

The immediate catalyst for the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People was the Springfield, Illinois race riot of August 1908, in which a white mob attacked the Black community of Abraham Lincoln's hometown, killing at least seven people, destroying forty homes, and driving hundreds of Black residents out of the city permanently. The riot — in the North, in Lincoln's city — shocked white liberal opinion in a way that Southern atrocities had not. Journalist William English Walling issued a call: "Either the spirit of the abolitionists must be revived and we must come to treat the Negro on a plane of absolute political and social equality, or [the Negro] will be [utterly] exterminated."

Ida B. Wells-Barnett
1862 – 1931
Born into slavery in Mississippi. As a journalist, conducted the first systematic investigation of lynching in the United States, documenting that the vast majority of lynching victims had not been accused of any crime. Co-founder of the NAACP, though her militancy made her a difficult partner for the more cautious founders. A founding figure of American civil rights journalism.

In February 1909 — the centennial of Lincoln's birth — a group of sixty founders, Black and white, signed the call that created the NAACP. The founding members included Du Bois, anti-lynching crusader Ida B. Wells-Barnett, Mary Church Terrell, and white progressive allies including Oswald Garrison Villard (grandson of abolitionist William Lloyd Garrison) and Moorfield Storey. The organization's strategy combined legal challenges to racial discrimination with a publicity campaign — Du Bois edited its magazine, The Crisis, which reached 100,000 subscribers by 1918 — to build public awareness and political pressure.

The NAACP chose the courts as its primary battlefield. Moorfield Storey, its first president and one of the country's leading constitutional lawyers, argued that Jim Crow laws could be challenged on Fourteenth Amendment grounds despite Plessy. Early victories came quickly: Guinn v. United States (1915) struck down grandfather clauses, and Buchanan v. Warley (1917) invalidated racial residential zoning laws. The organization's patient, litigation-centered strategy would eventually produce Brown v. Board of Education in 1954 — but that was forty-five years and a world war away.

The Great Migration

Ultimately, millions of African Americans voted with their feet. The Great Migration began around 1910 and would eventually move six million African Americans from the rural South to Northern and Western cities. The immediate causes were the combination of Southern terror and Northern industrial opportunity: the wartime labor shortage created by World War I opened factory jobs previously closed to Black workers, and labor recruiters from Northern industries moved through the South, distributing copies of the Chicago Defender (the leading Black newspaper, which called migration "the Great Northern Drive") and offering train tickets.
