About the Speaker

Daniel Immerwahr — How to Hide an Empire: A History of the Greater United States

Daniel Immerwahr is a professor of history at Northwestern University and the author of How to Hide an Empire (2019). In this talk, recorded at the Newberry Library in Chicago, Immerwahr uses one of the most famous events in American history — Pearl Harbor — as a door into a story that is almost never told. On December 7th, 1941, Japan attacked not just Hawaii but also Wake Island, Guam, and the Philippines. The Philippines attack was rated by the U.S. Army's own official history as militarily equal to Pearl Harbor. Yet FDR edited the Philippines out of his "Day of Infamy" speech — and out of American memory. This talk asks why, and what it costs us not to know.

Questions to Hold as You Watch

These are not graded. They are designed to focus your attention as you watch. Keep them in mind — they will come up again in your readings and in the Canvas discussion this week.

Opening — ~0:00–5:00

Immerwahr opens with Pearl Harbor — one of the most famous dates in American history. What do you already know about December 7th, 1941? And what does it mean that most Americans can name the day but not necessarily what else happened that same day?

What Japan Actually Attacked — ~2:00–9:00

Within hours of Pearl Harbor, Japan also struck Wake Island, Guam, and the Philippines — all U.S. territories. The Philippines attack was rated by the U.S. Army as militarily equal to Pearl Harbor. Why do you think the Philippines has been almost entirely erased from the standard American memory of that day?

FDR's Edits — ~6:00–12:00

Immerwahr shows us FDR's draft of the "Day of Infamy" speech, including a crossed-out line that originally mentioned both Hawaii and the Philippines. FDR deliberately edited the Philippines out. What does it tell us about American democracy that a president had to strategically decide which of his country's people to mention in a speech about an attack on his country?

The Logo Map — ~15:00–21:00

Immerwahr introduces the "logo map" — the familiar lower-48 shape that most Americans think of when they picture the United States. By 1940, nearly 1 in 8 people living under U.S. jurisdiction lived in an overseas territory. What is the effect of a map that leaves out millions of a country's own people? What else might it leave out — and why?

Europe First — ~24:00–31:00

As Japan invaded the Philippines, U.S. military strategy explicitly prioritized Europe. Philippine president Manuel Quezon said: "How typical of America — to writhe in anguish at the fate of a distant cousin while a daughter is being raped in the back room." Secretary of War Stimson responded to Churchill's question about the cost of this strategy with: "There are times when men have to die." Whose lives were being counted in that calculation — and whose weren't?

The Retaking of Manila — ~31:00–38:00

When the U.S. military returned to liberate Manila in 1944, it switched tactics to shelling any building that might contain the enemy. The commanding general said: "The loss of a single American life to save a building was unthinkable." An estimated 100,000 Filipino civilians died in a single month. Who counted as "American" in that sentence? What does this episode reveal about how empire decides whose lives matter?

The GI and Oscar — ~38:00–40:00

Immerwahr ends with a small, almost comic exchange: a U.S. soldier arriving in the Philippines who didn't know the Philippines was part of the United States — even though he'd been sent there to fight for it. A Filipino man named Oscar had to explain to him: "When you colonized us, you established English as the language of instruction. That's why I speak English." What does it mean that American soldiers could be sent to fight for a place their country owned without knowing they owned it?

The Big Question — for your readings this week

Immerwahr says he wants us to see U.S. history "not through the GI's eyes, but through Oscar's." What would change about the history you've studied so far in this course — Reconstruction, the Gilded Age — if you put the territories, and the people in them, back on the map? Whose story has been left out, and what do we lose by leaving it out?
