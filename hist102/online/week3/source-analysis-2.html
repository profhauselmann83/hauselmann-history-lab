Background & Historical Context
In the late 19th century, Jacob Riis used a new photographic technology — the magnesium flash — to document life in New York City's tenements. He published his photographs and descriptions in How the Other Half Lives (1890), which shocked middle-class America and helped drive housing reform. But Riis also wrote about his subjects in ways that many historians now consider condescending, stereotyping, and even racist. As you work through these sources, ask: what do they reveal about tenement conditions, and what do they reveal about Riis himself?

A Historian's Framework: Evaluating Photographs as Evidence

When historians evaluate whether a photograph is useful as historical evidence, they ask these questions:

When & where?Date, location, and historical context of the image
Who took it?Photographer's perspective, purpose, and potential biases
Why was it taken?Intended audience and purpose — reform, journalism, art, commerce?
Circumstances?Did subjects know they were being photographed? Was the scene staged?
Technology?What equipment was used, and how did it shape what could be captured?
What's missing?What isn't shown? Who chose the frame?
Document A Five Cents a Spot — Unauthorized Immigration Lodgings in a Bayard Street Tenement (1890)
Location: New York City Date: 1890 Type: Photograph Photographer: Jacob Riis

Riis took this photograph in a dark, windowless tenement using his magnesium flash. The men and women in the photograph did not know they were going to be photographed and were surprised by the sudden burst of light. In his notes, Riis reported that this 13-foot room "slept twelve men and women, two or three in bunks set in a sort of alcove, the rest on the floor."

Jacob Riis, Five Cents a Spot — Unauthorized Immigration Lodgings in a Bayard Street Tenement, 1890.

Document B Tramp in Mulberry Street Yard (1887–1888)
Location: Mulberry Bend, New York City Date: 1887–1888 Type: Photograph Photographer: Jacob Riis

This photograph was taken in Mulberry Bend, one of the most dangerous areas of the notorious Five Points neighborhood in New York City. The image shows a man sitting in front of a dilapidated structure. Riis paid the man ten cents to sit for the portrait.

Jacob Riis, Tramp in Mulberry Street Yard, 1887–1888.

If the image above does not display, your instructor will add it. The photograph shows a man seated in front of a ramshackle wooden structure.

In his lecture notes, Riis later wrote about this image: "On one of my visits to 'the Bend' I came across this fellow sitting . . . and he struck me as being such a typical tramp that I asked him to sit still for a minute and I would give him ten cents. That was probably the first and only ten cents that man had earned by honest labor in the course of his life and that was by sitting down at which he was an undoubted expert."

Source: Jacob Riis, lecture notes. Cited in Bonnie Yochelson & Daniel Czitrom, Rediscovering Jacob Riis, 2007.
Document C Italian Mother and Her Baby in Jersey Street (1888–1889)
Location: New York City Date: 1888–1889 Type: Photograph Photographer: Jacob Riis

This image shows an Italian immigrant woman and her baby sitting in their windowless one-room tenement. The room contains all their possessions, including a rolled-up mattress and a pallet that likely served as their bed.

Jacob Riis, Italian Mother and Her Baby in Jersey Street, 1888–1889.

Source: Jacob Riis. How the Other Half Lives. 1890. Museum of the City of New York.
Document D How the Other Half Lives — Chapter on Italian Immigrants (1890)
Author: Jacob Riis Date: 1890 Type: Published book excerpt Audience: Middle-class American readers

The Italian comes in at the bottom, and in the generation that came over the sea he stays there. In the slums he is welcomed as a tenant who "makes less trouble" than the contentious Irishman or the order-loving German, that is to say: is content to live in a pig-sty and submits to robbery at the hands of the rent-collector without murmur. . . .

Ordinarily he is easily enough governed by authority — always excepting Sunday, when he settles down to a game of cards and lets loose all his bad passions. Like the Chinese, the Italian is a born gambler. His soul is in the game from the moment the cards are on the table, and very frequently his knife is in it too before the game is ended.

Source: Jacob Riis. How the Other Half Lives. 1890. Charles Scribner's Sons.
