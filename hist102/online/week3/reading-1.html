19.1 Turner, Mahan, and the Roots of American Empire

Why did the United States look beyond its borders — and what ideas justified the turn outward?

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the economic, strategic, and ideological motivations behind American imperialism in the 1890s
  • Identify the key intellectual arguments that justified overseas expansion
  • Describe the process by which Hawaii was annexed and the controversy it generated

When Frederick Jackson Turner declared in 1893 that the American frontier had closed, he was not merely announcing a geographic fact. He was raising a question that would preoccupy American policymakers, businessmen, and intellectuals for the next two decades: if the frontier had defined American democracy and national character, what would replace it? Turner's answer, implicitly, was that Americans must look outward — beyond the continent — for new frontiers of settlement, trade, and civilization-building.

Naval strategist Alfred Thayer Mahan provided the strategic framework. In his 1890 work The Influence of Sea Power upon History, Mahan argued that national greatness depended upon naval power, which in turn required overseas coaling stations and naval bases, which in turn required colonies or protectorates in which to establish them. Without a powerful navy and a network of overseas bases, Mahan argued, the United States would remain a second-rate power, its commerce vulnerable and its borders insecure. The logic was circular and self-reinforcing: empire justified empire.

A third argument came from Social Darwinism and the ideology of Anglo-Saxon superiority. Minister Josiah Strong argued in Our Country (1885) that Anglo-Saxon civilization — Protestant, democratic, commercially vigorous — was destined to spread across the globe. Expansion was not merely economically advantageous; it was a moral obligation.

Hawaii: A Case Study in Annexation

Queen Liliʻuokalani
1838 – 1917
The last monarch of Hawaii, deposed in January 1893 by a group of American sugar planters with the aid of U.S. Marines. She petitioned Congress repeatedly to restore Hawaiian sovereignty and was placed under house arrest after a failed counter-revolution in 1895.

Hawaii illustrates how American imperialism operated in practice. American sugar planters had been established in Hawaii since the 1830s. Under the Reciprocity Treaty of 1875, Hawaiian sugar entered the United States duty-free, making Hawaiian plantations extraordinarily profitable. When Queen Liliʻuokalani came to the throne in 1891 and moved to strengthen Hawaiian sovereignty and limit the planters' power, they organized a coup. On January 17, 1893, a small group of American businessmen, backed by U.S. Marines who had landed from the USS Boston, overthrew the Hawaiian government and established a provisional government requesting annexation by the United States.

President Grover Cleveland investigated and concluded that the coup was illegal — "an act of war committed with the participation of a diplomatic representative of the United States." He refused to forward an annexation treaty to Congress. But President McKinley, taking office in 1897, was more favorable to expansion. Formal annexation came in July 1898, during the fever of the Spanish-American War, by a joint resolution of Congress rather than a treaty (which would have required a two-thirds Senate vote McKinley could not guarantee). Hawaii became a U.S. territory. It would not become a state until 1959.

19.2 The Spanish-American War and Its Aftermath

From Cuba to the Philippines — and the anti-imperialist backlash

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the causes of the Spanish-American War and the role of yellow journalism
  • Describe the outcomes of the war and the territories acquired by the United States
  • Analyze the arguments made by anti-imperialists and evaluate their critique

The Cuban revolt against Spanish colonial rule, which began in 1895, gave American imperialists their opening. The Cuban insurgents deliberately destroyed American-owned sugar plantations and railroads to force U.S. intervention. Spain responded with a brutal "reconcentration" policy that herded Cuban civilians into camps where tens of thousands died of disease and starvation. American newspapers — especially William Randolph Hearst's New York Journal and Joseph Pulitzer's New York World — covered the atrocities extensively, often embellishing them. This yellow journalism inflamed American public opinion against Spain.

The explosion of the USS Maine in Havana Harbor on February 15, 1898, killing 266 American sailors, provided the emotional trigger. Though the cause of the explosion was never established (a 1976 Navy investigation concluded it was likely an internal accident), Hearst's paper ran the headline: "Remember the Maine! To Hell with Spain!" Congress declared war on April 25, 1898. The conflict lasted only 113 days and resulted in a decisive American victory.

Theodore Roosevelt
1858 – 1919
Assistant Secretary of the Navy before the war, Roosevelt resigned to lead the volunteer cavalry unit known as the Rough Riders. The charge up Kettle Hill (misreported as San Juan Hill) made him a national hero and launched his political career toward the presidency.

The Treaty of Paris (1898) transferred Spain's remaining colonies — Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines — to the United States for $20 million. Cuba became nominally independent but was bound to the United States by the Platt Amendment, which gave the U.S. the right to intervene militarily in Cuban affairs. The Philippines proved the most controversial acquisition. Emilio Aguinaldo had led Filipino fighters against Spain expecting independence; instead, they found themselves fighting a brutal American counterinsurgency war that lasted until 1902 and killed an estimated 200,000 to 600,000 Filipinos, many from disease in reconcentration camps eerily similar to those used by Spain in Cuba.

The Anti-Imperialist League

Opposition to empire was vocal and distinguished. The Anti-Imperialist League, founded in 1898, included Andrew Carnegie, Mark Twain, former President Cleveland, and labor leader Samuel Gompers. Twain was scathing: he proposed a new American flag — the stars replaced by the skull-and-crossbones, the white stripes replaced by black. Carnegie offered to personally write a check for $20 million to buy the Philippines their independence. Their arguments were various: empire violated the consent of the governed (the principle of the Declaration of Independence), it would corrupt American democracy by requiring a large standing army and a culture of militarism, and it would require the assimilation or subjugation of non-white peoples for whom most Americans had no desire as fellow citizens.

"We cannot fly from our world duties; it is ours to execute the purpose of a fate that has driven us to be greater than our small intentions. We cannot retreat from any soil where Providence has unfurled our banner." — Senator Albert Beveridge (R-Indiana), January 9, 1900

21.1 Theodore Roosevelt and the Square Deal

The president as reformer — trust-busting, conservation, and the redefinition of federal power

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how Roosevelt's Square Deal redefined the relationship between the federal government and the economy
  • Describe TR's approach to trusts, labor disputes, and conservation
  • Identify the key legislation of the Roosevelt years and its significance

When an anarchist assassinated President McKinley in September 1901, Theodore Roosevelt — at forty-two, the youngest president in American history — took office. Republican party bosses had engineered his selection as vice president specifically to neutralize him (the New York governorship he had used to challenge party machines), and they were appalled. "Now look," groaned party boss Mark Hanna, "that damned cowboy is President of the United States."

Roosevelt brought to the presidency an expansive view of executive power. Where previous presidents had interpreted the Constitution to limit presidential action to specifically enumerated powers, Roosevelt invoked what he called the stewardship theory: the president could do anything not explicitly forbidden by the Constitution. This expansive reading gave him the tools to reshape the federal government's relationship to the economy.

Trust-Busting

Theodore Roosevelt
1858 – 1919
26th president (1901–1909). Filed forty-four antitrust suits, established five national parks and eighteen national monuments, and negotiated the end of the Russo-Japanese War (for which he won the Nobel Peace Prize). Famously remarked: "Speak softly and carry a big stick."

The Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890 had been on the books for over a decade but had been used primarily to break up labor unions rather than corporations. Roosevelt revived it. In 1902, he directed his attorney general to file suit against the Northern Securities Company, a massive railroad combine created by J. P. Morgan that effectively monopolized rail transport in the Northwest. When Morgan visited the White House expecting to negotiate a private settlement — as had been the Gilded Age custom — Roosevelt refused. The Supreme Court ordered Northern Securities dissolved in 1904, and TR became "the trust-buster." He filed forty-four antitrust suits during his presidency.

Roosevelt distinguished between "good trusts" — large corporations that were efficient and treated their workers fairly — and "bad trusts" that abused their market power. He did not oppose bigness per se; he wanted the federal government to regulate and supervise large corporations in the public interest. The Bureau of Corporations (1903) was the institutional expression of this philosophy: a federal watchdog that could investigate and publicize corporate misconduct.

Coal, Conservation, and the Square Deal

In 1902, 140,000 Pennsylvania coal miners went on strike, demanding higher wages, shorter hours, and union recognition. When mine operators refused to negotiate and winter approached, a fuel shortage threatened the East Coast. Roosevelt summoned both sides to the White House — an unprecedented presidential intervention in a private labor dispute — and threatened to seize the mines and operate them with the Army if the owners refused arbitration. They capitulated. The miners won a 10 percent wage increase and a nine-hour day; the union was not formally recognized but the principle was established: the federal government would not automatically side with capital against labor. This was the essence of Roosevelt's Square Deal.

Roosevelt was also the first conservation president. He added 150 million acres to the national forest system, created five national parks and eighteen national monuments, and signed the Newlands Reclamation Act (1902), which funded irrigation projects in the arid West. He appointed his friend Gifford Pinchot to head the new U.S. Forest Service, and together they fought to wrest control of western lands from mining, timber, and ranching interests that had long treated public lands as a private resource.

21.2 The Muckrakers and the Culture of Exposure

How investigative journalism drove legislative reform

Learning Objectives

  • Define muckraking journalism and explain its role in the Progressive reform movement
  • Identify key muckrakers and the specific abuses each exposed
  • Explain how muckraking journalism translated into federal legislation

Roosevelt coined the term muckrakers after a character in John Bunyan's Pilgrim's Progress who was so consumed with raking up the filth beneath him that he could not look upward toward the celestial crown. He meant it as mild criticism — some reformers, he worried, were so focused on exposure that they failed to propose constructive solutions. But the term was immediately embraced as a badge of honor by a generation of journalists who were transforming American magazines into instruments of democratic accountability.

Ida Tarbell
1857 – 1944
Her nineteen-part series in McClure's Magazine (1902–1904) documented how Standard Oil used secret railroad rebates, price-fixing, and industrial espionage to crush competitors. Roosevelt cited it directly when directing his attorney general to file antitrust suit against Standard Oil.

The most consequential muckraking work was Ida Tarbell's The History of the Standard Oil Company, published in nineteen installments in McClure's Magazine beginning in 1902. Tarbell had personal reasons to hate Standard Oil — Rockefeller had destroyed her father's independent oil business in the 1870s. She spent five years in the archives, interviewing hundreds of sources, and produced a meticulous, devastating account of how Standard Oil had used secret railroad rebates, industrial espionage, and predatory pricing to eliminate competition. The series reached an audience of nearly half a million readers and is widely credited with building the public pressure that led to the Supreme Court's 1911 decision breaking Standard Oil into thirty-four companies.

Lincoln Steffens' The Shame of the Cities (1904) documented machine corruption in Minneapolis, St. Louis, Pittsburgh, Philadelphia, Chicago, and New York, arguing that bribery and graft were not aberrations but the normal operating condition of American municipal government. Ray Stannard Baker investigated racial discrimination in Following the Color Line (1908). Samuel Hopkins Adams exposed the patent medicine industry in The Great American Fraud (1905), documenting how "medicines" claiming to cure everything from cancer to alcoholism were mostly mixtures of alcohol, opium, and cocaine — sold without any ingredient labeling.

From Exposure to Legislation

The most dramatic legislative consequence of muckraking was Upton Sinclair's The Jungle (1906). Sinclair, a socialist, intended the book as an indictment of the exploitation of immigrant workers in Chicago's meatpacking plants. Instead, readers fixated on the descriptions of contaminated food: rat-infested meat, tubercular pork, and workers who fell into rendering vats and were turned into lard. "I aimed at the public's heart," Sinclair wrote bitterly, "and by accident I hit it in the stomach." Roosevelt read the book, gagged at the descriptions while eating breakfast, and sent federal inspectors to Chicago. Their report confirmed Sinclair's account. Within months, Congress passed both the Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act of 1906.

21.3 Women and the Progressive Movement

From settlement houses to the Nineteenth Amendment

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the role of women in the Progressive reform movement and the settlement house movement
  • Trace the women's suffrage movement from Seneca Falls to the Nineteenth Amendment
  • Identify the key organizations and strategies used by suffragists
Jane Addams
1860 – 1935
Co-founded Hull House in Chicago in 1889. One of the most influential social reformers of the Progressive Era, she successfully advocated for child labor laws, workers' compensation, woman suffrage, and international peace. Won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1931.

Women were central to the Progressive reform project, but their path to political influence was indirect — constrained by the fact that in most states they could neither vote nor hold office. The settlement house movement gave educated women a platform. Hull House in Chicago, co-founded by Jane Addams and Ellen Gates Starr in 1889, became the most famous of more than four hundred settlement houses operating by 1910. Settlement house workers lived in poor neighborhoods, provided childcare, English classes, job training, and cultural programs, and simultaneously collected systematic data on poverty, disease, and working conditions that they used to lobby for legislative reform.

Florence Kelley, a Hull House resident, became one of the most effective Progressive lobbyists. She investigated child labor in Illinois, documenting children as young as three working in tenement sweatshops. Her findings helped pass an 1893 Illinois law prohibiting child labor under fourteen and limiting women's workdays to eight hours. She later became the first director of the National Consumers League, using the power of middle-class women's purchasing decisions to pressure retailers into meeting minimum labor standards.

The Road to the Nineteenth Amendment

The campaign for women's suffrage had roots stretching back to the Seneca Falls Convention of 1848, which had included women's voting rights among its demands. Progress was glacially slow. By 1900, only four western states — Wyoming, Colorado, Idaho, and Utah — had granted women full voting rights. The movement was divided: the National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA) pursued a state-by-state strategy, while Alice Paul's more militant National Woman's Party (NWP) demanded an immediate federal amendment and was willing to embarrass and confront the Wilson administration.

World War I created the decisive opening. Women's wartime contributions — as factory workers, nurses, and Red Cross volunteers — made arguments against their political equality increasingly untenable. When NWP picketers were arrested outside the White House and force-fed during hunger strikes in prison, public sympathy shifted. Wilson finally endorsed the amendment in September 1918. The Nineteenth Amendment was ratified on August 18, 1920, seventy-two years after the Seneca Falls Convention had first demanded it.

21.4 Taft, Wilson, and the Limits of Progressivism

The four reform amendments — and what the Progressive Era did not accomplish

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the differences between Roosevelt's New Nationalism and Wilson's New Freedom
  • Identify the four constitutional amendments of the Progressive Era and explain what each accomplished
  • Evaluate the limits and failures of the Progressive reform agenda
Woodrow Wilson
1856 – 1924
28th president (1913–1921). Former president of Princeton and governor of New Jersey. Campaigned on the "New Freedom" — breaking up trusts rather than regulating them. Established the Federal Reserve and the Federal Trade Commission, and signed the Clayton Antitrust Act, but also presided over the re-segregation of the federal workforce.

William Howard Taft, Roosevelt's chosen successor, proved a disappointment to progressives. Though he filed more antitrust suits than Roosevelt (ninety vs. forty-four), he lacked TR's flair for public leadership. His decision to back conservative Republicans against progressive insurgents in the 1910 midterms and to support a tariff bill that progressives considered a sellout to business interests led Roosevelt to break with him. In 1912, Roosevelt challenged Taft for the Republican nomination; when Taft's machine secured the nomination, Roosevelt ran on the new Progressive ("Bull Moose") Party, splitting the Republican vote and electing Woodrow Wilson.

Wilson's New Freedom differed in emphasis from Roosevelt's New Nationalism. Where TR accepted large corporations as inevitable and focused on federal regulation and supervision, Wilson argued that bigness was inherently corrupting and that the proper remedy was to restore competition by breaking up monopolies. In practice, the two approaches converged: Wilson's most significant institutional achievements — the Federal Reserve (1913) and the Federal Trade Commission (1914) — were quintessentially regulatory rather than trust-busting.

The Four Progressive Amendments

The Progressive Era produced four constitutional amendments in less than a decade — a pace of constitutional change exceeded only by the Reconstruction Amendments of the 1860s. The Sixteenth Amendment (1913) authorized a federal income tax. The Seventeenth Amendment (1913) established the direct election of senators, ending the Gilded Age system in which senators were effectively chosen by corporate-dominated state legislatures. The Eighteenth Amendment (1919) prohibited alcohol. The Nineteenth Amendment (1920) extended voting rights to women.

The Progressive Era's failures are as instructive as its achievements. Wilson, a Virginia-born son of Confederate sympathizers, presided over the re-segregation of the federal workforce, personally screening the film Birth of a Nation at the White House and reportedly remarking that it was "like writing history with lightning." Most Progressive reformers were indifferent to racial justice, and many were actively hostile. The era that produced the income tax and women's suffrage also produced the nadir of American race relations — the highest rates of lynching since Reconstruction, the systematic disenfranchisement of Black voters across the South, and the hardening of Jim Crow into a comprehensive apartheid system. Progressivism was a reform movement for some Americans, not all.

"I aimed at the public's heart, and by accident I hit it in the stomach." — Upton Sinclair, on the reception of The Jungle, 1906
