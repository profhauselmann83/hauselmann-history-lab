Introduction

What We're Studying This Week

By 1900, the United States was the world's leading industrial power — but also a society visibly fracturing under the weight of that achievement. Urban tenements packed millions of recent immigrants into airless rooms. Children worked twelve-hour shifts in coal mines and textile mills. Corporate trusts controlled prices, bought legislators, and crushed competitors. The Gilded Age had produced spectacular wealth and spectacular misery in equal measure, and millions of Americans had decided enough was enough.

The Progressive Era was their response. Between roughly 1890 and 1920, a broad and contentious coalition of journalists, settlement house workers, suffragists, labor organizers, politicians, and ordinary citizens worked to make government — at every level — a tool for reining in corporate power, protecting workers and consumers, expanding democracy, and confronting poverty. Their achievements were real: the Food and Drug Act, the Federal Reserve, the direct election of senators, women's suffrage, the graduated income tax. So were their failures and contradictions: most Progressives were indifferent or hostile to the rights of African Americans, and the era produced both landmark reforms and brutal episodes of racial violence.

This week you will trace American imperialism through the annexation of Hawaii and the Spanish-American War, follow the domestic reform agenda under Roosevelt, Taft, and Wilson, and examine how African Americans navigated an era that simultaneously celebrated democracy and enforced Jim Crow. By the end of the week, you should be able to evaluate whether the Progressive Era delivered on its promises — and for whom.
