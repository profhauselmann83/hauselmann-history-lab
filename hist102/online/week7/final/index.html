Part II — Before & After · 20 Points

Context & Chronology

Below are five anchor events from across the course, each with a brief description. For each anchor, drag one event from the pool into the Before slot and one into the After slot. Each card in the pool names an event, gives its year, and describes what happened.

This is not just about dates — read the descriptions. Each event belongs to a specific period and fits logically before or after only one of the anchors. Think about causes, consequences, and historical context.

Events to Place

Drag cards from this pool into the Before and After slots below. You can drag cards back here to undo.

Compromise of 18771877

Disputed presidential election resolved when Republicans agreed to withdraw federal troops from the South in exchange for the White House. Signaled the end of federal Reconstruction and opened the way for Southern states to impose new racial hierarchies.

Niagara Movement1905

Founded by W.E.B. Du Bois and a group of African American intellectuals demanding full civil and political rights, directly challenging Booker T. Washington's accommodationist strategy. A forerunner of the NAACP.

Lend-Lease ActMarch 1941

Congress authorized the United States to supply war material to Britain and later other Allies without formally entering combat. Ended any pretense of strict neutrality as the nation edged toward direct involvement in World War II.

Japanese American Internment1942

Following Executive Order 9066, roughly 120,000 Japanese Americans — most of them U.S. citizens — were forcibly removed from the West Coast and confined in government camps without trial, a wartime civil liberties violation later formally apologized for by Congress.

Executive Order 99811948

President Harry Truman ordered the desegregation of the U.S. armed forces, one of the first major federal acts against institutional racial segregation in the modern era and an important signal that the federal government might take civil rights seriously.

Montgomery Bus Boycott1955–1956

Sparked when Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat, a year-long Black-led boycott of the Montgomery, Alabama bus system ended in federal court victory and launched a young Baptist minister named Martin Luther King Jr. into national leadership.

Tet Offensive1968

A coordinated North Vietnamese and Viet Cong attack on cities across South Vietnam during the Lunar New Year. Though militarily repulsed, it shattered official U.S. claims that the war was being won and became a turning point in American public opinion.

Camp David Accords1978

President Jimmy Carter brokered a framework agreement between Egypt and Israel that led to a formal peace treaty — a rare post-Vietnam American foreign-policy success and a signal that U.S. diplomacy could still shape the international order.

Oklahoma City Bombing1995

A truck bomb destroyed a federal office building in Oklahoma City, killing 168 people. Carried out by an American anti-government extremist, it was the deadliest act of domestic terrorism in U.S. history to that point and raised new questions about radicalization at home.

Hurricane Katrina2005

A Category 3 hurricane breached levees and flooded New Orleans. Over 1,800 people died and hundreds of thousands were displaced. The failure of federal, state, and local response laid bare enduring inequalities of race and class in American disaster policy.

"Bloody Sunday"  ·  Edmund Pettus Bridge, Selma, Alabama  ·  March 7, 1965

On March 7, 1965, roughly 600 civil rights marchers attempted to walk from Selma, Alabama, to the state capital in Montgomery to demand voting rights. At the Edmund Pettus Bridge on the edge of Selma, Alabama state troopers and a local sheriff's posse attacked the marchers with nightsticks and tear gas. Television networks interrupted evening programming to broadcast footage of the attack. Within eight days, President Lyndon Johnson addressed a joint session of Congress and introduced what would become the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

Source: News photograph taken on March 7, 1965, at the Edmund Pettus Bridge. Widely distributed in the American press the following day.
