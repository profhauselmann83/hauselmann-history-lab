About this film

102 Minutes That Changed America (History Channel, 2008) is a documentary that assembles camcorder tape, cell-phone video, news feeds, and voicemails recorded in New York City between 8:46 a.m. — the moment American Airlines Flight 11 struck the North Tower of the World Trade Center — and 10:28 a.m., the moment the North Tower collapsed. The film has no narrator, no score, no talking-head interviews. There are no experts explaining what the footage means. The 102 minutes are the 102 minutes.

Watch it that way. Notice how long it takes for anyone on camera to understand what is happening. Notice what they think it is before they know. Notice the ordinariness of the background — the Tuesday-morning commute, the coffee, the dog walks — and how the footage refuses to let you skip past it. This is what the written record cannot show you: the experience of being inside an event before it has a name.

A note on content: the film includes distressing footage, including audio from 911 calls, images of people trapped above the impact zones, and sequences recorded during both collapses. Take breaks if you need them. If any part of this material is personally affecting and you would prefer an alternative assignment, email me — we can work something out.

What to pay attention to

  • What people don't know. In the first half hour, most of the people holding cameras are trying to figure out whether they are watching an accident or an attack. Listen for the moment each of them crosses that line — and notice how long it takes.
  • The source problem. This footage is primary-source evidence in the rawest possible sense: unedited, unauthored, recorded by people who had no idea what the record would later be used for. How does that change what the footage can and cannot tell you?
  • The language gap. Notice how many of the people on camera reach for war metaphors, movie metaphors, dream metaphors — anything that can give a name to what they are seeing. A historical event, in the moment, is not yet the event it will become.
  • The political aftermath is in Reading 7.1. The film ends at 10:28 a.m. — before the Bush Doctrine, before the Authorization for Use of Military Force, before the invasions of Afghanistan and Iraq. The readings pick up from there. Hold the film and the reading together as two different kinds of sources for the same event.
