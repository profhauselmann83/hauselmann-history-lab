Overview

Over the past seven weeks, you have done something unusual in a history course: you have read contemporary civic theory alongside primary historical evidence, and used each to test the other. Richard Haass argues that American democracy requires not just rights but obligations — from citizens, officials, institutions, and the press alike.

This paper asks you to take that work seriously. It is a research and reflection paper, which means two things at once: you will do real analytical work with historical evidence (the research), and you will also reflect on what that evidence means — for Haass's framework, for American democracy, and for you as a student of history (the reflection).

What makes this paper different This is not a book report. You are not summarizing Haass. This is not a standard history paper. You are not just narrating events. This is an argument paper that moves between historical evidence and civic theory — using each to interrogate the other. The best papers will have moments of genuine surprise: places where the history didn't confirm what you expected, or where Haass said something more complicated than it first appeared.

Required Sources

You must use all three types
  • The Bill of Obligations — at least one specific chapter, with a direct quotation
  • OpenStax US History — at least two specific sections from the course readings (cite by chapter and section, e.g., Ch. 16.2)
  • At least one primary source from the course — a document, speech, law, or other historical source (identify the document, author, and date)

Additional outside sources are permitted, but the paper should be grounded primarily in course materials. No Chicago or MLA footnotes required — clear in-text attribution is sufficient. Do not over-quote. Use quotation when exact wording matters. Otherwise, paraphrase and cite.

Choose Your Prompt

Each prompt approaches the same core question — what does American history reveal about the obligations of democratic citizenship? — from a different angle. Read all four before choosing.

Option A

The Broken Obligation

Which obligation has history violated most consistently — and why?

Your paper must:
  • Identify the obligation clearly and explain what Haass means by it
  • Demonstrate the pattern of violation across at least three historical moments (from three different course weeks)
  • Use at least one primary source as evidence
  • Engage with Haass directly: quote him, then show evidence that complicates or confirms his claim
  • Conclude by asking: what would it take to actually fulfill this obligation?
Example Thesis "Despite Haass's claim that promoting the common good is an obligation of all citizens, American history from the Gilded Age through the COVID-19 pandemic reveals a consistent pattern in which the language of common good has been invoked to protect privilege rather than extend it — suggesting the obligation has structural as well as individual barriers."
Option B

The Blind Spot

Where does Haass's framework fail to account for the experience of a specific group?

Your paper must:
  • Identify your chosen group and the historical period you're focusing on
  • Explain what Haass's framework says — in his own words — about the relevant obligations
  • Show, using specific historical evidence, how the framework fails this group
  • Identify at least one obligation your group was fulfilling that Haass doesn't recognize
  • Conclude by proposing a revision or addition to Haass's framework that the history demands
Example Thesis "Haass's obligation to 'reject violence' assumes democratic institutions will restrain state violence against nonviolent protesters. The Birmingham Campaign of 1963 exposes this as the framework's central vulnerability: the strategy's success depended not on the absence of violence but on the state's willingness to conduct it publicly."
Option C

The Crossroads Moment

A specific historical moment where things could have gone differently.

Your paper must:
  • Identify the historical moment precisely — a specific event or decision, not a vague era
  • Explain clearly which obligation(s) were violated and what Haass says about them
  • Analyze what happened using specific evidence from course readings
  • Make a disciplined counterfactual argument: what alternative was available, and what evidence shows it was genuinely possible?
  • Reflect on what the gap reveals about individual obligation vs. structural forces
Example Thesis "The Compromise of 1877 represents a moment when the obligation to 'put country first' was invoked to end Reconstruction — but Haass's own framework reveals the decision as its opposite: political actors prioritized partisan advantage over the democratic project, abandoning four million citizens for whom democracy had barely begun."
Option D

The Obligation That Matters Most Now

History as a guide to the present moment.

Your paper must:
  • Identify the obligation and make a clear argument for why it is most urgent now
  • Trace its historical record across at least three course weeks
  • Use at least one contemporary example (from the last five years) alongside the historical evidence
  • Engage with at least one counterargument: why might someone choose a different obligation?
  • Conclude with a specific, concrete claim about what fulfilling this obligation would actually require in the current context
Example Thesis "Of Haass's ten obligations, 'Be Informed' is the most urgent in 2026 — not because Americans are uniquely uninformed, but because the history of Reconstruction, the Progressive Era, and the Civil Rights Movement reveals that the collapse of shared factual ground has always preceded the collapse of democratic norms."

Paper Structure

A 1,500–2,000 word analytical paper typically moves through five functional sections. You do not need to use these as labeled headers — the best papers integrate them fluidly — but every paper should accomplish all five of these things.

Introduction
½ page		 Opens with a specific historical moment or image, not a generic statement. States your thesis clearly before the end of the first page. Tells the reader which prompt you chose and what argument you're making.
Haass Framework
½–¾ page		 Explains the obligation(s) at the center of your argument. Quotes Haass directly at least once. Shows that you understand what he is arguing before you test it against history.
Historical Evidence
2–3 pages		 The core of the paper. Deploys specific historical evidence — from the textbook, primary sources, and lecture — to develop your argument. Does not simply narrate; analyzes what the evidence reveals about the obligation. Moves across at least two historical periods.
Critical Engagement
½–1 page		 This is where you push back — on Haass, on the history, on your own argument. Acknowledges complexity, tension, or a counterargument. Shows that you are thinking, not just reporting.
Conclusion
½ page		 Does not simply restate the thesis. Reflects on what the paper has revealed: about Haass's framework, about American democracy, or about the relationship between past and present. Ends with something worth thinking about.

Advice for Strong Papers

Start with the history, not with Haass

The most common weakness is beginning with a long summary of Haass and adding historical examples at the end. Reverse it. Start with a specific historical moment — a scene, a document, a decision — and let Haass's framework enter as a lens. The history should do the argumentative work; Haass provides the vocabulary.

Your thesis should be arguable — and specific

"Haass's obligation to promote the common good is important" is not a thesis. A thesis makes a specific claim someone could disagree with: it names an obligation, makes a claim about the pattern, and suggests what that pattern reveals. Read the example theses above to see the level of specificity required.

Use the friction, not just the confirmation

The most interesting papers find where Haass's framework breaks down — where the history doesn't behave the way his theory predicts. Every weekly discussion guide identified a "productive friction." Your paper will be stronger if you engage with that friction honestly rather than using Haass only where he confirms your point.

Reflection is not summary

The reflective dimension asks you to say something about what the analysis means — for Haass's framework, for democracy, for how you think about past and present. This is not a place to summarize what you wrote in the body. It is a place to take a risk: say something you genuinely believe, based on the work you've done.

A note from Prof. Hauselmann The best papers I receive are the ones where I can hear the student thinking — wrestling with the evidence, pushing back on the argument, arriving somewhere they didn't expect to be when they started. Haass is a useful framework. He is not a sacred text. American history is a rich, contradictory, surprising record. Your job is to put the two in genuine conversation — and to have something to say about what that conversation reveals.
