Week 7  ·  April 20 – 26

Contemporary America and Beyond

The final week — from the fall of the Soviet Union to January 6th, the pandemic, and the present moment

~10 hours 60 pts + paper Canvas + Hypothesis

The course ends where it began: with the question of whether American democracy can hold. Seven weeks ago you opened The Bill of Obligations alongside OpenStax Ch. 16 on Reconstruction. Now you read Haass's Chapter 1 — Be Informed alongside Ch. 31, which runs from the fall of the Berlin Wall to the storming of the Capitol and the Covid pandemic.

Haass places “Be Informed” first for a reason: every other obligation depends on it. A citizenry that cannot distinguish fact from misstatement, opinion from prediction, cannot compromise well, deliberate well, or recognize when norms are being eroded. The history you have studied — Reconstruction's collapse, Jim Crow's long violence, the rise of mass media, the Cold War's propaganda wars, Watergate's tapes, the Reagan revolution's information infrastructure — all feed into the present.

Your task this week is to see the present as history. The 2000 election, September 11, the Iraq War, the financial crisis, the Obama years, Trump's presidency, January 6th, the pandemic — none of these are “current events” in the narrow sense. They are the culmination of the patterns you have been tracking for seven weeks.

Learning Objectives

  • Trace the political, economic, and social transformations from the end of the Cold War through the present day
  • Analyze how the post-9/11 security state and the War on Terror reshaped American constitutional and foreign-policy practice
  • Explain how deregulation, deindustrialization, and financialization produced the 2008 Great Recession
  • Evaluate the use of congressional debate as a historical source — and distinguish what senators disagreed about from why they disagreed
  • Apply Haass's framework (fact / misstatement / opinion / prediction / recommendation) to a real contemporary political controversy

Central Question

“History also provides lessons for contemporary challenges.” After seven weeks — what lessons? And: what did U.S. senators disagree about when debating the October 2002 Iraq Resolution?
