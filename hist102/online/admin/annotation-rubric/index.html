Annotation Grading Rubric

HIST 102: Primary Source Discussion & Haass Chapter Annotations

Primary Source Discussion Annotations

Category A/B — Excellent/Good C — Satisfactory D/F — Minimal/Missing
Golden Line A/BSelects meaningful passage AND explains why it's important to the central question. Explanation shows what the passage reveals about the author's political theory, strategy, or beliefs. Citation present (A: fully developed; B: somewhat developed). CSelects passage but explanation is partial or surface-level. May describe what the passage says rather than what it reveals. Citation may be missing. D/FSelects passage but provides only personal reaction or basic description with no real explanation. Little to no citation. (D: minimal effort; F: missing).
Sourcing A/BCorrectly identifies creator, audience (specific, not vague), and purpose. Explains how the creator's context shaped the argument. All elements present and developed (A: thorough; B: adequate). CIdentifies creator and some other elements, but one element is missing or incorrect. Explanation of context is weak or vague. Partial understanding shown. D/FIdentifies creator only, or misidentifies audience/purpose. No meaningful explanation of context. Major elements missing (D: minimal elements present; F: missing/wrong).
Contextualization A/BSelects relevant context from OpenStax and cites correctly. Explains how context illuminates the specific document. Shows why this context matters for understanding the document (A: clear and thorough; B: clear but could be deeper). CSelects and cites context, but connection to document is weak or vague. May apply context to the wrong document or fail to show why it matters. D/FCites context but doesn't meaningfully connect it, applies to wrong document, or provides no citation (D: minimal connection; F: missing/incoherent).
Corroboration A/BIdentifies both documents and their claims. Clearly names the contradiction. Explains what it reveals about the larger historical pattern or assumption (A: thorough interpretation; B: adequate interpretation). CIdentifies both documents and names a contradiction, but stops there. Little to no interpretation of what the contradiction reveals about larger patterns. D/FIdentifies only one document, names only surface disagreement, or provides no real contradiction or interpretation (D: minimal engagement; F: missing).
Research A/BFinds credible source (government, academic, peer-reviewed). Evaluates credibility and connects meaningfully to passage. Includes specific quote or evidence (A: strong connection; B: adequate connection). CFinds credible source with some credibility evaluation. Connection to passage is somewhat vague or surface-level. Evidence may be present but not specific. D/FFinds source but provides minimal evaluation or weak connection. Little evidence included (D: minimal effort; F: no source or unreliable).
Discussion Question A/BAsks open-ended question that cannot be answered with yes/no. Presses on genuine tension or contradiction. Invites interpretive debate (A: strong pressure; B: adequate pressure). CQuestion hints at an issue but is too narrow, vague, or somewhat answerable with a fact. Doesn't fully press on tension. D/FAsks yes/no question or asks for clarification rather than interpretation. No real tension pressed (D: minimal engagement; F: missing).
Peer Responses A/BResponds directly to specific peer annotation. Extends peer's argument OR asks genuine follow-up question. Shows engagement (A: deep engagement; B: good engagement). CResponds to peer but only agrees or affirms without extending thinking. May lack specificity. Minimal engagement with peer's idea. D/FResponse is generic, not tied to specific peer work, or shows minimal understanding (D: minimal effort; F: missing).

Haass Chapter Annotations

Category A/B — Excellent/Good C — Satisfactory D/F — Minimal/Missing
Golden Line A/BSelects meaningful, important passage. Provides explanation of why it's central to Haass's main argument. Shows how passage reveals a key concept (A: fully developed; B: adequately developed). CSelects passage and provides some explanation of importance. May focus on what the passage says rather than what it reveals about Haass's broader argument. D/FSelects passage but provides only basic description or personal reaction. Little explanation of why passage matters (D: minimal effort; F: missing).
Question A/BAsks open-ended question that cannot be answered with yes/no. Presses on genuine tension or gap in Haass's argument. Invites interpretive debate (A: strong pressure; B: adequate pressure). CQuestion hints at issue but is vague, too narrow, or somewhat answerable with a fact. Doesn't fully press on genuine tension. D/FAsks yes/no question or asks for clarification rather than interpretation (D: minimal engagement; F: missing).
Agree/Challenge A/BClearly identifies author's claim. States agreement. Identifies genuine limitation or gap in author's argument. Provides specific case where position breaks down (A: fully developed; B: adequately developed). CIdentifies claim and states agreement, but challenge is weak, vague, or underdeveloped. May name a problem without explaining why it's a problem. D/FIdentifies claim and agrees but provides no real challenge. Just restates or affirms (D: minimal effort; F: missing).

Example Responses

A Excellent Response

Golden Line Example

"You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold."
A-Level Response: "Bryan's closing image of the 'cross of gold' is the most important moment in this speech because it transforms an economic argument into a moral and religious one. By invoking the crucifixion—'cross'—Bryan reframes monetary policy as a matter of faith and sacrifice, not just policy preference. This rhetorical move does crucial work: it allows him to claim moral authority and frames his opponents as actively harming the people. The metaphor is strategic: it makes the audience's economic suffering feel sacred, which justifies the radical political change he's asking for. Without this image, his speech is just economics; with it, he's asking for moral transformation."

Why This is A Level:

  • ✓ Identifies passage
  • ✓ Fully explains what it reveals about Bryan's strategy
  • ✓ Shows how the phrase functions in the larger argument
  • ✓ Develops interpretation thoroughly

C Satisfactory Response

Golden Line Example

"You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold."
C-Level Response: "This passage is important because Bryan uses religious language to appeal to farmers. The 'cross of gold' shows his connection to the Populist movement. It's a powerful phrase that resonates with the audience."

Why This is C Level:

  • ✓ Identifies passage
  • ✓ Describes what it does (religious language, appeals to farmers)
  • ✗ Doesn't explain WHY or what it reveals
  • ✗ Stops at surface level; no interpretation

The Gap: The C-level response identifies and describes the passage. The A-level response takes that same passage and explains what it reveals about the author's strategy, why the author made that choice, and what it accomplishes in the overall argument. That's the analytical move that separates satisfactory from excellent.
