27.1 The Origins of World War II — Fascism, Appeasement, and Pearl Harbor

From isolationism to engagement — how the United States entered a second world war

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how Fascism and Nazism rose in Europe and Japan’s military expansion in Asia
  • Identify the steps by which FDR moved the U.S. from neutrality toward engagement
  • Describe the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor and its consequences

The years between the wars were defined by economic collapse, political instability, and the rise of totalitarian regimes. In Italy, Benito Mussolini created a Fascist state. In Germany, the Great Depression accelerated Adolf Hitler’s Nazi Party: by 1932 the Nazis were Germany’s largest party, and by 1935 Hitler had withdrawn from the League of Nations and begun rearming. Britain and France appeased him at the 1938 Munich Conference, surrendering Czechoslovakia’s Sudetenland. Germany invaded Poland on September 1, 1939; Britain and France declared war two days later.

Franklin D. Roosevelt
1882 – 1945
32nd president (1933–1945). Led the U.S. through both the Depression and WWII. Navigated between an isolationist public and a growing Fascist threat through tools like Lend-Lease and Cash and Carry before Pearl Harbor forced the decision from his hands.

American public opinion was deeply isolationist. Congress passed a series of Neutrality Acts. FDR gradually moved toward support for the Allies: “Cash and Carry” (1939) allowed Britain to purchase weapons; the Lend-Lease Act of 1941 provided weapons on credit. In August 1941 Roosevelt and Churchill drafted the Atlantic Charter, articulating a vision of a postwar democratic order.

In the Pacific, U.S. oil embargoes put Japan under severe pressure. On December 7, 1941, Japan launched a surprise attack on the U.S. Pacific fleet at Pearl Harbor, sinking four battleships, destroying nearly 200 aircraft, and killing 2,400 servicemen. Roosevelt called it “a date which will live in infamy” and asked Congress for a declaration of war the next day. Germany and Italy declared war on December 11. America was in.

American isolationism was the dominant mood of the 1930s — yet the U.S. ended up in the war anyway. Trace the steps by which FDR moved the country from neutrality toward engagement. At what point, if any, do you think intervention became unavoidable?

27.2 The Home Front — Rosie the Riveter, Double V, and Executive Order 9066

Who sacrificed, who benefited, and whose loyalty was questioned

Learning Objectives

  • Describe how the war transformed employment and migration patterns
  • Analyze the Double V campaign and African American military service
  • Explain Executive Order 9066 and evaluate the internment of Japanese Americans

World War II finally ended the Great Depression: defense contracts pumped money into the economy; corporate profits rose from $6.4 billion in 1940 to $11 billion in 1944. Ten million men were drafted; millions of women and minority workers filled the jobs they left behind. Women entered the defense industry in unprecedented numbers, and the government’s propaganda figure of Rosie the Riveter normalized their presence in factories. After the war, most women were pushed out of these jobs as veterans returned — a pattern by now familiar from WWI.

The 442nd Regimental Combat Team
1943 – 1946
The most decorated U.S. military unit for its size and length of service in American history — composed almost entirely of Japanese Americans, many of whose families were simultaneously imprisoned in internment camps. Their motto: “Go for Broke.”

African Americans responded with the Double V campaign. A. Philip Randolph’s threatened March on Washington forced FDR to sign Executive Order 8802, creating the Fair Employment Practices Committee. The Tuskegee Airmen served with distinction. Yet race riots erupted in Detroit, Harlem, and elsewhere, and the military remained segregated throughout the war.

The war’s most sweeping civil liberties violation was the internment of Japanese Americans. Executive Order 9066 authorized the forced removal of 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry — two-thirds of them American citizens — to camps where families lost homes, businesses, and property. Despite this, nearly 33,000 Japanese Americans served in the military; the 442nd Regimental Combat Team became the most decorated unit for its size in U.S. military history.

27.3 Victory in the European Theater — D-Day and the Holocaust

The liberation of Europe and the discovery of its darkest secret

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the major turning points of the European campaign
  • Describe what Allied forces discovered as they advanced through Europe
  • Explain the goals of the wartime summit conferences

The Allied strategy followed a “Europe First” approach. After campaigns in North Africa and Italy, the decisive blow came on June 6, 1944 — D-Day — when 24,000 Allied troops stormed the beaches of Normandy. More than 10,000 were killed or wounded. Paris was liberated in August; Germany surrendered May 8, 1945 — V-E Day.

As Allied forces advanced, they discovered the Holocaust: a network of over 40,000 camps where eleven million people — six million of them Jewish — had been systematically murdered. The scale of the atrocity reshaped international human rights law and the postwar world order.

The Big Three — Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin — met at Teheran (1943) and Yalta (February 1945) to coordinate strategy and plan the postwar settlement. At Yalta, Stalin agreed to enter the war against Japan and join the new United Nations in exchange for concessions in Eastern Europe. Roosevelt died April 12, 1945; Harry Truman became president and inherited the war’s most consequential decision.

Reading Notes — Victory in Europe & the Holocaust
The United States fought in Europe to defeat Nazism — and discovered the Holocaust as it advanced. What does the Holocaust reveal about the consequences of the ideas and political structures the Allies were fighting against? How should it change how we think about this war?

27.4 The Pacific Theater and the Atomic Bomb

Island hopping, the Manhattan Project, and the decision that ended the war

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the island-hopping strategy and the major Pacific battles
  • Explain the decision to use atomic bombs and the reasons offered for it
  • Analyze the moral and strategic arguments surrounding the bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki

In the Pacific, the United States pursued an island-hopping strategy, bypassing Japanese strongholds to seize strategic positions closer to Japan. The battles of Iwo Jima and Okinawa — in which American forces suffered 17,000 and 36,000 casualties respectively — gave military planners a terrifying preview of a potential home island invasion. Some estimates projected 250,000 American deaths to conquer Japan.

The Manhattan Project, which had secretly been developing the atomic bomb at a cost of $2 billion, provided an alternative. On August 6, 1945, the Enola Gay dropped “Little Boy” on Hiroshima; on August 9, “Fat Man” fell on Nagasaki. The bombs killed an estimated 125,000 to 250,000 people, most of them civilians. Japan surrendered August 15, 1945. World War II was over.

“Yesterday, December 7th, 1941 — a date which will live in infamy — the United States of America was suddenly and deliberately attacked.” — President Franklin D. Roosevelt, address to Congress, December 8, 1941

Week 4  ·  Final Synthesis

Connections Across All Three Readings

WWI → Depression → WWII: one long story

These three readings together cover thirty years of American history — from Wilson’s failed League of Nations to Roosevelt’s successful Grand Alliance; from the Espionage Act’s prosecution of dissenters to Executive Order 9066’s internment of citizens; from the “Red Summer” of 1919 to the race riots of Detroit in 1943. The United States fought two world wars for democracy while practicing racial inequality at home. It emerged from each war more powerful — and each time struggled to reckon with what that power was for.

The Haass chapter for this week argues that citizens and leaders must “put country first.” Wilson’s Senate opponents believed they were putting the country first when they rejected the League. FDR believed he was putting the country first when he signed the internment order. Japanese American soldiers of the 442nd believed they were putting the country first when they served the nation that had imprisoned their families. “Country first” turns out to be a contested principle — not a clear answer to hard questions.
