23.1 American Isolationism and the European Origins of War

From a nation of immigrants to a nation at war — why neutrality failed

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Wilson’s foreign policy vision and why he found it difficult to maintain neutrality
  • Identify the key factors that pushed the U.S. toward declaring war on Germany in April 1917
  • Analyze the role of submarine warfare, economic ties, and the Zimmermann Telegram

When war broke out in Europe in the summer of 1914, President Woodrow Wilson was determined to keep the United States out of it. In a message to Congress, he urged Americans to be “impartial in thought as well as in action.” This was easier said than done. The United States was a nation of immigrants with ties to every warring country, and its economy was deeply intertwined with Britain and France through trade and loans. Neutrality in practice meant open markets, not equal treatment of both sides.

Woodrow Wilson
1856 – 1924
28th president (1913–1921). A Princeton professor and idealist who believed American values were superior and that democracy was the world’s best hope. He won re-election in 1916 on the slogan “He kept us out of war” — and asked Congress for a declaration of war five months later.

The economic stakes were enormous. From 1914 to 1916, American exports to the Allies quadrupled from $750 million to $3 billion, while exports to Germany collapsed from $350 million to $30 million, thanks to the British naval blockade. American banks, led by J. P. Morgan, lent the Allies more than $500 million. The United States had, in effect, become the financial and material backbone of the Allied war effort — hardly neutral in its consequences, even if it was in its stated intention.

Germany’s response was submarine warfare. In February 1915, Germany declared the waters around Britain a war zone and threatened to sink any ships found there. The U-boat campaign reached its terrible peak on May 7, 1915, when a German submarine sank the British passenger liner RMS Lusitania, killing nearly 1,200 civilians, including 128 Americans. The attack horrified the American public and tested Wilson’s commitment to neutrality. After fierce diplomatic pressure, Germany temporarily restricted its submarine warfare — but it did not stop.

The final push came in early 1917. Germany, desperate to break the British blockade before starvation ended the war, resumed unrestricted submarine warfare, sinking four American merchant ships in March alone. Then British intelligence intercepted and decoded the Zimmermann Telegram, in which Germany offered Mexico an alliance: help invade the United States, and Germany would help recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. On April 2, 1917, Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war. Congress voted on April 6 — with 56 “no” votes, the largest dissent against a war resolution in American history.

“Every man who really loves America will act and speak in the true spirit of neutrality, which is the spirit of impartiality and fairness and friendliness to all concerned.” — Woodrow Wilson, message to Congress, August 1914

23.2 The United States Prepares for War

Mobilizing men, money, food — and public opinion

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the steps taken to mobilize men, money, and supplies for the war effort
  • Explain how the government used propaganda to build support for the war
  • Analyze how the Espionage and Sedition Acts restricted civil liberties during wartime

When the United States declared war, its army ranked seventh in the world in size, with about 200,000 enlisted men. Germany had mobilized over eleven million soldiers. Congress passed the Selective Service Act in 1917, and over ten million men registered for the draft on the very first day. By war’s end, two million American soldiers had seen combat overseas.

Financing the war required innovation. The Liberty Loan Act allowed the federal government to sell war bonds, raising $23 billion. The new federal income tax, made possible by the Sixteenth Amendment (1913), provided additional revenue. To manage food and fuel, Wilson created federal agencies with sweeping powers: Herbert Hoover’s Food Administration promoted “Meatless Mondays” and “Wheatless Wednesdays,” while Harry Garfield’s Fuel Administration introduced “fuel holidays” and — for the first time in American history — daylight saving time.

George Creel
1876 – 1953
Former journalist appointed by Wilson to direct the Committee on Public Information. Creel deployed 75,000 “Four Minute Men” to speak in movie theaters, mobilized artists and filmmakers, and produced propaganda that cultivated a ferocious anti-German sentiment across the country.

Winning the war required public support, which Wilson was not willing to leave to chance. He created the Committee on Public Information (CPI) under journalist George Creel, deploying artists, filmmakers, and 75,000 volunteer speakers known as “Four Minute Men” who gave brief patriotic speeches in movie theaters nationwide. The campaign was effective — and ugly. German language classes were banned in some schools; restaurants renamed sauerkraut “liberty cabbage.”

The government also used law to suppress dissent. The Espionage Act of 1917 and the Sedition Act of 1918 made it a crime to criticize the government or the war effort. Over two thousand people were prosecuted; many received prison sentences of twenty years or more. Socialist Party leader Eugene Debs received ten years for encouraging draft resistance. In Schenck v. United States (1919), the Supreme Court upheld these laws unanimously, with Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes writing that wartime dissent presented a “clear and present danger.”

Reading Notes — Mobilization & Civil Liberties
The U.S. government used both propaganda and legal repression to manage public opinion during WWI. What does this episode reveal about the tension between national security and civil liberties? Does wartime change the rules — or reveal them?

23.3 A New Home Front

Labor, women, and African Americans — who gained, who was left behind

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how wartime labor shortages changed the position of organized labor
  • Describe how the lives of women and African Americans changed as a result of the war
  • Identify the connection between the war effort and the passage of the 18th and 19th Amendments

The war transformed the American home front in ways that outlasted the conflict. The massive mobilization of men into the military created severe labor shortages, giving workers and unions new leverage. Samuel Gompers of the AFL negotiated a “no-strike pledge” in exchange for the federal government’s protection of workers’ rights to organize and bargain collectively. The result: union membership surged from 2.6 million in 1916 to 4.1 million in 1919, and the eight-hour workday became standard in many industries. However, wages failed to keep pace with inflation, which ran 15–20 percent annually, meaning that real purchasing power actually declined for many workers even as conditions improved.

The 369th Infantry
“Harlem Hellfighters”
Served on the French front line for six months — longer than any other American unit. 171 men received the Legion of Merit. They returned home to a New York City parade and to a country that continued to deny them basic civil rights.

Women also entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers. More than one million women took jobs for the first time, and eight million working women found higher-paying positions, many in industries previously closed to them. Some drove trucks, repaired railroads, and worked on assembly lines. The Women’s Land Army deployed twenty thousand women to run farms in the absence of male workers. When the war ended and men returned, women were largely pushed out of these positions. But the experience of economic independence — however brief — proved difficult to erase. It helped build the political momentum for the Nineteenth Amendment, ratified in 1920.

African Americans experienced the war as both opportunity and betrayal. About 350,000 Black men served in the military, though in segregated units and often in menial or support roles. At home, the wartime labor shortage accelerated the Great Migration, drawing hundreds of thousands of Black workers north to fill industrial jobs. Yet racism followed them. White workers and residents responded to Black competition for jobs and housing with violence: in 1917 alone, there were race riots in twenty-five cities. Returning Black veterans, who had risked their lives for a democracy that denied them full citizenship, faced renewed violence and the hardening of Jim Crow. The “Red Summer” of 1919 brought twenty-five bloody race riots to Northern cities, with over 250 killed.

“Impress the negro with the fact that he is defending the flag, inflate his untutored soul with military airs, teach him that it is his duty to keep the emblem of the Nation flying triumphantly in the air — it is but a short step to the conclusion that his political rights must be respected.” — Senator James K. Vardaman of Mississippi, 1917 — arguing against Black military service

World War I created new opportunities for women and African Americans — yet both groups faced significant limits and backlash. What does this pattern reveal about how and when American society expands opportunity? Who benefits, and on what terms?

23.4 From War to Peace — Wilson’s Fourteen Points and the League of Nations

The dream of a new world order — and why the Senate killed it

Learning Objectives

  • Describe Wilson’s postwar vision as expressed in the Fourteen Points
  • Explain what the U.S. got at the Paris Peace Conference and what Wilson failed to achieve
  • Analyze why the Senate rejected the Treaty of Versailles and what that rejection meant

Wilson announced his postwar peace plan — the Fourteen Points — in January 1918, months before American troops fired their first shots in the war. The plan called for open diplomacy, free trade, national self-determination, and the creation of a League of Nations to resolve future conflicts through collective security rather than war. Wilson believed the United States’ entry into the war gave it the moral authority to reshape the world order on American principles.

The Paris Peace Conference of 1919 was a disappointment. Britain, France, and Italy arrived with different goals: territorial gains, reparations, revenge. The Treaty of Versailles imposed crushing reparations on Germany, stripped it of territory and colonies, and included the humiliating “war guilt clause” requiring Germany to accept sole responsibility for the war. Of Wilson’s Fourteen Points, only the League of Nations survived intact.

Henry Cabot Lodge
1850 – 1924
Republican chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and Wilson’s chief opponent on the League of Nations. Lodge’s “fourteen reservations” — a pointed rebuke of Wilson’s Fourteen Points — focused especially on Article X, which he feared would obligate the U.S. to intervene militarily in every future international dispute.

The Senate refused to ratify the treaty. Republican leader Henry Cabot Lodge feared that Article X of the League Covenant would drag the United States into every future conflict. Some senators were “Irreconcilables” who opposed any League under any conditions; others, “Reservationists,” might have accepted a modified version. Wilson, physically devastated by a stroke he suffered during a cross-country speaking tour, refused all compromise. The Senate defeated the treaty twice, in November 1919 and March 1920. The United States never joined the League of Nations — the very institution Wilson had created. He received the Nobel Peace Prize in October 1919 for his effort. It was cold comfort.

Reading Notes — Wilson’s Fourteen Points & the League
Wilson arrived at Paris as the most powerful man in the world and left with a deeply flawed treaty. What did he want, what did he get, and what does the Senate’s rejection of the League of Nations reveal about American attitudes toward international commitment?

23.5 Demobilization and Its Difficult Aftermath

The flu, the Red Scare, and the “Red Summer” — why 1919 nearly tore the country apart

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the multiple crises that converged in the United States in 1919
  • Explain the origins and consequences of the Red Scare
  • Analyze the racial violence of the “Red Summer” and what it revealed about postwar America

Rather than a triumphant return to peace, 1919 was a year of cascading crises. Returning soldiers brought home a new strain of influenza that would ultimately kill 675,000 Americans — more than the war itself. Inflation, running at nearly double the 1916 price level by year’s end, squeezed workers who had already seen their real wages fall during the war. More than four million workers went on strike in 1919 alone — a record that would stand for decades.

Fear of Communism — inflamed by the Bolshevik Revolution in Russia — produced the Red Scare. Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer, whose own house had been bombed, directed J. Edgar Hoover’s Bureau of Investigation to raid radical meeting houses. The “Palmer Raids” resulted in 4,000 arrests; nearly 250 people were deported on board a ship the press called “the Soviet Ark.” The raids targeted not just actual radicals but anyone associated with labor organizing, socialism, or immigrant politics.

Racial violence was equally severe. The “Red Summer” of 1919 saw twenty-five bloody race riots across Northern cities, killing over 250 people. White residents attacked Black neighborhoods in Chicago, Omaha, Washington, D.C., and elsewhere, responding to wartime migration with deadly violence. In 1921, the worst racial massacre in American history unfolded in Tulsa, Oklahoma, where white mobs destroyed the prosperous Black Greenwood District — “Black Wall Street” — killing an estimated 50 to 300 people. These events were omitted from many textbooks for decades.

Reading Notes — Postwar Upheaval
The immediate postwar period brought economic anxiety, anti-communist hysteria, and race violence rather than the liberal peace Wilson had promised. Why did 1919 produce fear and reaction rather than celebration? What does this reversal suggest about the fragility of wartime gains?

Week 4  ·  Synthesis

Connections Across the Reading

From isolationism to world power — and back again

Chapter 23 traces a rapid and painful arc: the United States entered the war committed to making the world safe for democracy, fought a war that required suppressing democracy at home, emerged victorious from the conflict, and then retreated from the very international commitments that victory had made possible. Wilson’s idealism collided with the realities of European power politics, Senate isolationism, and the sheer human cost of the war. The America that entered 1920 was exhausted, frightened, and deeply ambivalent about its new role as a world power.

The domestic story is equally contradictory. The war opened factory floors and military ranks to women and African Americans — and then closed them again when it was over. It produced the Nineteenth Amendment and the Red Summer in the same year. It built the largest government apparatus in American history and used it to prosecute people for expressing unpopular opinions. Understanding WWI means holding these contradictions together rather than resolving them.
