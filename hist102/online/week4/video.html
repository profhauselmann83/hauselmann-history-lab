About This Film

Abby Ginzberg & Ken Schneider — And Then They Came for Us (2017)

This award-winning documentary tells the story of the forced removal and incarceration of 120,000 Japanese Americans during World War II through the voices of survivors — including actor and activist George Takei, who was incarcerated as a child — and through newly rediscovered photographs by Dorothea Lange, many of which were suppressed by the U.S. government for decades. The film covers the decades of anti-Japanese discrimination that preceded Pearl Harbor, the signing of Executive Order 9066, life inside the camps, the loyalty questionnaire, Fred Korematsu's legal challenge, and the long road to the redress movement of the 1980s. It also connects this history to the present, following Japanese American activists speaking out against civil liberties restrictions on Muslim Americans. Winner of the American Bar Association's Silver Gavel Award.

As you watch, keep the week's central question in mind: Why did the United States government incarcerate Japanese Americans — and what does the answer reveal about democracy, race, and law in America? You will be working with five primary source documents on this question in the Canvas discussion. The Munson Report, The Crisis editorial, the Korematsu decision, and Personal Justice Denied will make much more sense after you've seen this film.

Questions to Hold as You Watch

These are not graded. They are designed to focus your attention on key moments in the film and to set up the close reading you'll do in the primary source discussion. After the film, you'll answer a short quiz before moving on.

~0:00–6:00 — Before Pearl Harbor

One survivor says: "What happened to us didn't just suddenly happen because of Pearl Harbor. There was 40 years of anti-Japanese movement from the early 1900s." The film opens with "yellow peril" — the idea that Asian people posed a threat to Western civilization. Why does it matter that incarceration had a 40-year backstory? Does that change how you understand Executive Order 9066 — was it a panicked wartime decision, or the culmination of something much older?

~6:00–15:00 — Registration, Removal, and Language

Notice the language the government used: "evacuation," "assembly centers," "relocation centers." One survivor pushes back directly: "This euphemistic language distorted what they were doing to us. Evacuation rather than forced removal. Assembly centers rather than temporary prison camps." Why does the choice of words matter? What does calling it "evacuation" instead of "forced removal" do — politically and historically?

~15:00–28:00 — Dorothea Lange, Ansel Adams, and Government Censorship

The government hired photographers to document incarceration — then impounded photographs that showed too much reality. Lange's photos were buried in the National Archives for decades. What does it mean that the government suppressed its own photographic record? Who gets to control the historical memory of an injustice — and what happens when that record is hidden?

~28:00–34:00 — The Loyalty Questionnaire and Tule Lake

One man's father answered "no, no" to the loyalty questionnaire — refusing to swear allegiance to a country that had imprisoned him. He was sent to Tule Lake maximum security camp. His daughter says he was not disloyal: "Disloyal, they weren't disloyal, they were outraged." What is the loyalty questionnaire really asking? Is it possible to be both patriotic and refuse to answer it the way the government demanded?

~34:00–42:00 — Korematsu and the Government's Lies

The film reveals that the government lied to the Supreme Court — fabricating evidence of Japanese American espionage and sabotage that never existed. FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover wrote that there was no illicit signaling from shore to ship. A Justice Department attorney called the government's statements "intentional falsehoods." If the Court upheld incarceration based on lies, what does that say about the reliability of "military necessity" as a justification for suspending civil rights?

~42:00–48:00 — Then and Now

The film ends by connecting Japanese American incarceration to post-9/11 restrictions on Muslim Americans. George Takei says: "National security was used when Pearl Harbor was bombed to incarcerate us. And national security is being brought up again." One activist describes a process: "They begin with the fomenting of hate... then slow targeting." Is the historical parallel the film draws convincing? What are the limits of comparing these two moments?

The Big Question — connecting to Haass

Richard Haass argues that citizens and leaders must "put country first." FDR cited national security when he signed EO 9066. The 442nd veterans served the country that imprisoned their families. Fred Korematsu refused to comply and spent 40 years fighting to clear his name. Karen Korematsu says her father would never accept a pardon because "a pardon means that you are guilty. And he was never guilty." Who in this film is "putting country first" — and does Haass's framework help you answer that, or make it harder?
