25.1 The Stock Market Crash of 1929

Black Tuesday and the structural weaknesses behind it

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the structural economic weaknesses that made the Depression possible
  • Explain what happened on Black Tuesday and why it triggered a broader collapse
  • Describe how the crash affected people beyond the small percentage who held stock

The stock market crash of October 1929 did not cause the Great Depression alone — but it was the trigger that exposed structural weaknesses already corroding the American economy. Only about 10 percent of American households held stock, yet nearly a third would lose their savings and jobs in the years that followed. The real problems ran deeper: the Federal Reserve had kept interest rates artificially low throughout the 1920s, inflating a decade of speculation; 80 percent of American families had virtually no savings; and wealth was so concentrated in so few hands that there were no new buyers to sustain the market once speculation collapsed.

The 1920s had brought a speculative frenzy. Buyers purchased stock on margin. Florida real estate, Ponzi schemes, and overvalued industrial stocks fed the bubble. On Black Tuesday, October 29, over sixteen million shares traded and stockholders lost $14 billion in wealth in a single day. Banks that had invested in stocks failed by the thousands; their depositors lost everything. Unemployment tripled from 1.5 million in 1929 to 4.5 million by the end of 1930 — and kept climbing toward 25 percent by 1933.

Herbert Hoover
1874 – 1964
31st president (1929–1933). An orphan who became a self-made millionaire, global humanitarian, and champion of “American individualism.” His refusal to provide direct federal relief during the Depression was not callousness — it was deeply held ideology. That ideology destroyed his presidency.

The contagion spread fast. With investors losing billions, new investment dried up. Auto production fell to 1919 levels. Not a single new hotel or theater was built in New York City for thirty years. The gross national product declined by over 25 percent within a year. By mid-1930, the slide into economic chaos had begun — but was nowhere near complete.

25.2 President Hoover’s Response

The limits of voluntarism and the ideology of individualism

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Hoover’s philosophy and how it shaped his response to the Depression
  • Identify what Hoover actually did — and why it was not enough
  • Describe the Bonus Army crisis and its political consequences

Hoover was not heartless — he had coordinated massive humanitarian relief in Belgium during WWI. But his response to the Depression was constrained by a sincere philosophical commitment to American individualism. He summoned business leaders to Washington and asked them to voluntarily maintain wages. He created agencies to coordinate private charity. He signed the Smoot-Hawley Tariff, which triggered retaliatory tariffs abroad and deepened the global depression. In 1932 he created the Reconstruction Finance Corporation, lending to banks but nothing to the people the banks had failed.

The Bonus Army marched on Washington in spring 1932. When Hoover ordered MacArthur to clear them out, soldiers burned their camps and tear-gassed the crowd. The spectacle sealed Hoover’s political fate. “Hoovervilles” of cardboard shantytowns spread across every city. He left office with one of the lowest approval ratings in American history.

25.3 The Depths of the Great Depression

Race, gender, region — the unequal geography of suffering

Learning Objectives

  • Describe how African Americans, farm families, and women experienced the Depression differently
  • Analyze the Dust Bowl as both environmental catastrophe and economic consequence
  • Explain the Scottsboro Boys case and what it revealed about Depression-era racial justice

African Americans faced unemployment at double and triple the rates of white Americans. By 1932 approximately half of all Black Americans were unemployed. White workers now competed for jobs previously considered beneath them — household servants, janitors — displacing Black workers entirely. Racial violence rose. The Scottsboro Boys case, in which nine Black teenagers were falsely convicted of rape in Alabama, exposed the brutality of the Southern legal system to national attention.

The Dust Bowl
1931 – 1939
A catastrophic drought compounded by decades of overplowing that stripped the Great Plains of native grasses. “Black blizzards” of topsoil buried homes and suffocated livestock. In 1935 alone, over 850 million tons of topsoil blew away — topsoil that takes nature 500 years per inch to regenerate. The Dust Bowl drove 3.5 million people from the land.

Farmers suffered acutely. Between 1930 and 1935, nearly 750,000 family farms disappeared through foreclosure. The Dust Bowl transformed the agricultural heartland into a wasteland. Farmers who had plowed under native grasses during WWI-era high prices had stripped the land of its natural windbreak. When drought hit, the soil blew away. “Okies” drove west to California, only to find exploitation waiting. Steinbeck’s The Grapes of Wrath immortalized their exodus. The Depression also shifted cultural values from individual success toward community solidarity, creating the political conditions for the New Deal.

25.4 Assessing Hoover — and the Road to FDR

How the Depression shifted American values and opened the door for the New Deal

Learning Objectives

  • Evaluate Hoover’s presidency fairly and critically
  • Explain how the Depression shifted American cultural values
  • Identify why FDR’s election in 1932 was nearly inevitable

American culture responded to the Depression by shifting from individual success to community solidarity. Frank Capra’s films — Mr. Smith Goes to Washington (1939) — celebrated the ordinary man standing against corrupt power. Steinbeck’s The Grapes of Wrath followed displaced Oklahomans whose dignity survived even as the system failed them. The Gilded Age myth of the self-made man who deserved his success gave way to a harder truth: that forces larger than any individual could destroy the hardworking and the virtuous alike.

Hoover’s foreign policy record was more creditable: he helped lay the groundwork for the “Good Neighbor” policy with Latin America and articulated the Hoover-Stimson Doctrine refusing to recognize Japanese territorial conquest. None of it mattered to the millions who were starving. By 1932, one in four workers was unemployed. Franklin Roosevelt won the election with 57 percent of the popular vote. The era of active federal government was about to begin.

“Democracy is a harsh employer.” — Herbert Hoover, reflecting on his 1932 defeat
