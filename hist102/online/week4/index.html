Week 4  ·  March 30 – April 5

World Wars and Their Impact

From the trenches of the Western Front to the atomic clouds over Japan — thirty years that remade America

Mar 30 – Apr 5 190 pts Midterm Exam

This week covers the thirty-year arc from the United States’ entry into World War I through the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki that ended World War II. Along the way, you’ll encounter the Great Depression that connected the two conflicts, the propaganda and civil liberties crises that accompanied each war, and the persistent gap between America’s democratic ideals and its treatment of minority citizens at home. Richard Haass’s argument that citizens must “put country first” takes on new dimensions when you ask: whose country, and on what terms?

This week also includes your Midterm Exam, which covers all material from Weeks 1–4. Complete all readings and source analyses before sitting the exam.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain why American neutrality was unsustainable in WWI
  • Analyze the domestic costs of WWI: propaganda, civil liberties, race
  • Trace the structural causes of the Great Depression and Hoover’s response
  • Describe the unequal impact of the Depression by race, gender, and region
  • Explain the road from isolationism to WWII engagement
  • Evaluate Executive Order 9066 and the internment of Japanese Americans
  • Assess the decision to use atomic weapons against Japan
  • Analyze primary sources on the League of Nations and the Dust Bowl
Central Question

“The United States fought two world wars for democracy while limiting democratic rights at home. Is this hypocrisy, necessity, or something more complicated?”

This question threads through all three readings and both source analyses. The synthesis question in Reading 5.3 asks you to return to it directly.

Haass Connection — Ch. 10 “Put Country First”: Wilson, FDR, Henry Cabot Lodge, and Japanese American 442nd veterans all believed they were putting country first. What does that principle actually mean?
