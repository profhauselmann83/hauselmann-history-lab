16.3 Radical Reconstruction, 1867–1872

Military rule, Black political participation, and the Fifteenth Amendment

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the main provisions of the Military Reconstruction Acts of 1867
  • Describe the role of African Americans in Southern politics during Radical Reconstruction
  • Analyze the significance and limits of the Fifteenth Amendment
  • Explain why Johnson was impeached and what the outcome revealed about the separation of powers

The refusal of most former Confederate states to ratify the Fourteenth Amendment gave Radical Republicans in Congress the justification they needed to take direct control of Reconstruction. Beginning in March 1867, Congress passed a series of Military Reconstruction Acts that fundamentally remade the political landscape of the South. The legislation divided the former Confederacy into five military districts, each commanded by a Union general. To be readmitted to the Union, Southern states were required to draft new constitutions granting Black men the right to vote, ratify the Fourteenth Amendment, and submit their new constitutions for congressional approval.

The Military Districts and New State Constitutions

Under military oversight, Southern states held constitutional conventions that for the first time included Black delegates alongside white Unionists and Northern migrants — derided by opponents as "carpetbaggers" and "scalawags." The new state constitutions these conventions produced were among the most progressive documents in American history at the time. They established public school systems for the first time in many Southern states, expanded rights for women in property ownership, and abolished imprisonment for debt. The constitutions required ratification of the Fourteenth Amendment as a condition of readmission.

Johnson continued to obstruct Reconstruction at every turn. In August 1867 he attempted to remove Secretary of War Edwin Stanton, a Radical Republican ally, in violation of the Tenure of Office Act (which prohibited the president from removing Senate-confirmed officials without congressional approval). The House of Representatives responded in February 1868 by voting to impeach Johnson — the first presidential impeachment in American history. The trial in the Senate lasted eleven weeks. Johnson was acquitted by a single vote, falling one short of the two-thirds required for removal from office. His political power, however, was effectively destroyed. Republican Ulysses S. Grant won the presidency in November 1868, and Radical Reconstruction continued.

African American Political Participation

Hiram Revels
1827 – 1901
In 1870, Revels became the first African American to serve in the United States Senate, representing Mississippi — the state that had produced Jefferson Davis, president of the Confederacy. His election symbolized the dramatic transformation wrought by Radical Reconstruction.

With the protection of federal troops and the franchise now legally guaranteed, African American men in the South voted in large numbers and quickly became a powerful force in Southern politics. Between 1867 and 1877, more than 600 Black men served in Southern state legislatures. Fourteen Black congressmen were elected to the U.S. House of Representatives, and two — Hiram Revels and Blanche Bruce, both of Mississippi — served in the U.S. Senate. These officeholders pushed for public education, civil rights legislation, and economic development.

Black political power during Reconstruction was real but constrained. African Americans never controlled any Southern state government outright, and their officeholding was always dependent on the continued presence of federal troops and the political will to enforce Reconstruction policy. White conservatives organized systematic campaigns of fraud and violence to suppress Black votes wherever possible, even during the height of Radical Reconstruction.

The Fifteenth Amendment

To place Black male suffrage on a constitutional footing that could not be undone by future legislation, Congress passed the Fifteenth Amendment in February 1869; it was ratified in March 1870. The amendment prohibited the federal government and the states from denying the right to vote "on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." It was hailed by Frederick Douglass as "the culmination of our movement" and celebrated with parades and church services across the country.

Yet the amendment contained significant gaps that white supremacists would exploit within years. It said nothing about poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, or intimidation — the tools that would be deployed after Reconstruction's collapse to systematically disenfranchise Black voters for nearly a century. Women's suffrage advocates, including Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Susan B. Anthony, bitterly opposed the amendment because it extended the vote to Black men while leaving women of all races still disenfranchised. The amendment's passage created a sharp rupture in the alliance between the abolitionist and women's rights movements.

The KKK and the Limits of Federal Power

Even as Black men gained formal political rights, white supremacist organizations moved to terrorize them into submission. The Ku Klux Klan, founded in Pulaski, Tennessee in 1865 by former Confederate General Nathan Bedford Forrest, spread rapidly across the South. Klan members — operating in disguise, usually at night — attacked, beat, and murdered Black men and women, Republican officeholders, Black church and school leaders, and anyone else who challenged white supremacy. The Klan was not a fringe organization: it operated with the tacit support or active participation of many local law enforcement officials and Democratic politicians.

Congress responded with the Enforcement Acts (1870–1871), also known as the Ku Klux Klan Acts, which made it a federal crime to use force, intimidation, or threats to prevent citizens from voting, holding office, or enjoying equal protection under law. President Grant used the acts aggressively: he suspended habeas corpus in nine South Carolina counties and sent federal troops to arrest hundreds of Klan members. The prosecutions, led by Attorney General Amos Akerman, were largely successful in suppressing organized Klan activity by 1872. But the underlying conditions — white supremacist determination to restore racial hierarchy, combined with limited federal capacity and declining Northern political will — remained.

16.4 The Collapse of Reconstruction, 1873–1877

Economic depression, Northern fatigue, and the end of federal protection

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the economic and political factors that weakened Northern support for Reconstruction
  • Describe how "Redeemer" Democrats used violence and fraud to reclaim Southern state governments
  • Analyze the Compromise of 1877 and its consequences for Black Southerners
  • Evaluate the long-term legacy of Reconstruction

By the early 1870s, the political will to sustain Reconstruction was fading in the North. The Liberal Republican movement of 1872 criticized the corruption scandals of the Grant administration — including the Crédit Mobilier railroad scandal — and called for an end to Reconstruction in the name of "reconciliation." The economic panic triggered by the collapse of Jay Cooke & Company's bank in September 1873 sent the country into a severe depression lasting five years. Unemployment spread, and Northern voters turned their attention from Southern racial justice to economic survival.

Redeemer Democrats and the Return of White Supremacy

Southern Democrats, calling themselves "Redeemers," seized the opportunity. Using a combination of electoral fraud, economic coercion of Black workers and tenants, and outright paramilitary violence, they systematically dismantled Reconstruction state governments. White rifle clubs and paramilitary groups such as the White League in Louisiana and the Red Shirts in South Carolina openly campaigned through intimidation and murder. In the Colfax Massacre of April 1873, a white mob killed approximately 150 Black men — most of them after surrendering — in the bloodiest single act of political violence in Reconstruction history. In the Hamburg Massacre of 1876, Red Shirts murdered members of a Black militia company in South Carolina.

Federal courts began to narrow the scope of Reconstruction legislation. In United States v. Cruikshank (1876), the Supreme Court ruled that the Fourteenth Amendment protected citizens only from state action, not from violence by private individuals — effectively stripping the federal government of the power to prosecute the perpetrators of the Colfax Massacre. The ruling devastated the federal government's ability to protect Black Southerners from white supremacist terror.

The Compromise of 1877

The presidential election of 1876 brought Reconstruction to its formal end. Republican Rutherford B. Hayes and Democrat Samuel Tilden both claimed victory in a disputed electoral count hinging on contested returns from Florida, Louisiana, and South Carolina — the three Southern states still under Republican Reconstruction governments. Congress established a special Electoral Commission, which awarded all disputed electoral votes to Hayes along strict party lines.

In the backroom negotiations known as the Compromise of 1877, Hayes agreed to withdraw the remaining federal troops from the South in exchange for Democratic acceptance of his election. The deal ended Reconstruction as a national policy. Within weeks, federal troops departed, and the last Republican Reconstruction governments in Florida, Louisiana, and South Carolina collapsed. "Redeemer" Democrats swept into power. Black political officeholders were removed, replaced with white Democrats. The Freedmen's Bureau had already been dissolved. The constitutional amendments remained on paper, but the machinery to enforce them was gone.

The Legacy of Reconstruction

The end of Reconstruction inaugurated a period of systematic racial oppression that would last nearly a century. Southern states enacted Jim Crow laws mandating racial segregation in every aspect of public life. Poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and outright violence stripped Black men of the vote they had exercised during Reconstruction. Sharecropping and tenant farming systems bound Black families to cycles of debt that replicated many of the conditions of slavery. Lynching became a systematic tool of racial terror: between 1877 and 1950, more than 4,000 Black people were lynched in the South.

Historians debate whether Reconstruction failed or was destroyed. The constitutional amendments it produced — the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth — lay dormant but intact, waiting to be invoked by the Civil Rights Movement nearly a century later. The schools, churches, and institutions built during Reconstruction by African Americans survived as foundations for future struggles. "The slave went free," the historian W.E.B. Du Bois wrote, "stood a brief moment in the sun; then moved back again toward slavery." That "brief moment" transformed the Constitution and established the legal framework on which the unfinished work of American democracy would eventually rest.

Why Reconstruction Ended: A Synthesis

The collapse of Reconstruction resulted from a convergence of forces: Northern economic depression and political fatigue; the systematic violence and fraud of Redeemer Democrats; Supreme Court rulings that gutted federal enforcement power; the death or retirement of key Radical Republican leaders; and the fundamental unwillingness of the Northern public to sustain an indefinite military occupation of the South in the name of Black civil rights. Reconstruction's failure was not inevitable — it required active effort by many people to destroy it. But neither was it surprising: it was attempting something without precedent in world history, transforming a slave society into a multiracial democracy within a generation, against fierce resistance, without the sustained political will to see it through.

What Reconstruction left behind, however, was not nothing. The Reconstruction amendments would eventually serve as the constitutional foundation for the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965. The HBCUs founded during Reconstruction educated generations of Black leaders. The vision of a genuinely multiracial democracy, glimpsed during the brief years of Radical Reconstruction, survived as an aspiration. "If the Negro is to be a man, full and complete," Frederick Douglass wrote, "he must be allowed to take his chances with other men." That chance was not given during Reconstruction. But the demand did not disappear.
