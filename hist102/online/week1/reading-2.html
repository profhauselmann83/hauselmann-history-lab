16.3 Radical Reconstruction, 1867–1872

Congressional Republicans seize control of Reconstruction

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of the second phase of Reconstruction and key legislation put forward by Congress
  • Describe the impeachment of President Johnson
  • Discuss the benefits and drawbacks of the Fifteenth Amendment

During the Congressional election in the fall of 1866, Republicans gained even greater victories, due in large measure to northern voter opposition toward President Johnson because of his inflexible attitude and missteps during his speaking tour. Leading Radical Republicans included Massachusetts senator Charles Sumner and Pennsylvania representative Thaddeus Stevens. Stevens considered that the Southern states had forfeited their rights as states when they seceded, and were no more than conquered territory that the federal government could organize as it wished. He envisioned the redistribution of plantation lands and U.S. military control over the former Confederacy.

The Reconstruction Acts

The 1867 Military Reconstruction Act set a new direction for Reconstruction in the South. The act divided the ten Southern states that had yet to ratify the Fourteenth Amendment into five military districts. Martial law was imposed, and a Union general commanded each district with twenty thousand federal troops charged with protecting freed people. When a supplementary act extended the right to vote to all freed men of voting age, the military in each district oversaw elections and voter registration. Only after new state constitutions had been written and states had ratified the Fourteenth Amendment could they rejoin the Union. Predictably, President Johnson vetoed the Reconstruction Acts — and just as predictably, Congress overrode his vetoes.

The Impeachment of President Johnson

To prevent Johnson from interfering in congressional Reconstruction, Radical Republicans passed the Tenure of Office Act in 1867, requiring the president to gain Senate approval whenever he appointed or removed officials. In August 1867, Johnson removed Secretary of War Edwin M. Stanton without gaining Senate approval. The House of Representatives quickly drafted a resolution to impeach him — a first in American history. In the Senate, Johnson barely survived: the final vote was 35 to 19, one vote short of the required two-thirds majority. Though Johnson survived, the events effectively silenced him for the remainder of his term.

The Fifteenth Amendment & Women's Suffrage

Hiram Revels
1827 – 1901
A freeborn man from North Carolina, Revels rose to prominence as a minister before becoming the first African American elected to the U.S. Senate, representing Mississippi in 1870.

In the winter of 1869, Republicans introduced the Fifteenth Amendment, which directed that "[t]he right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." The amendment was ratified in 1870.

However, the amendment had weaknesses. Drafters specifically excluded language addressing literacy tests and poll taxes — the most common ways Black people were disenfranchised. Leading women's rights activists, including Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton, saw the amendment as a major disappointment, as it failed to extend the franchise to women. The consequence was the effective split of a civil rights movement that had once been united. In 1869, Stanton and Anthony organized the National Woman Suffrage Association (NWSA). When Virginia Minor tried to register to vote in Missouri in 1872, the Supreme Court ultimately declared in 1874 that the Constitution did not confer the right of suffrage upon anyone, dismissing her claim.

Nevertheless, the Fifteenth Amendment brought real political gains. Black voter registration surged, and Union Leagues worked to enroll newly enfranchised voters across the South. In the 1870s, fifteen Black members of the House of Representatives and two Black senators served in Congress. Hundreds of Black people served at the local and state levels as sheriffs, judges, school board commissioners, and city council members. Reconstruction governments invested in public education, infrastructure, hospitals, and orphanages.

16.4 The Collapse of Reconstruction

Terror, politics, and the betrayal of Reconstruction's promise

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the reasons for the collapse of Reconstruction
  • Describe the efforts of white southern "redeemers" to roll back the gains of Reconstruction

Sharecropping

The end of slavery meant a transition to wage labor, but this conversion did not bring economic independence for formerly enslaved people. Under the crop-lien system, store owners extended credit to farmers under the agreement that debtors would pay with a portion of their future harvest at high interest rates.

Throughout the South, sharecropping took root. Freed people rented the land they worked — often the same plantations where they had been enslaved — paying landlords with as much as half their crops. Sharecroppers often became trapped in a never-ending cycle of debt, unable to buy their own land. The consequences affected the entire South for many generations, severely limiting economic development and ensuring that the South remained an agricultural backwater.

The Ku Klux Klan

The Ku Klux Klan was founded in 1866 as an oath-bound fraternal order of Confederate veterans in Tennessee, with former Confederate General Nathan Bedford Forrest as its first leader. It quickly evolved into a vigilante terrorist group that vented white southerners' collective frustration over the loss of the war through intimidation and violence. Other anti-Black vigilante groups — the Red Shirts, the Knights of the White Camelia, and the White League — also sprang up at this time, often operating as military wings of the Democratic Party.

The Klan terrorized newly freed Black people to deter them from exercising their citizenship rights. It burned public schools to prevent freed people from attaining an education, murdered Black Republican officials, and used violence to stop Black citizens from voting. Congress passed a series of Enforcement Acts in 1870 and 1871 to outlaw Klan intimidation, and President Grant imposed martial law in parts of South Carolina. But federal efforts waned after 1872.

The Compromise of 1877 and the End of Reconstruction

Three major events undermined Republican control in the 1870s: a severe economic depression beginning in 1873, an internal split in the Republican Party between mainstream Republicans and "Liberal Republicans" who argued for downsizing Reconstruction, and a series of scandals that mired the Grant administration — including the "Whiskey Ring" and the Crédit Mobilier affair.

By the 1876 presidential election, Reconstruction had come to an end in most Southern states. The election between Republican Rutherford B. Hayes and Democrat Samuel Tilden produced disputed returns from four states. A special electoral commission awarded the presidency to Hayes on a party-line vote. In what became known as the Compromise of 1877, Republican leaders agreed to withdraw all remaining federal troops from the South. Democrats took over the remaining Southern states, creating the Solid South. Hayes believed that Southern leaders would obey and enforce the Reconstruction-era constitutional amendments. His trust was soon proved to be misguided.

"Those who worked to change and modernize the South typically did so under threats of violence. Black Republican officials in the South were frequently terrorized, assaulted, and even murdered with impunity." — OpenStax, U.S. History, Chapter 16
