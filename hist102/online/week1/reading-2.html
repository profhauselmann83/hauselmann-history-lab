16.3 Radical Reconstruction, 1867–1872

Congressional Republicans seize control — and Black Americans enter political life

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of the Military Reconstruction Acts and what they required of Southern states
  • Describe the impeachment of President Johnson and its significance
  • Analyze the gains and limitations of the Fifteenth Amendment
  • Describe the achievements of Black political participation during Reconstruction

During the Congressional elections of fall 1866, Republicans gained even larger majorities in both houses, due in large part to Northern voter anger at Johnson's inflexibility, his reckless speaking tour, and the clear evidence that Southern states were using his lenient Reconstruction to restore white supremacy through Black Codes and violence. Leading Radical Republicans included Massachusetts senator Charles Sumner — who had been beaten unconscious on the Senate floor by a pro-slavery congressman in 1856 — and Pennsylvania representative Thaddeus Stevens, one of the most uncompromising advocates for racial equality in American political history. Stevens believed the Southern states had forfeited their rights as states when they seceded and were now conquered territory subject to whatever terms Congress chose to impose. He envisioned the redistribution of plantation lands and sustained U.S. military control over the former Confederacy until genuine change had taken root.

The Reconstruction Acts

The Military Reconstruction Act of 1867 set an entirely new direction for Reconstruction, bypassing Johnson's approach entirely. The act divided the ten Southern states that had yet to ratify the Fourteenth Amendment into five military districts, each commanded by a Union general with roughly twenty thousand federal troops charged with maintaining order and protecting freed people. Martial law replaced civilian government. The act required Southern states to hold constitutional conventions with delegates elected by universal male suffrage — including Black men — draft new state constitutions guaranteeing Black voting rights, ratify the Fourteenth Amendment, and have their new governments approved by Congress before they could be readmitted to the Union.

Supplementary Reconstruction Acts in 1867 further extended the right to vote to all freed men of voting age and gave military commanders power to remove disloyal state officials. The military oversaw voter registration throughout the South, and for the first time in American history, Black men in former Confederate states were registered to vote in large numbers. In several states, newly registered Black voters outnumbered white registered voters. Predictably, President Johnson vetoed the Reconstruction Acts — and just as predictably, Congress overrode his vetoes.

The Impeachment of President Johnson

To prevent Johnson from undercutting congressional Reconstruction through his power to appoint and remove officials, Radical Republicans passed the Tenure of Office Act in 1867. The act required the president to gain Senate approval before removing any official whose appointment had required Senate confirmation. It was explicitly designed to protect Secretary of War Edwin M. Stanton, a holdover from Lincoln's cabinet who was sympathetic to the Radical Republican agenda and gave Congress a critical voice inside the executive branch.

In August 1867, Johnson defied the act by suspending Stanton and appointing General Ulysses Grant as an interim secretary of war. When the Senate refused to concur with Stanton's removal, Johnson formally fired him in February 1868. The House of Representatives responded within three days, drafting articles of impeachment — a first in American history. The charges centered on Johnson's violation of the Tenure of Office Act, but the underlying issue was his systematic obstruction of congressional Reconstruction. In the Senate trial, Johnson barely survived: the final vote was 35 to 19 in favor of conviction, one vote short of the required two-thirds majority. The acquittal was secured partly through deal-making with Republican moderates who worried about the precedent of removing a president over policy disagreements. Though Johnson survived, he was politically destroyed for the remainder of his term and played no further role in shaping Reconstruction policy.

The Fifteenth Amendment and Black Political Life

Hiram Revels
1827 – 1901
A freeborn man from North Carolina, Revels rose to prominence as a minister before becoming the first African American elected to the U.S. Senate, representing Mississippi in 1870. He filled the seat that had been vacated by Jefferson Davis when Davis left to lead the Confederacy.

In the winter of 1869, Republicans introduced the Fifteenth Amendment, which directed that "[t]he right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." The amendment was ratified in 1870. Republicans celebrated it as the completion of the work begun by the Thirteenth and Fourteenth Amendments, creating a constitutional trilogy that dismantled the legal architecture of slavery.

The amendment had significant weaknesses, however, that its drafters knew and accepted. It did not prohibit literacy tests, poll taxes, grandfather clauses, or other mechanisms that had nothing to do with race explicitly but were used almost exclusively against Black voters. Radical Republicans accepted these gaps as the political price of passing anything at all — several Northern states also used literacy tests to restrict immigrant voting and would not have ratified an amendment that prohibited them. Leading women's rights activists, including Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton, saw the amendment as a betrayal: it explicitly enfranchised Black men but said nothing about women of any race. The result was a bitter split in the reform coalition. In 1869, Stanton and Anthony organized the National Woman Suffrage Association (NWSA), separating from the more moderate American Woman Suffrage Association, which supported the amendment. When Virginia Minor sued for her right to vote in Missouri in 1872, the Supreme Court ruled in 1874 that the Constitution did not confer suffrage on anyone — it merely prohibited specific discriminatory denials of a right that states themselves had to grant.

Despite its limitations, the Fifteenth Amendment produced remarkable and rapid political change. Black voter registration surged across the South, aided by Union Leagues that organized Republican political clubs in Black communities, distributed information, and provided mutual protection at the polls. In the 1870s, fifteen Black members served in the U.S. House of Representatives and two — Hiram Revels and Blanche K. Bruce of Mississippi — served in the Senate. Hundreds more Black men served as state legislators, judges, sheriffs, school board commissioners, and city council members throughout the former Confederacy. Reconstruction state governments, now racially integrated for the first time, funded public school systems, built hospitals and orphanages, and invested in railroad and infrastructure development.

16.4 The Collapse of Reconstruction

Terror, economic crisis, and the political abandonment of freed people

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how sharecropping and the crop-lien system perpetuated economic dependency after emancipation
  • Describe the role of the Ku Klux Klan and other paramilitary groups in dismantling Reconstruction
  • Analyze the political, economic, and moral dimensions of Reconstruction's collapse

Sharecropping and Economic Dependency

The end of slavery created an immediate labor crisis throughout the South. Formerly enslaved people refused to work in the gang-labor system of the plantation, associating it directly with slavery. White landowners, stripped of their enslaved workforce but still holding their land, needed a new arrangement. The result was the sharecropping system, which gave freed people the appearance of independence while largely perpetuating their economic dependence.

Under sharecropping, freed people rented land — often the same plantations where they had been enslaved — paying landlords with a share of their crops, often as much as half the harvest. In theory, sharecroppers could accumulate savings over time and eventually purchase their own land. In practice, the crop-lien system made this nearly impossible. Store owners extended credit to sharecroppers for seeds, tools, food, and supplies at interest rates that could reach 50 to 70 percent annually, secured by a lien on the future crop. At harvest time, sharecroppers rarely earned enough to cover their debts, let alone save anything. They were legally bound to the same creditor — often the same landowner — for the following year's credit, and their options for leaving were severely restricted. Many sharecroppers found themselves deeper in debt each year, effectively immobilized on land they would never own.

The consequences were generational. Without land ownership, Black families in the South could not build wealth, could not use land as collateral for loans, and could not invest in education or business. The sharecropping system ensured that the South remained a low-wage, agricultural economy long after other regions had industrialized — devastating not just for Black Southerners but for the entire regional economy for generations.

The Ku Klux Klan and the Counter-Revolution

White resistance to Reconstruction was not limited to economic exploitation. From the beginning of Congressional Reconstruction, paramilitary violence was the primary instrument through which white Southerners sought to dismantle the new political order. The Ku Klux Klan was founded in Pulaski, Tennessee in 1866 as an oath-bound fraternal organization of Confederate veterans; former Confederate General Nathan Bedford Forrest served as its first national leader, or "Grand Wizard." Within two years it had transformed into a terrorist organization whose explicit purpose was the violent suppression of Black political participation and the destruction of Republican government throughout the South.

Klan members and allied groups — the Red Shirts in the Carolinas, the White League in Louisiana, the Knights of the White Camelia — operated largely as the military wing of the Democratic Party. Their tactics were systematic and calculated. They murdered Black Republican officials, burned schools built by the Freedmen's Bureau to prevent Black education, attacked Union League meetings, and threatened or killed Black voters and anyone who helped them register. The goal was not random racial terror but the specific dismantling of Reconstruction governments: by eliminating Black voters, driving off Republican officeholders, and intimidating white Republican "scalawags" and Northern "carpetbaggers," these groups could restore Democratic control and with it the racial hierarchy Reconstruction had challenged.

Primary Source — Abram Colby, 1871
"On the 29th of October, they came to my house and broke my door open, took me out of my bed and took me to the woods and whipped me three hours or more and left me in the woods for dead. They said to me, 'Do you think you will ever vote another damned Radical ticket?' . . . If there was an election to-morrow, I would vote the Radical ticket."
— Testimony of Abram Colby, Georgia state legislator, before the Joint Select Committee, Atlanta, Georgia, October 27, 1871

Congress responded with the Enforcement Acts of 1870 and 1871, which made it a federal crime to interfere with voting rights and gave the president power to suspend habeas corpus in areas dominated by Klan terrorism. President Grant imposed martial law in nine counties of South Carolina and arrested hundreds of Klan members. Federal prosecutions broke up organized Klan activity in many areas. But by 1874, federal will was fading: the Justice Department cut funding for Reconstruction enforcement, Northern public opinion turned against prolonged military occupation, and paramilitary violence continued under different organizational names. The federal government had demonstrated it could suppress the Klan when it chose to — but it would not sustain that effort.

The Compromise of 1877 and the End of Reconstruction

Three converging forces undermined Republican commitment to Reconstruction through the early 1870s. First, a severe economic depression beginning with the Panic of 1873 devastated Northern workers, shifting public focus away from Southern policy and toward economic recovery. Second, a growing faction of "Liberal Republicans" broke from the party mainstream, arguing that Reconstruction had gone on too long, that the South should be left to govern itself, and that the focus on Black rights was displacing attention to economic reform and anti-corruption efforts. Third, a cascade of scandals in the Grant administration — the Whiskey Ring, in which Treasury officials embezzled millions from whiskey tax revenues, and the Crédit Mobilier affair, linking top Republicans to railroad bribery — eroded public trust in the party that had championed Reconstruction.

By the 1876 presidential election, Reconstruction had already collapsed in most Southern states through a combination of terror and Democratic electoral victories. The presidential contest between Republican Rutherford B. Hayes and Democrat Samuel Tilden ended in disputed electoral returns from Florida, Louisiana, South Carolina, and Oregon. A special electoral commission created by Congress awarded all disputed electoral votes — and the presidency — to Hayes on a straight party-line vote of 8 to 7.

The resolution came through the Compromise of 1877: in exchange for Democratic acceptance of Hayes's election, Republican leaders agreed to withdraw all remaining federal troops from the South. The last Reconstruction governments in South Carolina and Louisiana fell within weeks. Democrats swept to power across the former Confederacy, creating the Solid South that would remain monolithically Democratic for nearly a century. Hayes believed in good faith that Southern Democratic leaders would honor the constitutional amendments and protect Black civil rights. Within years, systematic disenfranchisement, segregation laws, and racial terror had effectively nullified everything Reconstruction had achieved. The promises of the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments would not be meaningfully enforced until the Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 1960s.

"Those who worked to change and modernize the South typically did so under threats of violence. Black Republican officials in the South were frequently terrorized, assaulted, and even murdered with impunity." — OpenStax, U.S. History, Chapter 16
