About This Lecture

Week 5 — The Civil Rights Movement

This lecture covers the thirty-year arc from the end of World War II through the Stonewall Riots of 1969. You’ll get an overview of postwar affluence and its racial limits, the legal and grassroots strategies that drove the early civil rights movement, the Kennedy administration’s cautious approach to civil rights, and the emergence of movements that expanded the definition of who deserved equal treatment under the law.

Pay particular attention to the tension between legal progress and lived reality — why did Brown v. Board require federal troops to enforce? Why did JFK move slowly despite his rhetoric? And what does the Stonewall uprising tell us about who gets left out of official reform? These tensions run through both readings and both source analyses this week.

As you watch, keep the week’s central question in mind: The postwar United States promised opportunity and freedom — but for whom? What strategies did excluded groups use to claim what democracy promised them?

After the Lecture Continue to Reading 6.1 (OpenStax Ch. 28.3–28.5) to begin working through the 1950s content in detail. The reading builds directly on what you’ve seen in the lecture.
