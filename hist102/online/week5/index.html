Week 5  ·  April 6 – 12

The Civil Rights Movement

From Levittown to Little Rock to Stonewall — who the postwar promise included, and who it left behind

Apr 6 – 12 90 pts Discussion + Notes

The United States emerged from World War II as the most powerful nation on earth, and in the 1950s it turned its energy inward — building suburbs, buying televisions, raising the baby boom. The prosperity was real. But it was distributed unequally, and the gap between America’s democratic ideals and its racial realities could not hold. This week traces the effort to close that gap: from the first legal victories against segregation through the grassroots activism of the Montgomery Bus Boycott and the Little Rock crisis, to the promises and limits of the Kennedy years, and finally to the Stonewall Riots of 1969 — a different kind of civil rights uprising that would launch a new movement.

Richard Haass’s argument in Chapter 5 that citizens must “reject violence” and instead pursue change through civil disobedience takes on new dimensions this week. Rosa Parks, John Lewis, Martin Luther King Jr., and the patrons of the Stonewall Inn all faced a choice about how to respond to injustice. Their answers were different — and each was shaped by what they believed democracy owed them.

Learning Objectives
  • Describe postwar suburbanization and its racial dimensions
  • Explain how rock and roll and television reflected 1950s cultural tensions
  • Trace the legal and grassroots strategies of the early civil rights movement
  • Analyze the gap between JFK’s civil rights rhetoric and his actions
  • Evaluate John Lewis’s criticism of the Kennedy administration
  • Explain the origins and significance of the Stonewall Riots
  • Analyze how Haass’s concept of nonviolent resistance applies to these movements
Central Question

“The postwar United States promised opportunity and freedom — but for whom? What strategies did excluded groups use to claim what democracy promised them?”

This question runs through both readings and both source analyses. Keep it in mind as you encounter Levittown’s racial covenants, the Montgomery Bus Boycott, JFK’s cautious civil rights record, and the Stonewall patrons who had nothing left to lose.

Haass Connection — Ch. 5 “Reject Violence”: Haass argues nonviolent civil disobedience is the legitimate path to change. But was that path equally available to everyone in these movements? What does it require from those who use it?
