28.3 The American Dream

Eisenhower, suburbia, and the limits of postwar prosperity

Learning Objectives

  • Describe President Eisenhower’s domestic and foreign policies and his leadership style
  • Explain how suburban growth transformed American society after WWII
  • Identify who was included in — and excluded from — the postwar American Dream

Against the backdrop of the Cold War, Americans dedicated themselves to building a peaceful and prosperous society after the deprivation and instability of the Great Depression and World War II. Dwight D. Eisenhower, the general who led the United States to victory in Europe in 1945, proved to be the perfect president for the new era. Lacking strong conservative positions, he steered a middle path between conservatism and liberalism, and presided over a peacetime decade of economic growth and social conformity. In foreign affairs, Eisenhower’s New Look policy simultaneously expanded the nation’s nuclear arsenal and kept conventional defense spending in check.

Eisenhower’s approach to the presidency reflected his military background. He was calm, delegated authority to cabinet members on domestic affairs, and consistently sought a middle ground. He strove to balance the federal budget but retained much of the New Deal and even expanded Social Security. He maintained high defense spending but, in his farewell speech in 1961, warned about the growth of the military-industrial complex, the web of relationships between Defense Department officials and defense industry executives who all benefited from increases in spending.

William Levitt
1907 – 1994
Developer who pioneered mass-produced suburban housing. His Levittown communities in New York, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania sold thousands of identical homes for $8,000 each. Levitt used restrictive racial covenants to ensure only White families could purchase them — practices upheld by the federal mortgage system.

The postwar era brought dramatic suburban growth. In 1940, the rate of homeownership in the United States was 43.6 percent. By 1960, it was almost 62 percent. Unused land on the fringes of American cities provided the perfect place for new housing. Developers like William Levitt used prefabricated construction techniques to build thousands of inexpensive homes rapidly. The first day Levitt’s houses were offered for sale, more than one thousand were purchased. But Levittown homes were similar in one crucial way beyond their architecture: Levitt used restrictive covenants in his agreements to ensure that only White families could live there.

The suburbs grew by 46 percent between 1950 and 1960. This growth reshaped American life in profound ways. Suburban development increased reliance on the automobile; annual production of passenger cars leaped from 2.2 million to 8 million between 1946 and 1955. The federal government funded massive interstate highway construction — projects that often destroyed urban working-class neighborhoods to ease suburban commutes while leaving less money for public transportation.

The economic picture was mixed. Following World War II, the majority of White Americans were members of the middle class. Most African Americans, however, were not. In 1950, the median income for White families was $20,656, whereas for Black families it was $11,203. By 1960, the gap had narrowed somewhat, but Black families still earned roughly 55 cents for every dollar White families earned. Conformity was the watchword of suburban life — neighborhoods had rules about clotheslines, lawn maintenance, and parking. Above all, conforming meant marrying young and having children: the baby boom produced the largest generation in American history.

“The houses were so similar that workers told of coming home late at night and walking into the wrong one.” — OpenStax U.S. History, on Levittown

28.4 Popular Culture and Mass Media

Rock and roll, television, and the Red Scare’s reach into Hollywood

Learning Objectives

  • Describe Americans’ different responses to rock and roll music and what those responses revealed
  • Explain how the HUAC hearings affected Hollywood and what they reveal about Cold War culture
  • Discuss the way television reflected and reinforced postwar American values

The baby boomers sought to define and redefine their identities in numerous ways. Music, especially rock and roll, reflected their desire to rebel against adult authority. In the 1940s, various evolutions of uptempo blues, jazz, and country gave way to Rhythm and Blues, predominantly created by Black artists. When White artists began covering R&B songs and adapting the style, rock and roll went mainstream. Alan Freed’s radio promotion brought the music to White audiences, and sales exploded.

The theme of rebellion — present in many rock and roll songs — appealed to teenagers. White artists like Bill Haley and Elvis Presley rose to stardom by adopting musical motifs from the African American community and achieving mainstream success. This dynamic revealed a recurring pattern in American popular culture: Black artists created the form; White artists were rewarded with mainstream fame and fortune. Despite adults’ dislike of the genre, or perhaps because of it, more than 68 percent of the music played on the radio in 1956 was rock and roll.

The Hollywood Ten
1947
Ten film industry workers who refused to testify before HUAC and were subsequently blacklisted. They included directors, screenwriters, and producers. Some never worked in Hollywood again; others wrote under assumed names. The blacklist eventually grew to over 300 names.

Hollywood faced its own Cold War crisis. The HUAC hearings targeted the film industry. When eleven “unfriendly witnesses” were called to testify about Communism in October 1947, ten refused to answer questions. The Hollywood Ten were cited for contempt of Congress, and film executives promptly declared they would no longer be employed. More than three hundred actors, screenwriters, directors, and musicians were eventually blacklisted.

Meanwhile, television transformed American life. In 1950, fewer than 4 million American households owned a television. By 1955, that number exceeded 30 million — nearly 65 percent of all households. The new medium projected an idealized image of White suburban family life: happy housewife mothers, wise fathers, and mischievous but manageable children, as on Leave It to Beaver and Father Knows Best. These shows reinforced values of individualism and family that were explicitly framed as “American” in opposition to Communist collectivism.

28.5 The African American Struggle for Civil Rights

Courts, boycotts, and the violent backlash against change

Learning Objectives

  • Trace the legal strategies that dismantled segregation in the 1940s and 1950s
  • Explain how grassroots activism worked alongside legal challenges
  • Describe White resistance to desegregation and what it reveals about the obstacles activists faced

In the aftermath of World War II, African Americans began to mount organized resistance to racially discriminatory policies. Many veterans returned home to find that despite their sacrifices, the United States was not willing to extend them greater rights. The GI Bill, which provided education benefits and low-interest mortgages, was administered in ways that systematically excluded Black veterans from its most valuable provisions.

Legal challenges mounted. In 1946, the Supreme Court ruled in Morgan v. Virginia that segregation on interstate buses was unconstitutional. In 1948, Shelley v. Kraemer ruled that courts could not enforce racial real estate covenants. In 1954, Thurgood Marshall and the NAACP won their landmark case: Brown v. Board of Education overturned Plessy v. Ferguson and declared segregated public schools unconstitutional.

Emmett Till
1941 – 1955
A 14-year-old from Chicago who was abducted and murdered while visiting relatives in Mississippi after allegedly speaking to a White woman. His mother Mamie Till insisted on an open-casket funeral to show America what had been done to her son. The acquittal of his killers shocked the nation and galvanized the civil rights movement.

The challenge now was enforcement. The Supreme Court ordered desegregation to proceed “with all deliberate speed.” For many districts, that was very, very slow. In 1957, when nine Black students attempted to attend Central High School in Little Rock, Arkansas, Governor Orval Faubus called out the state National Guard to block them. After mob violence, President Eisenhower — who privately disagreed with the Court’s ruling — was forced to send the Little Rock Nine federal military protection. It was the first time since Reconstruction that federal troops protected the rights of Black Americans in the South.

White resistance was fierce. The murder of Emmett Till in August 1955 shocked the nation. One of those inspired by Till’s death was Rosa Parks, an NAACP member from Montgomery, Alabama. On December 1, 1955, Parks refused to give her seat to a White man. The Women’s Political Council, a group of Black female activists, organized a boycott of Montgomery’s buses. A young minister named Martin Luther King Jr. helped lead and sustain it. For 381 days, Black Montgomery residents walked, organized carpools, and refused to ride the buses. In June 1956, a federal court found the segregation ordinance unconstitutional.

“In 1956, 101 members of Congress signed ‘The Southern Manifesto,’ in which they accused the U.S. Supreme Court of misusing its power and violating the principle of states’ rights.” — OpenStax U.S. History

White Responses to Desegregation

Resistance, violence, and the limits of legal change

Many White Americans greeted desegregation with fury. Some withdrew their children from public schools and enrolled them in newly created all-White private academies. Many southern school districts closed their public schools entirely rather than integrate them — one county in Virginia kept its schools closed for five years. Southern segregationists also filed lawsuits against the NAACP trying to bankrupt the organization, and 101 members of Congress signed the “Southern Manifesto” accusing the Supreme Court of abusing its power.

Violence was a constant tool of resistance. When Little Rock’s Central High School desegregated, an irate Klansman threatened to kill the school board members. White racists murdered Black activists who challenged the racial order. Beyond individual acts of violence, the persistence of racial segregation in housing, employment, and education showed that court orders and legislation could not by themselves change deeply embedded social systems. Enforcing Brown required troops. Enforcing voting rights would require federal legislation. Even in the North, where Jim Crow was not legally mandated, structural racism shaped where Black Americans could live, work, and go to school.

Chapter 28  ·  Haass Connection

Nonviolence as Strategy

Civil disobedience and the logic of the Montgomery Boycott

The Montgomery Bus Boycott exemplified the nonviolent civil disobedience that Richard Haass describes in Chapter 5 as the legitimate tool for political change in a democracy. Rather than responding to segregation with violence, activists organized a sustained, disciplined, community-wide economic action. They chose to accept legal penalties — Rosa Parks was arrested — rather than simply comply with unjust law. They appealed to the conscience of the nation and the pocketbook of the bus company. The boycott cost Montgomery’s transit system enormous revenue and drew national media attention.

Haass argues that nonviolent protest succeeds partly because it “occupies the political high ground” — keeping the focus on the injustice being challenged rather than the tactics of those challenging it. The Montgomery campaign did exactly this. But it also required enormous personal sacrifice from ordinary people: 40,000 Black residents walked, carpooled, or simply stayed off the buses for 381 days, facing harassment and violence throughout. Civil disobedience, as this episode illustrates, is not a passive strategy — it requires courage, organization, and a willingness to endure punishment for a just cause.

Week 5  ·  Synthesis

Connections Across the Reading

Prosperity, conformity, and the struggle for inclusion

The 1950s were a decade of contradiction. Prosperity was real — but racially bounded. Conformity was celebrated — but resisted by teenagers through rock and roll and by Black Americans through boycotts and lawsuits. Television projected a vision of American family life that excluded most of the country. The interstate highway system built suburban opportunity while destroying urban communities of color. Legal victories mounted — Morgan, Shelley, Brown — and were met with federal troops, closed schools, and murder. Understanding this decade means holding all of these contradictions at once.

The civil rights movement that emerges from this reading is not the simple story of heroes overcoming obstacles. It is a story of relentless, creative, disciplined organizing against a system of racial inequality that was baked into law, economics, culture, and violence. The activists who sustained the Montgomery Boycott for 381 days, the mothers of the Harlem Nine who kept their children home from inferior schools, the Little Rock Nine who faced mobs every day — these were not exceptional individuals. They were ordinary people demanding that America live up to its stated ideals.
