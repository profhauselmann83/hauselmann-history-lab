29.1 The New Frontier

JFK’s image, his election, and the limits of domestic reform

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why Kennedy’s style and image were so significant — and what limits they imposed
  • Describe Kennedy’s approach to domestic policy and why it was so constrained
  • Assess Kennedy’s Cold War strategy

No one symbolized the hopes and energies of the new decade more than John Fitzgerald Kennedy. The son of a wealthy Boston family, Kennedy graduated from Harvard and served as a Navy officer during World War II, saving the crew of his PT boat after it was destroyed by the Japanese. He was elected to the House in 1946 and to the Senate in 1952. For many Americans, Kennedy represented a bright, shining future: young, vigorous, idealistic, committed to change.

John F. Kennedy
1917 – 1963
35th president (1961–1963). Behind Kennedy’s healthy, sporty public image was a gravely ill man whose wartime injuries caused him daily pain. He concealed his Addison’s disease and chronic back pain from the public. The gap between image and reality was central to his presidency.

Kennedy’s popular reputation owed much to style and attitude. He and his wife Jacqueline conveyed optimism and glamour. Nowhere was his style more evident than in the first televised presidential debate in September 1960: radio listeners judged Nixon the winner, while television viewers believed the more telegenic Kennedy made the better showing. Kennedy won the election by the closest margin since 1888 — defeating Nixon with only 0.01 percent more of the record 67 million votes cast.

Kennedy dedicated his inaugural address to a vision of national challenge. “Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country.” His lofty goals ranged from fighting poverty to winning the space race. He assembled an administration of confident, talented people — but his domestic reform plans were immediately constrained by his narrow victory and the power of Southern Democrats in Congress. As a result, his achievements in domestic policy were modest: expanding food stamps, extending unemployment benefits, and passing the Mental Health Centers Construction Act. He remained hesitant to propose new civil rights legislation for fear of losing Southern support.

Kennedy the Cold Warrior

Bay of Pigs, the Cuban Missile Crisis, and flexible response

Learning Objectives

  • Explain Kennedy’s “flexible response” approach and how it differed from Eisenhower’s New Look
  • Describe the Bay of Pigs invasion and its consequences
  • Analyze how the Cuban Missile Crisis was resolved and what it revealed about Cold War brinkmanship

Kennedy focused most of his energy on foreign policy. He replaced Eisenhower’s doctrine of massive nuclear retaliation with flexible response — developing a range of military capabilities to respond more appropriately to different kinds of threats. He created the Peace Corps and the Alliance for Progress to counter Soviet influence in the developing world through aid and development rather than military force alone.

Kennedy’s Cold War record had a spectacular failure early on. In April 1961, approximately 1,400 CIA-trained Cuban exiles landed at the Bay of Pigs hoping to inspire a popular uprising against Fidel Castro. Kennedy cancelled the promised air support, and the invasion was quickly crushed. The disaster deepened Cuba’s ties to the Soviet Union, which soon began building missile sites there.

In October 1962, U.S. spy planes confirmed Soviet nuclear missiles in Cuba — within easy range of American cities. Kennedy imposed a naval quarantine and demanded the missiles be removed. For thirteen days, the world stood on the brink of nuclear war. Working behind the scenes, Robert Kennedy and Soviet ambassador Dobrynin reached a compromise: the Soviets would remove the missiles in exchange for Kennedy’s pledge not to invade Cuba and a secret agreement to remove U.S. missiles from Turkey. The Cuban Missile Crisis ended peacefully — and led to the Limited Test Ban Treaty (1963) and a direct telephone hotline between Washington and Moscow.

“Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country.” — President Kennedy, inaugural address, January 20, 1961

Tentative Steps Toward Civil Rights — and Vietnam

The limits of Kennedy’s domestic commitments

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why Kennedy moved cautiously on civil rights — and what finally pushed him to act
  • Describe the U.S. involvement in Vietnam under Kennedy and the choices he faced

Cold War concerns also motivated Kennedy’s steps toward racial equality. Realizing that legal segregation hurt the country’s chances of gaining allies in Africa, Asia, and Latin America, the administration increased efforts to secure civil rights — but slowly and reluctantly. Kennedy focused primarily on voting rights, which he hoped would generate Black political power without provoking Southern Democrats whose votes he needed for his other priorities.

Kennedy was pushed toward more direct action by events he could not control. When James Meredith attempted to enroll at the University of Mississippi in 1962, riots broke out and Kennedy sent federal troops. When two Black students attempted to enroll at the University of Alabama in 1963 and Governor George Wallace physically blocked the schoolhouse door, Kennedy federalized the Alabama National Guard and delivered the televised address you read in Source Analysis 5.1 — calling for major civil rights legislation. He would not live to see it passed.

Vietnam presented a different kind of challenge. The United States had been supporting South Vietnam’s anti-Communist government since the mid-1950s. By November 1963, Kennedy had 16,000 military advisors in Vietnam. A few weeks before Kennedy’s own assassination, South Vietnamese President Diem was murdered in a military coup that U.S. officials had tacitly supported, leaving the country in political chaos.

Tragedy in Dallas

November 22, 1963 — and what it means to lose a president

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the circumstances of Kennedy’s assassination and its immediate aftermath
  • Explain why Kennedy’s assassination has remained so contested in American memory

Kennedy traveled to Texas in November 1963 to shore up his political base in the South. On November 22, as his motorcade made its way through the streets of Dallas, shots rang out. Kennedy was rushed to Parkland Hospital and pronounced dead. Lee Harvey Oswald, an employee at the Texas School Book Depository from which the shots appeared to come, was arrested later that day and charged with the murder. Two days later, Oswald himself was shot and killed by Jack Ruby, a local nightclub owner, while being transferred to the county jail.

Almost immediately, conspiracy theories began to circulate. President Johnson appointed the Warren Commission to investigate. It concluded that Oswald had acted alone. The commission’s ruling failed to satisfy many, and multiple theories have circulated for decades. No credible evidence has ever proven that someone other than Oswald murdered Kennedy or that Oswald had co-conspirators.

Kennedy’s assassination cut short a presidency whose historical legacy remains genuinely ambiguous. He had made stirring promises about civil rights, poverty, and a new generation of leadership — but had delivered relatively little. He had come to the brink of nuclear war and stepped back. He had sent 16,000 advisors to Vietnam and left the decision about escalation to his successor. What Kennedy might have done had he lived is one of American history’s great unanswerable questions.

Connections Across Both Readings

From the Montgomery Boycott to Dallas — who democracy served

These two readings together trace a decade and a half of American history — from the postwar suburban boom through Kennedy’s assassination. They tell a single story from two angles: the story of who was included in American democracy and who was not, and the long, difficult, ongoing work of closing the gap between stated ideals and lived reality. Levittown’s racial covenants and Kennedy’s political calculations about Southern Democrats are not separate stories. They are the same story: about how racial inequality was embedded in the political, economic, and legal structures of postwar America — and how activists, community organizers, and ordinary people worked to dismantle it.

Haass argues in Chapter 5 that nonviolent civil disobedience is the legitimate path to change in a democracy. The evidence of these readings complicates this claim. Legal strategies (Brown) required federal troops to enforce. Nonviolent action (Montgomery) required 381 days of personal sacrifice. Political lobbying (civil rights leaders pressing Kennedy) was blocked by the calculus of electoral politics. Change came, when it came, because the movement made the cost of inaction greater than the cost of acting — and because the courage of ordinary people made it impossible to look away.
