Video Lecture

Watch before beginning the readings — approx. 30–40 minutes

~30–40 min

This week, we turn to one of the most dramatic transformations in American history — the rise of industrial capitalism in the Gilded Age. In this lecture, I'll walk you through the explosive growth of big business, the hardships faced by workers and farmers, and the collision of peoples that defined westward expansion. By the end, you should have a clear framework for the reading and discussion work ahead.

As you watch, pay attention to the tensions that emerge between wealth and poverty, progress and exploitation — and think about whose story gets told when we call this era a triumph of American enterprise.

Questions to Keep in Mind While Watching

These questions will guide your reading and come up again in the Canvas discussion.

  1. What did Mark Twain mean when he called this era the “Gilded Age” — and what was beneath the gilding?
  2. How did workers and farmers respond to industrial capitalism, and why did those responses largely fail?
  3. What does “going west” look like from the perspective of Native Americans versus homesteaders versus railroad companies?
  4. Was the inequality of the Gilded Age a flaw in the system — or a feature of it?
Heads Up After completing the textbook readings and questions, you'll move to Canvas for this week's discussion. There you'll analyze primary sources on Populism and the Election of 1896, and on federal tribal land policies — two cases that put the era's contradictions in sharp relief.
