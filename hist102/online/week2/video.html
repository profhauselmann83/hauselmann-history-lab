What This Lecture Covers

This lecture introduces the major themes of Module 3, which spans OpenStax Chapters 17, 18, and 20. Rather than summarizing every section of the textbook, the lecture focuses on three interlocking questions that will guide your reading and discussion work this week.

1. What made the Gilded Age "gilded"? Mark Twain's famous phrase — that the era had a shiny surface concealing corruption and inequality beneath — is the key to understanding this period. The lecture examines the coexistence of spectacular industrial wealth and profound working-class hardship, and asks you to think about whether that combination was a flaw in the system or a feature of it.

2. How did ordinary people respond to industrial capitalism? Workers organized, struck, and sometimes rioted. Farmers built the most successful third-party movement in American history. The lecture traces these responses and explains why they largely failed to reshape the political economy of the era — and what that failure meant for the future.

3. What did "going west" actually mean — and for whom? Westward expansion was not simply the triumphant march of civilization across an empty continent. It was a collision of peoples, each with their own claims and ambitions. The lecture examines the experiences of homesteaders, miners, cowboys, exodusters, and Native Americans, and introduces the debate over Frederick Jackson Turner's "Frontier Thesis."

Before moving to Reading 3.1: Note down two or three questions the lecture raised for you. The reading questions will return to these themes, and your Canvas source analyses will ask you to evaluate primary evidence connected to them.
