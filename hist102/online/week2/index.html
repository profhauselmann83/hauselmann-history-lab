The Gilded Age and Industrialization

How did industrialization, labor conflict, westward migration, and Populist politics reshape the United States between 1870 and 1900?
Week of Mar 16 130 pts total OpenStax Ch. 17–18, 20

The Gilded Age, spanning roughly from the end of the Civil War to the turn of the twentieth century, was marked by tremendous change, growth, and challenge in the United States. This period witnessed the rise of industrialization, massive migrations, the clash of labor and capital, and the development of new technologies that forever altered the American landscape. It was a time of both incredible opportunity and stark inequality — a glittering surface, as Mark Twain's famous phrase suggested, concealing deep social tensions beneath.

This week we cover a wide range of interconnected topics: the rise of big business and industrial capitalism, the struggles of workers and the early labor movement, the farming crisis and the Populist revolt, and the westward migration that transformed the continent — including its devastating consequences for Native American peoples. By the end of the week you should be able to explain how these developments were connected, and why the Gilded Age remains one of the most consequential periods in American history.

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the key economic and social developments of the Gilded Age and their impact on the United States.
  • Analyze the factors that led to industrialization and technological innovation during this period.
  • Examine the experiences of laborers, immigrants, and Native Americans and their role in shaping the era.
  • Evaluate the influence of the Populist Movement on American society and politics.
  • Explore the concept of westward migration, its motivations, and its consequences for both settlers and indigenous peoples.
  • Discuss the policies and conflicts involving Native Americans, including the Dawes Act and the Ghost Dance Movement.
  • Analyze Frederick Jackson Turner's "Frontier Thesis" and its implications for American identity.
  • Examine the impact of immigration and urbanization on American cities and society.
Central Question

Who paid the price for America's industrial and territorial expansion?

The Gilded Age produced spectacular wealth and technological innovation. But as you read this week, keep asking: who benefited, and at whose expense? What did factory workers, farmers, immigrants, and Native Americans experience as the nation grew? And what does it mean that the United States became the world's leading industrial power during these same decades?

Your primary source work on Canvas will push you to engage with these questions through the voices of people who lived through this transformation.
