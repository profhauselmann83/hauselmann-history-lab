Introduction

What We're Studying This Week

Between 1877 and 1900, the United States transformed itself into the world's leading industrial power. Steel mills, oil refineries, railroads, and telegraphs knit the country together into a national economy of breathtaking scale — and breathtaking inequality. A handful of men, Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan, and a few others, amassed fortunes previously unimaginable in American life. Millions of workers labored sixty hours a week for wages that kept them perpetually at the edge of poverty.

This week you will examine how the Gilded Age economy was built, who benefited and who paid the price, and how ordinary Americans responded — through labor organizing, political activism, and the Populist movement. You will read about the Haymarket affair, the subtreasury plan, and the long debate over gold versus silver that culminated in the election of 1896. You will also examine the westward expansion that accompanied industrialization: the Homestead Act, the cattle drives, the displacement of Native Americans, and the diverse communities that settled the Great Plains.

By the end of the week you should be able to explain what made the Gilded Age "gilded" — brilliant on the surface, base metal beneath — and evaluate the argument that this era laid the foundations for the reform movements of the twentieth century.
