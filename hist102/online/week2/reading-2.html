17.1 The Westward Spirit and Manifest Destiny

Who went west — and what did they think they were doing?

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the concept of Manifest Destiny and its role in westward expansion
  • Identify the diverse groups of settlers who moved west after 1865
  • Describe the legislation that facilitated western settlement

While a small number of settlers had pushed westward before the mid-nineteenth century, the land west of the Mississippi was largely unexplored by the United States. Most Americans, if they thought of it at all, viewed this territory as an arid wasteland suitable only for American Indians. But beginning in the 1840s, a combination of economic opportunity and ideological encouragement changed the way Americans thought of the West.

The concept of Manifest Destiny found its roots in the long-standing traditions of territorial expansion upon which the nation itself was founded. Magazine editor John O'Sullivan coined the phrase in 1845, writing that it was "our manifest destiny to overspread the continent allotted by Providence for the free development of our multiplying millions." The idea was simple: Americans were destined — and indeed divinely ordained — to expand democratic institutions across the continent. Manifest Destiny justified in Americans' minds their right and duty to govern any other groups they encountered during their expansion, as well as absolved them of any questionable tactics employed in the process.

While the commonly held view of the day was of a relatively empty frontier, the reality was quite different. Hispanic communities in the Southwest, diverse tribes throughout the western states, and settlers from Asia and Western Europe already lived in many parts of the country. American expansion would necessitate a far more complex exchange than simply filling empty space.

Legislation Opening the West

To assist settlers, Congress passed two significant pieces of legislation in 1862. The Homestead Act allowed any head of household, or individual over the age of twenty-one — including unmarried women — to receive a parcel of 160 acres for only a nominal filing fee, provided they "improved the land" within five years. Under this act, the government transferred over 270 million acres of public domain land to private citizens. The Pacific Railway Act commissioned the Union Pacific Railroad to build west from Omaha while the Central Pacific moved east from Sacramento; the first transcontinental railroad was completed at Promontory, Utah, in the spring of 1869.

Who Went West?

The Exodusters
1870s – 1890s
Over 500,000 African Americans lived west of the Mississippi by 1890. Some 25,000 arrived in Kansas alone in 1879–80, fleeing Southern racism. Approximately 12,000 worked as cowboys; others served as Buffalo Soldiers.

The original wave of western settlers consisted of moderately prosperous, white, native-born farming families. By 1870, western migration was opened to Americans of more modest means. Germans, Scandinavians, and Irish were among the most common immigrant groups, tending to settle close together and recreating the communities they had left behind. In 1900, more than one-third of the population of Minnesota and Wisconsin was foreign-born; nearly 45 percent of North Dakota's population was immigrant-born.

Several thousand African Americans — known as exodusters, referencing the biblical flight from Egypt — migrated west following the Civil War to escape the racism of the South. Over 500,000 Black people lived west of the Mississippi by 1890. Approximately twelve thousand worked as cowboys, and some became Buffalo Soldiers, serving in all-Black cavalry and infantry units that protected settlers and helped build western infrastructure.

17.2 Homesteading: Dreams and Realities

What 160 acres actually delivered — and what it cost

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the environmental and economic hardships faced by homesteaders on the Great Plains
  • Analyze how frontier conditions both constrained and empowered pioneer women

As settlers and homesteaders moved westward, they faced difficult and often insurmountable challenges. The land was difficult to farm, there were few building materials, and harsh weather, insects, and inexperience led to frequent setbacks. The prohibitive prices charged by railroad lines made it expensive to ship crops to market or have goods sent out.

The first houses were typically made of mud and sod with thatch roofs. Rain presented constant problems, with mud falling into food and vermin scampering across bedding. Weather patterns brought tornadoes, droughts, blizzards, and insect swarms. One Kansas newspaper reported a locust swarm in 1878 during which the insects devoured "everything green," covering the ground six to twelve inches deep. Railroads charged notoriously high rates for farm equipment, and banks charged high interest rates on loans. Each year, farmers produced ever-larger crops, flooding the markets and driving prices down further. Although some understood the economics of supply and demand, none could overtly control such forces.

Pioneer Women

Pioneer Women
1860s – 1900s
A typical farm wife in 1900 devoted nine hours per day to household chores plus two additional hours to barn and garden work — more than most industrial workers. Yet frontier conditions gave women legal and social standing rarely found in the East.

Women faced all the physical hardships men encountered — plus childbirth with no medical assistance. They could not sell property, sue for divorce, serve on juries, or vote. As late as 1900, a typical farm wife devoted nine hours per day to household chores plus two additional hours to barn and garden work. One wife commented in 1879: "[We are] not much better than slaves. It is a weary, monotonous round of cooking and washing and mending and as a result the insane asylum is a third filled with wives of farmers."

Yet challenges also empowered women. If widowed, a wife typically took over the farm rather than passing it to a male relation — a level of management rare back East. Pioneer women were considered more equal partners in the homestead's success. It is not surprising that the first states to grant women's rights, including the right to vote, were those in the Pacific Northwest and Upper Midwest, where women worked the land side by side with men.

17.3 Making a Living in Gold and Cattle

From the Gold Rush to the cattle drives — and the myths that outlasted both

Learning Objectives

  • Describe how both the mining and cattle industries shifted from individual opportunity to corporate control
  • Identify the diverse demographics of miners and cowboys
  • Evaluate the gap between the myth of the Wild West and its reality

Although homestead farming was the primary goal of most western settlers, a small minority sought to make their fortunes quickly through mining. The discovery of gold at Sutter's Mill in California set a pattern repeated again and again for the next decade: a sudden rush of prospectors, followed by camp followers — saloonkeepers, prostitutes, store owners, and criminals — all arriving in droves. The original Forty-Niners were individual prospectors who sifted gold through panning or sluice boxes.

By the 1860s and 1870s, however, the lowest-hanging fruit had been picked. The Comstock Lode of Nevada (1859) repeated the California pattern. Individual efforts were less successful, and larger businesses began underwriting mining operations, paying workers daily wages to labor underground in extreme heat, poor ventilation, and constant danger from fires, explosions, and cave-ins. By some accounts, close to eight thousand miners died on the frontier during this period, with three times that number suffering crippling injuries.

The Cattle Industry

The Chisholm Trail
1867 – 1885
The primary cattle drive route from Texas to the railroad terminus in Abilene, Kansas. Between 1865 and 1885, as many as 40,000 cowboys worked the Great Plains; close to one-third were Hispanic or African American.

The cattle industry lacked the romance of the Gold Rush, but its role in western expansion should not be underestimated. At the end of the Civil War, as many as five million longhorn steers roamed the Texas frontier. The completion of the transcontinental railroad made it profitable to round them up and transport them east for thirty to fifty dollars per head. So began the famous cattle drives of the 1860s and 1870s along the Chisholm Trail to railroad terminals in Abilene, Wichita, and Dodge City, Kansas. Between 1865 and 1885, as many as forty thousand cowboys roamed the Great Plains; close to one-third were Hispanic or African American. The saddles, lassos, chaps, and lariats that define cowboy culture all arose from the Mexican ranchers who had used them before the cowboys arrived.

The era of the free range ended in 1873, when the invention of barbed wire allowed ranchers to fence off their lands. Range wars erupted as wealthy ranchers used barbed wire to claim prime grazing lands, cutting off smaller operators. In Johnson County, Wyoming, cattle ranchers organized a "lynching bee" in 1891–1892, hiring armed guns from Texas to hunt cattle rustlers; only the intervention of federal troops prevented a greater massacre.

17.4 The Assault on American Indian Life and Culture

Treaties, massacres, and the policy of Americanization

Before You Read — Watch First

Watch the following video on the "Indian Problem" from the National Museum of the American Indian (Smithsonian). These documents come directly from the policies described here — you'll analyze them in Source Analysis 2.2.

As American power and population grew in the 19th century, the United States gradually rejected the main principle of treaty-making — that tribes were self-governing nations — and initiated policies that undermined tribal sovereignty. For Indian nations, these policies resulted in broken treaties, vast land loss, removal and relocation, population decline, and cultural decimation.

Historical Thinking Skill — Chronological Reasoning

Chronological thinking is at the heart of historical reasoning. As you read this section, practice identifying how U.S. policy toward Native Americans changed over time. Ask yourself:

  • How did the government's approach shift from the 1850s to the 1890s?
  • What patterns of continuity run through these changes?
  • What caused the shifts when they happened?

Source: American Historical Association, "What Skills Should You Have When You Leave a History Class?" (Accessed October 27, 2023)

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the range of methods the U.S. government used to dispossess Native Americans
  • Explain how U.S. policy shifted from treaty-making to military force to forced assimilation
  • Evaluate the consequences of the Dawes Act for Native American communities

On the eve of westward expansion, as many as 250,000 Native Americans populated the Great Plains. The first strategy was to negotiate treaties to move tribes out of the path of white settlers. The First Treaty of Fort Laramie (1851) established distinct tribal borders in exchange for annual payments and promises of noninterference. Due to government corruption, many annuity payments never reached the tribes. Within a decade, as settlers pushed into even designated reservation lands, backed by state and local militias, some tribes resisted and were massacred.

In 1862, frustrated Dakotas in Minnesota rebelled after annuity payments stopped arriving and settlers encroached on their lands. Over one thousand white settlers were killed before a militia regained control. President Lincoln intervened to reduce death sentences, but thirty-eight Dakota men were hanged in the largest mass execution in American history. In Colorado in 1864, Colonel Chivington's militia attacked a Cheyenne camp flying the American flag and white flag of surrender, murdering close to one hundred people — the majority women and children — in the Sand Creek Massacre. Chivington proudly displayed his collection of nearly one hundred Native American scalps for the rest of his life.

Little Bighorn to Wounded Knee

Sitting Bull
c. 1831 – 1890
Hunkpapa Lakota leader who led the coalition that defeated Custer at Little Bighorn (1876). Fled to Canada, later returned and was killed by Indian police in 1890, two weeks before the Wounded Knee Massacre.

The Battle of Little Bighorn (June 25, 1876) was the single greatest U.S. military defeat by a Native American force during westward expansion. Sitting Bull led nearly three thousand Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho warriors who surrounded and killed Colonel Custer and 262 of his men. Despite the victory, the tribes were hunted down, and Sitting Bull eventually fled to Canada. The final episode of the Indian Wars came at the Wounded Knee Massacre (December 29, 1890), where the 7th Cavalry opened fire on a group of Lakota Ghost Dancers who were surrendering, killing between 150 and 300 men, women, and children. The U.S. government awarded twenty soldiers the Congressional Medal of Honor for this action.

Americanization and the Dawes Act

Government officials then turned to Americanization. Native American children were removed from their homes and placed in boarding schools such as the Carlisle Indian School, where they were forced to abandon their tribal languages, clothing, and customs. The school's founder, Richard Pratt, summarized the policy's goal: "Kill the Indian in him, and save the man." The Dawes Severalty Act of 1887 divided tribal lands and granted 160 acres to each head of family, with the government holding the land in trust for twenty-five years. Surplus land was sold to white settlers. Under the Dawes Act, Native Americans were assigned the most arid, useless land; the government sold as much as eighty million acres of Native American land to white settlers.

Who Really Went West?

The diverse communities that built the American West

The popular image of the westward settler was a white Anglo-Saxon Protestant farmer. The reality was far more diverse. German, Scandinavian, and Irish immigrants settled in large numbers in Minnesota, Wisconsin, North Dakota, and South Dakota. Hispanic communities in the Southwest had been there long before American settlers arrived; the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) granted American citizenship to Mexicans who stayed, promising to protect their language, religion, culture, and property. Within one generation, however, their legal property protections had largely dissolved and their culture was under assault.

Chinese Immigrants
1849 – 1882
More than 200,000 Chinese arrived in California between 1876 and 1890. They built much of the transcontinental railroad under dangerous conditions and for less pay than white workers. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 was the first federal law to bar immigration by national origin.

More than 200,000 Chinese arrived in California between 1876 and 1890, originally drawn by the Gold Rush and later employed building the transcontinental railroad — doing the most dangerous work for the least pay. They faced severe discrimination and violence at every turn. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 was the first federal law to bar immigration on the basis of national origin, and it remained in effect for over sixty years.

The women who participated in western settlement ranged from homesteading farm wives — who worked alongside their husbands under conditions of grinding hardship — to teachers and businesswomen in more established cities. As wealthy men brought families west, middle-class women organized churches, schools, and civic clubs that gradually transformed rough frontier towns into communities. Congress passed the Page Act (1875) to prohibit bringing women to the U.S. for prostitution — reflecting growing pressure to bring eastern moral standards to western spaces.

Week 2  ·  Synthesis

Connections Across the Readings

Industrial capitalism in the East and westward expansion in the West — two stories, one system

This week's two readings cover what might appear to be separate topics — industrial capitalism in the East and westward expansion in the West. But they were deeply connected. The railroads that made industrialization possible also opened the West; the steel that built the factories also built the tracks; the workers who labored in mines and on cattle drives were part of the same national labor market as factory workers in Pittsburgh and Chicago. The federal government's willingness to use violence against Native Americans to clear land for settlement mirrored its willingness to deploy troops against striking railroad workers.

Frederick Jackson Turner's famous Frontier Thesis, presented in 1893, argued that the existence of the frontier had shaped American democracy, individualism, and national character — and that its closing (declared by the Census Bureau in 1890) marked a turning point in American history. Turner's thesis was enormously influential but deeply controversial: it erased Native Americans from the story, romanticized conquest, and assumed that the frontier's values were universally shared.

The Populist Movement emerged from both stories. Farmers pushed off the land by railroad monopolies, debt, and falling prices were experiencing a western version of the same industrial capitalism that was crushing factory workers in the East. The Populist Party's 1892 Omaha Platform was a direct challenge to the political order that had allowed this to happen — and its failure in 1896, when William McKinley defeated the Populist-backed William Jennings Bryan, marked the consolidation of industrial capitalism's dominance over American political life for a generation.

"Up to our own day American history has been in a large degree the history of the colonization of the Great West. The existence of an area of free land, its continuous recession, and the advance of American settlement westward, explain American development." — Frederick Jackson Turner, "The Significance of the Frontier in American History," 1893
