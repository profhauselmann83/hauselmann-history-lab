18.1 Inventors of the Age

How did new technologies transform the American economy — and everyday life?

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how late nineteenth-century inventions contributed to the rise of big business
  • Explain how inventions of the late nineteenth century changed everyday American life

The late nineteenth century was an energetic era of inventions and entrepreneurial spirit. Building upon the mid-century Industrial Revolution in Great Britain, as well as answering the increasing call from Americans for efficiency and comfort, the country found itself in the grip of invention fever, with more people working on their big ideas than ever before. To understand the scope of this zeal for creation, consider the U.S. Patent Office, which in its first decade of existence recorded only 276 inventions. By 1860, the office had issued a total of 60,000 patents. But between 1860 and 1890, that number exploded to nearly 450,000, with another 235,000 in the last decade of the century. While many of these patents came to naught, some inventions became lynchpins in the rise of big business and the country's move toward an industrial-based economy.

From corrugated rollers that could crack hard homestead-grown wheat into flour, to refrigerated train cars and garment-sewing machines, new inventions fueled industrial growth around the country. As late as 1880, fully one-half of all Americans still lived and worked on farms. However, the development of commercial electricity by the close of the century permitted more industries to concentrate in cities. In turn, newly arrived immigrants sought employment in new urban factories. Immigration, urbanization, and industrialization coincided to transform the face of American society from primarily rural to significantly urban. From 1880 to 1920, the number of industrial workers quadrupled from 2.5 million to over 10 million, while urban populations doubled to reach one-half of the country's total population.

In offices, worker productivity benefited from the typewriter (1867), the cash register (1879), and the adding machine (1885). Inventions also slowly transformed home life. The vacuum cleaner arrived during this era, as did the flush toilet, which improved public health through the reduction of contamination associated with outhouses. Tin cans and, later, experiments with frozen food eventually changed how women shopped for and prepared food for their families, gaining valuable time in their daily schedules — a step that partially laid the groundwork for the modern women's movement.

Steel: The Cornerstone of Industry

Perhaps the most important industrial advancement of the era came in the production of steel. Manufacturers and builders preferred steel to iron due to its increased strength and durability. The Bessemer process, named for English inventor Henry Bessemer, and the open-hearth process, changed the way the United States produced steel. In 1860, the country produced thirteen thousand tons of steel. By 1900, this figure had risen to ten million tons, and just ten years later the United States was the top steel producer in the world at over twenty-four million tons annually. As production increased, the price of steel dropped by over 80 percent, making it the cornerstone of the American economy through the end of World War II.

Bell, Edison, and the Communications Revolution

Alexander Graham Bell
1847 – 1922
Scottish-born inventor who patented the telephone in 1876. His National Bell Telephone Company eventually became AT&T. By 1900, 1.35 million telephones were in use across the United States.

Alexander Graham Bell patented the telephone in 1876. While he was not the first to invent the concept, Bell was the first to capitalize on it, eventually creating what became the American Telephone and Telegraph Company (AT&T). Western Union, which had originally turned down Bell's machine, commissioned Thomas Edison to invent an improved version — it is actually Edison's version that most resembles the modern telephone. Fearing a costly legal battle, Western Union sold Edison's design to the Bell Company. By 1900, 1.35 million telephones were in use in the United States, and hundreds of American cities had local service for their citizens.

Thomas A. Edison
1847 – 1931
The era's most prolific inventor, with 1,093 patents. His Menlo Park laboratory employed up to 25 scientists and produced the phonograph, the motion picture projector, the dictaphone, and the incandescent light bulb.

Thomas Alva Edison registered 1,093 patents over his lifetime. In 1879, he invented the practical incandescent light bulb — exploring over six thousand different materials for the filament before settling on carbonized cotton thread. With financial backing from J. P. Morgan, he created the Edison Electric Illuminating Company, which began supplying electrical current to customers in New York City in 1882. George Westinghouse advanced electric power further by promoting alternating current (AC) power. Unlike Edison's direct current (DC), AC could be delivered over vast distances, meaning factories, homes, and farms anywhere could be served. Edison publicly proclaimed AC power best suited for use in the electric chair, hoping the smear campaign would deter homeowners from adopting it. He eventually lost this "War of Currents" as AC's advantages became undeniable.

18.2 From Invention to Industrial Growth

Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan — and the methods behind their fortunes

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the contributions of Carnegie, Rockefeller, and Morgan to the new industrial order
  • Describe the visions, philosophies, and business methods of Gilded Age industrialists

The post-Civil War inventors generated ideas that transformed the economy, but they were not big businessmen. The evolution from technical innovation to massive industry took place at the hands of entrepreneurs whose business gambles paid off. Railroads quickly became the nation's first "big business." By 1890, railroad lines covered nearly every corner of the United States, bringing raw materials to industrial factories and finished goods to consumer markets. The amount of track grew from 35,000 miles at the end of the Civil War to over 200,000 miles by the close of the century. Financing came through a combination of private capital and government loans and grants totaling $150 million and 185 million acres of public land. Among railroads' highly questionable tactics was the practice of differential shipping rates — larger enterprises received discounted rates to transport goods, while small producers paid higher rates that essentially subsidized those discounts. Midwest farmers came to refer to railroad tycoons as "robber barons," as their business dealings were frequently shady and exploitative.

Andrew Carnegie and The Gospel of Wealth

Andrew Carnegie
1835 – 1919
Immigrated from Scotland to Pennsylvania in 1848. Rose from bobbin boy to steel magnate, running an annual profit exceeding $40 million before Morgan bought him out in 1901 for $500 million.

Andrew Carnegie has the prototypical rags-to-riches story — one of the few that was actually true. Born in Scotland, Carnegie immigrated with his family to Pennsylvania in 1848. Following a brief stint as a bobbin boy and then a telegram messenger, he ingratiated himself to the president of the Pennsylvania Railroad, worked his way into management, and began investing his earnings. One investment in the oil fields of northwest Pennsylvania in 1864 earned him over $1 million in cash dividends, providing the capital he needed to transform the steel industry. His first company, the J. Edgar Thompson Steel Works, eventually ran an annual profit in excess of $40 million.

Carnegie more than any other businessman of the era championed the idea that wealthy tycoons owed a debt to society. His famous essay, The Gospel of Wealth, argued that the accumulation of wealth was natural and beneficial, but that the rich had a duty to use their fortunes for the public good. He borrowed from Herbert Spencer's theory of Social Darwinism, which held that society developed much like plant or animal life through a process of evolution in which the most fit and capable enjoyed the greatest material success. The myth of the rags-to-riches businessman was a potent one — author Horatio Alger made his own fortune writing stories about enterprising young boys who beat poverty through a combination of "luck and pluck."

Rockefeller and the Standard Oil Trust

John D. Rockefeller
1839 – 1937
Founded Standard Oil in 1870. Through horizontal then vertical integration, he controlled 95% of U.S. oil refining by 1879. Critics portrayed his company as a massive octopus with tentacles wrapped around industry, Congress, and the White House.

Like Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller was born of modest means. When he learned of oil discoveries near Titusville, Pennsylvania, in 1859, he chose a more certain investment than wildcatting: refining crude oil into kerosene for heating and lamps. Beginning in 1872, he forged agreements with several railroad companies to obtain discounted freight rates and gather intelligence on competitors. Through horizontal integration, Standard Oil grew to control nearly 95 percent of all oil refining in the country by 1879. Rockefeller then pursued vertical integration, acquiring companies involved in everything from barrel-making to pipelines to tanker cars. Other industrialists quickly followed suit; Gustavus Swift used vertical integration to dominate the U.S. meatpacking industry.

To control this empire, Rockefeller created a new legal entity called a trust. When the Ohio Supreme Court ruled Standard Oil must dissolve as a monopoly in violation of state and federal statutes, Rockefeller shifted to a holding company model. By 1905, over three hundred business mergers had occurred in the United States, affecting more than 80 percent of all industries, and 1 percent of the country's businesses controlled over 40 percent of the nation's economy.

J. Pierpont Morgan and Financial Power

J. P. Morgan
1837 – 1913
Investment banker whose firm held 341 directorships in 112 corporations controlling over $22 billion in assets — more than the assessed value of all land west of the Mississippi River.

Unlike Carnegie and Rockefeller, J. P. Morgan was no rags-to-riches hero. He was born to wealth and became much wealthier as an investment banker, making wise financial decisions in support of the entrepreneurs building their fortunes. Morgan's firm bought and sold stock in growing companies, and in return for his investment Morgan demanded seats on the companies' boards of directors, giving him even greater control than investment alone. A U.S. congressional subcommittee called Morgan's enterprise a form of "money trust" even more powerful than the trusts operated by Rockefeller. Morgan argued that his firm brought stability and organization to a hypercompetitive capitalist economy. His most notable investment was buying out Andrew Carnegie in 1901 for $500 million, then consolidating Carnegie's holdings with smaller steel firms to create the U.S. Steel Corporation — the country's first billion-dollar firm, capitalized at $1.4 billion.

"When the nineteenth century shall have passed into history, the impartial eyes of the reviewers will be amazed to find that the U.S. … tolerated the presence of the most gigantic, the most cruel, impudent, pitiless and grasping monopoly that ever fastened itself upon a country." — New York World on Standard Oil, 1880

18.3 Building Industrial America on the Backs of Labor

Wages, working conditions, violence, and the struggle to organize

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the qualities of industrial working-class life in the late nineteenth century
  • Analyze both workers' desire for labor unions and the reasons for unions' inability to achieve their goals

The growth of the American economy in the last half of the nineteenth century presented a paradox. The standard of living for some workers increased — as Carnegie wrote in The Gospel of Wealth, "the poor enjoy what the rich could not before afford." But for the multitudes in the working class, conditions in the factories and at home remained deplorable. In 1900, the average factory wage was approximately twenty cents per hour, for an annual salary of barely six hundred dollars — leaving approximately 20 percent of the population in industrialized cities at or below the poverty level. An average factory work week was sixty hours; in steel mills, workers put in twelve hours per day, seven days a week. As late as 1913, nearly 25,000 Americans lost their lives on the job, with another 700,000 workers suffering injuries that resulted in at least one missed month of work.

Factory owners embraced Frederick Taylor's principles of scientific management, dividing tasks into short, repetitive segments and effectively making workers cogs in a machine. One result was that owners could hire women and children to perform many tasks. From 1870 through 1900, the number of women working outside the home tripled to five million wage earners, with one-quarter working factory jobs. Women were paid less than men. Child labor in factories also tripled over this same period.

The Molly Maguires and the Great Railroad Strike

Workers were well aware of the vast discrepancy between their lives and the wealth of factory owners. In the 1870s, Irish coal miners in eastern Pennsylvania formed a secret organization called the Molly Maguires, which used kidnappings, beatings, and murder to bring attention to the miners' plight. Mine owners hired detectives to pose as miners and obtain the names of members. By 1875, they had acquired the names of twenty-four suspected Maguires; ten were hanged in 1877 in a public "Day of the Rope." The lesson workers took from this episode: sporadic violent protest would be met by escalated violence.

The Great Railroad Strike of 1877 was more significant. After a pay cut, railroad workers spontaneously walked out across the country. In Pittsburgh, the state militia opened fire on crowds, killing twenty individuals and wounding twenty-nine. President Hayes eventually sent federal troops to major cities to restore order. The strike lasted forty-five days and workers gained nothing — but a new era of labor conflict had arrived.

The Knights of Labor and the Haymarket Affair

Terence V. Powderly
1849 – 1924
Led the Knights of Labor from 1879, growing it to over 700,000 members with its inclusive "One Big Union" philosophy. The KOL collapsed after the Haymarket affair, which it had nothing to do with.

The Knights of Labor (KOL) grew from a small presence during the Panic of 1873 to over 700,000 members by 1886. The KOL's concept of "One Big Union" welcomed all wage workers regardless of occupation, with the exception of doctors, lawyers, and bankers. It welcomed women, African Americans, Native Americans, and immigrants of all trades and skill levels — a notable break from the earlier tradition of craft unions. Through sheer size, the Knights claimed victories in several railroad strikes in 1884–1885, including one against notorious robber baron Jay Gould.

Their popularity collapsed in a single night. On May 4, 1886, at Chicago's Haymarket Square, an anarchist group gathered in response to a death at an earlier labor demonstration. Although the protest was quiet, the police arrived armed for conflict. Someone in the crowd threw a bomb, killing one officer and injuring another. Seven anarchists speaking at the protest were arrested, charged with murder, and sentenced to death — despite no evidence that they had thrown the bomb. The press immediately blamed the KOL, though neither the organization nor Powderly had anything to do with the event. Membership fell to barely 100,000 by the end of 1886.

The AFL, Homestead, and Pullman

Samuel Gompers
1850 – 1924
Founded the American Federation of Labor in 1886 and led it until his death. His "bread and butter" focus on wages and hours for skilled workers achieved incremental gains, but excluded most factory workers from the movement.

The American Federation of Labor (AFL), led by Samuel Gompers, took a more conservative "bread and butter" approach, focusing almost entirely on economic gains for its members and seldom straying into political issues. By 1920 the AFL claimed four million members, yet still represented only 15 percent of nonfarm workers in the country, excluding most factory laborers.

Two final violent events all but crushed the labor movement for a generation. In the Homestead Strike of 1892, Carnegie's factory manager Henry Frick ordered a lockout and hired three hundred Pinkerton detectives. A gunfight on the river resulted in the deaths of three Pinkertons and six workers. The Pennsylvania militia arrived to escort strikebreakers, and the union was defeated. A subsequent failed assassination attempt on Frick by anarchist Alexander Berkman further strengthened public animosity toward the union. In the Pullman Strike of 1894, after George Pullman cut wages by 25 percent while maintaining rents and prices in his company town, Eugene Debs led a nationwide railroad strike. President Cleveland sent federal troops and had Debs arrested for interference with U.S. mail delivery. The strike ended with no labor gains and public opinion of unions lower than ever.

18.4 A New American Consumer Culture

Department stores, mail-order catalogs, and the birth of American consumerism

Despite the challenges workers faced, the rise of big business turned America into a culture of consumers desperate for time-saving and leisure commodities. Gone were the days when the small general store was the only option; by the end of the nineteenth century, people could shop in large department stores like Macy's in New York, Gimbel's in Philadelphia, and Marshall Field's in Chicago. Chain stores like A&P and Woolworth's, both opening in the 1870s, served those who lived farther from major urban areas. Industrial advancements contributed to this proliferation — new construction techniques permitted buildings with higher ceilings for larger displays, and the production of larger sheets of plate glass led to the development of larger store windows and glass display cases where shoppers could observe a variety of goods at a glance.

Rural families found new possibilities through mail-order catalogs. Aaron Montgomery Ward established the first significant mail-order business in 1872; Sears, Roebuck & Company followed in 1886, distributing over 300,000 catalogs annually by 1897, later breaking the one million annual mark in 1907. Richard Sears understood that farmers sought alternatives to the higher prices and credit purchases they were forced to endure at small-town country stores. By clearly stating the prices in his catalog, Sears built the company's image as "the consumer's bible," supplying much of America's hinterland with products ranging from farm supplies to bicycles, toilet paper to automobiles.

The tremendous variety of goods required businesses to compete in entirely new ways. By 1900, American businesses were spending almost $100 million annually on advertising. Professional advertising agencies emerged in the 1880s. Stores allowed people to open accounts and purchase on credit — then as today, the risks of buying on credit led many into debt. As advertising expert Roland Marchand observed, in an era when access to products became more important than access to the means of production, Americans quickly accepted the notion that they could live a better lifestyle by purchasing the right clothes, the best hair cream, and the shiniest shoes, regardless of their class. American consumerism had begun.

Reading Notes — Consumer Culture
The rise of a consumer culture in the Gilded Age seems to contradict the era's widespread poverty and low wages — yet both were real. How do you explain this apparent contradiction? What role did department stores, mail-order catalogs, advertising, and consumer credit play in shaping a new American identity around purchasing? In what ways did consumerism serve the interests of industrial capitalism, and in what ways might it have offered ordinary people something genuine?

20.2 The Key Political Issues: Patronage, Tariffs, and Gold

Whose interests did the federal government serve during the Gilded Age?

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the difference between the spoils system and civil service
  • Recognize how tariffs impacted different sectors of the economy
  • Explain why Americans were split on the question of gold versus silver

Weak president after weak president took office between 1877 and 1896, and not one incumbent was reelected. Once elected, presidents had barely enough power to repay the political favors that ensured their narrow victories. At the heart of each administration was the protection of the spoils system — the power of the president to practice widespread political patronage. Given the close calls in presidential elections during the era, maintaining political machinery through patronage was important to all presidents regardless of party affiliation.

The Republican Party was divided between the Stalwarts, who strongly supported the spoils system, and the Half-Breeds, who advocated for modest reform. An assassin's bullet accelerated change. On July 2, 1881, Charles Guiteau shot President Garfield, allegedly crying "I am a Stalwart of Stalwarts!" Guiteau had wanted an ambassadorship to France as reward for his campaign support. His actions were widely blamed on the spoils system. Chester Arthur — himself a product of the spoils system — surprised everyone by championing reform. The Pendleton Civil Service Act of 1883 set aside approximately 10 percent of government positions to be filled through competitive examination. Future presidents could enlarge but never shrink the list.

Tariffs and the Gold Standard

Tariffs — taxes on international imports — were another defining issue. High tariffs protected big domestic industries at the expense of consumers who could have benefited from international competition; lower tariffs reduced prices but threatened American manufacturers. Both Arthur and Grover Cleveland pushed for reform, but industrial interests blocked meaningful reductions. Harrison's McKinley Tariff of 1890 raised some rates to 50 percent.

No issue was more relevant to working-class Americans and farmers than the nation's monetary policy and the debate over gold versus silver. The government remained committed to the gold standard, including the official demonetizing of silver in 1873. Such a stance greatly benefitted prominent businessmen engaged in foreign trade while forcing more farmers and working-class Americans into greater debt. Advocates of a bimetallic standard argued that adding silver would put more money into circulation, raise farm prices, and help farmers climb out of debt. Harrison's Sherman Silver Purchase Act of 1890 was too modest to do real good, and President Cleveland signed its repeal in 1893 — further enraging farmers and debtors and setting the stage for the Populist revolt.

20.3 Farmers Revolt in the Populist Era

From overproduction and debt to the most significant third-party movement in American history

Learning Objectives

  • Understand how the economic and political climate promoted the formation of the farmers' protest movement
  • Explain how the farmers' revolt moved from protest to politics

The challenges American farmers faced in the last quarter of the nineteenth century were severe. They contended with rapidly declining farm prices, prohibitively high tariffs on items they needed to purchase, and foreign competition. One of the largest challenges was overproduction: the glut of farm products in the marketplace drove the price lower and lower. Falling deeper into debt, farmers responded by producing even more — which only drove prices down further still. To a hard-working farmer, the notion that their own overproduction was the greatest contributing factor to their debt was a completely foreign concept.

The initial response was to organize. Oliver Hudson Kelly's Patrons of Husbandry (the Grange) grew to over 1.5 million members within a decade, briefly succeeding in urging the passage of Granger Laws regulating railroad rates in several Midwestern states. But the Supreme Court struck down these laws in the Wabash case of 1886, ruling that states could not regulate interstate commerce. The Grange's political party, the Greenback Party, faded when only seven delegates appeared at an 1888 national convention.

Mary Elizabeth Lease
1850 – 1933
Kansas attorney and lecturer who became one of the most prominent voices of the Farmers' Alliance. Famous for urging farmers to "raise less corn and more hell!"

The Farmers' Alliance took root in the Grange's wake. In 1890, Dr. Charles Macune of the Southern Alliance urged the creation of a national alliance between his organization, the Northwest Alliance, and Tom Watson's Colored Alliance — the largest African American organization in the United States, with over one million members. Nearly 250,000 women joined the movement. Alliance spokeswoman Mary Elizabeth Lease famously urged members to "raise less corn and more hell!" All told, the Farmers' Alliance brought together over 2.5 million members.

The Alliance's most creative proposal was the subtreasury plan: the federal government would store farmers' crops in government warehouses, provide loans worth 80 percent of current crop prices, and allow farmers to sell when market prices rose sufficiently. Congress never seriously considered it. Angry at the government's continued unwillingness to act, the Alliance formed the Populist Party (People's Party) in 1891.

Their Omaha Platform, written by Ignatius Donnelly, vilified railroad owners, bankers, and big businessmen as part of a widespread conspiracy to control farmers. It called for adoption of the subtreasury plan, government control over railroads, an end to the national bank system, a federal income tax, and the direct election of U.S. senators. Populist candidate James B. Weaver polled one million votes in the 1892 presidential election — a remarkable showing for a new third party. The Populists anxiously awaited 1896, believing that if industrial workers faced hardships similar to farmers, a powerful alliance between the two groups could carry them to victory.
