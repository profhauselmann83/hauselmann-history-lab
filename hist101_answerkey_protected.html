PART 1: HISTORICAL SKILLS & DEMOCRATIC NORMS

1. The Four Historical Skills:
  • Sourcing: Who created it? When? Why? (Consider author's perspective)
  • Contextualization: What else was happening at the time?
  • Corroboration: How do multiple sources compare? Why do they differ?
  • Interpretation: What most likely happened based on evidence?
2. When Smith's Pocahontas story changed (1608 ‚Üí 1624), this teaches us:
Documents are shaped by their authors' purposes, audiences, and contexts. Smith had different motives in 1624 (after Pocahontas became famous in England) than in 1608 (when he was documenting events for investors). Sources can change based on when and why they were written.
3. Norms are unwritten expectations that help disagreement remain manageable / productive / peaceful.
4. Haass's Main Argument: Democracy needs both rights and obligations to survive.
5. Five of Haass's Ten Obligations (any 5 acceptable):
  • Be informed
  • Get involved
  • Be open to compromise
  • Remain civil
  • Reject violence
  • Value norms
  • Promote the common good
  • Respect government service
  • Support teaching of civics
  • Put country first
6. Misinformation violates: Be informed
7. Pence certifying 2020 election demonstrated: Put country first / Value norms

PART 2: CONSTITUTION & CHECKS AND BALANCES

8. Checks and Balances prevent tyranny by forcing negotiation / restraint / compromise among branches.
9. Madison: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary."
10. Three examples of checks:
  • Congress makes laws, but President can veto them.
  • President appoints judges, but Senate confirms them.
  • Courts use judicial review to strike down unconstitutional laws.
11. Why did Framers WANT friction?
Slow processes prevent tyranny and force compromise/negotiation. Friction ensures no single branch can act unilaterally and protects against concentrated power.
12. When checks FAILED: Jackson refused to enforce Supreme Court ruling ‚Üí Trail of Tears (Cherokee removal)

PART 3: EARLY COLONIES ‚Äî JAMESTOWN & VIRGINIA

13. Jamestown colonists entered Powhatan Confederacy territory (led by Powhatan / Wahunsenacawh).
14. Why did colonists die?
  • Disease (brackish water, poor location)
  • Starvation (couldn't/wouldn't farm)
  • Conflict with Native Americans / Poor leadership / Lack of supplies
15. What saved Virginia economically? Tobacco (cash crop)
16. Labor evolved from indentured servitude ‚Üí racialized slavery
17. 1619 significance:
  • House of Burgesses established (first representative assembly)
  • First enslaved Africans arrived / Beginning of slavery in English North America
18. Why might Smith add the Pocahontas rescue story in 1624?
Pocahontas had become famous in England; Smith needed to justify colonization and make it seem divinely ordained; enhance his own reputation; make survival look like success; appeal to audiences who knew Pocahontas as a Christian convert.

PART 4: NEW ENGLAND & THE PURITANS

19. Puritans wanted to "purify" the Church. They believed in predestination.
20. Winthrop's "City upon a Hill" emphasized:
  • Community cooperation / discipline / responsibility over individual freedom
  • Survival through mutual responsibility / discipline
  • Colony as example / model to the world
21. "Selfless inward, selfish outward" means:
Puritans showed care, sacrifice, and responsibility toward their own community members but showed less concern for outsiders. They were cooperative within their group but exclusive toward those outside it.
22. Cotton's land argument: Native land was "vacant" without English-style farming.

PART 5: VIRGINIA vs. NEW ENGLAND COMPARISON

Virginia New England
Who came? Young men Family groups
Why? Economic profit / gain Religious reform / mission
Main crop? Tobacco Mixed farming
Labor? Indentured ‚Üí slavery Family farms
Politics? Narrow (wealthy white landowners) Broader (among insiders)
Tolerance? Greater diversity Narrower tolerance / conformity
23. Key lesson: Demographics (who showed up) shaped political / governance systems.
24. Why does this matter?
Understanding who arrived (demographics) helps explain why colonies developed different political cultures. Virginia's young male laborers led to concentrated elite power, while New England's families created broader participation within tight communities. Population composition directly shaped governance structures.

PART 6: ARCHIVES & DOCUMENT ANALYSIS

25. Archives are: Structured collections of evidence / Organized repositories of documents that preserve evidence for future debate.
26. Archives don't eliminate disagreement; they make it manageable / productive.
27. Why archives matter for democracy:
They slow down disagreement, create a shared factual baseline, replace immediacy with documentation, make accountability possible without violence, and allow future generations to revisit and reinterpret events.
28. When analyzing Winthrop's sermon:
  • Sourcing: Who? John Winthrop   When? 1630   Audience? Puritan colonists / fellow settlers
  • Context: Puritans feared the colony would fail like Jamestown; they needed to establish order, discipline, and unity without formal institutions yet in place.
  • Norms established:
    • Mutual care / responsibility
    • Collective discipline / accountability
    • Community over self
29. Should historians reject biased sources?
No. All sources have bias/perspective. Historians should understand the bias and evaluate sources critically, not reject them entirely. Even biased sources provide valuable information when properly analyzed through sourcing and contextualization.
