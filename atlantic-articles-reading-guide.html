Central Argument: The question of what Jefferson meant by "all men" has defined American law and politics for too long. The focus should shift from Jefferson's intent to how Americans‚Äîparticularly Black Americans‚Äîhave given meaning to those words through their struggles.

Before You Read

Key Question: Where did the idea of universal human rights come from?

Central Argument: The idea that everyone has intrinsic rights to life and liberty was a radical break with millennia of human history. It's worth preserving.

Additional Relevant Articles (Shorter Guides)

These articles may also be useful depending on your democratic norm. While they're not part of the main 7 articles above, you can reference them in your analysis:

"The Myth of Mad King George" by Rick Atkinson (p. 14)

Central Argument: King George III was more complex than the "reactionary dolt" portrayed in American mythology. Understanding him helps us understand what the Revolution was actually rebelling against.

Key Questions:

  1. How does Atkinson complicate the traditional American narrative of George III as a tyrant?
  2. What does a more nuanced view of George III tell us about the causes of the Revolution?
  3. How does this article show that the Revolution was about institutional conflicts, not just a bad king?

For Democratic Norms: Consider how this article helps you think about the rule of law vs. personal rule, and institutional checks on power.

"No One Gave a Speech Like Patrick Henry" by Drew Gilpin Faust (p. 22)

Central Argument: Patrick Henry's oratory was crucial to mobilizing support for independence, and his speeches helped democratize political participation by appealing to ordinary people, not just elites.

Key Questions:

  1. How did Henry's speaking style differ from other Founders?
  2. Why did some view his populist approach as potentially "demagogic"?
  3. How did Henry's speeches help shift power from elite circles to broader participation?

For Democratic Norms: Consider how democratic persuasion works, and the role of popular mobilization vs. elite deliberation.

"Secrets of a Radical Duke" by Danielle Allen (p. 38)

Central Argument: A lost copy of the Declaration found in England reveals connections between American and British radicals who believed in democracy and rights.

Key Questions:

  1. How did the Declaration influence British radicals?
  2. What does the connection between American and British democrats tell us about the Revolution's ideas?
  3. How does this article show the Revolution as part of a broader Atlantic movement for rights?

For Democratic Norms: Consider how democratic ideas spread across borders and how different reform movements influenced each other.

"The Black Loyalists" by Andrew Lawler (p. 48)

Central Argument: Thousands of Black Americans fought for the British because they promised freedom from slavery, revealing the Revolution's deep contradictions about liberty.

Key Questions:

  1. Why did so many enslaved people join the British side?
  2. What happened to Black Loyalists after the war?
  3. How does this complicate the narrative of the Revolution as a fight for freedom?

For Democratic Norms: Consider who the Revolution's promises of freedom applied to, and how people excluded from those promises responded.

"The 27th Grievance" by Ned Blackhawk (p. 98)

Central Argument: Native nations shaped the Revolution in crucial ways, but the Declaration's final grievance against "merciless Indian Savages" shows how the Revolution depended on excluding Indigenous peoples from its vision of rights.

Key Questions:

  1. How did conflict with Native nations influence the decision to declare independence?
  2. Why did the Declaration's authors include the "merciless Indian Savages" grievance?
  3. How did Native peoples practice their own forms of sovereignty and self-government?

For Democratic Norms: Consider who was included and excluded from Revolutionary ideals of self-government, and how territorial expansion conflicted with democratic principles.

Making Connections Between Your Two Articles

Questions to Consider:

After completing both article analyses, think about how they relate to each other:

  • Different perspectives: How do your two articles offer different perspectives on the same democratic norm?
  • Ideals vs. failures: Does one article focus on ideals while the other focuses on failures to live up to those ideals?
  • Success or failure: How do different authors evaluate whether the Revolution "succeeded" at establishing democratic norms?
  • Complementary insights: What does each article add that the other doesn't? How do they work together to deepen your understanding?
  • Contradictions: Do your articles present any contradictory views? If so, how do you reconcile them?

Connecting to Haass's Bill of Obligations:

As you synthesize your two articles, think about which obligations these articles relate to:

  • Be informed: Do your research; know what you're talking about
  • Get involved: Participate in the democratic process
  • Stay open to compromise: Reject all-or-nothing politics
  • Remain civil: Respect others; reject violence
  • Reject violence: Use lawful means to pursue political goals
  • Value norms: Respect rules, traditions, and institutions
  • Promote the common good: Consider the collective interest
  • Respect government service: Honor those who serve
  • Support the teaching of civics: Educate the next generation
  • Put country first: Place national interest above partisan gain
